Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Draa Valley, south of the Atlas Mountains, an Amazigh farmer checks the level in the seguia — the communal irrigation channel that has distributed snowmelt to date palm groves for centuries. The water is lower this year. His dates will be smaller, his yield lower, and his share of the cooperative payment will not cover the diesel he needs for the pump that supplements the seguia when the snowmelt runs short. Three hours north, in Casablanca, a woman fills a kettle with tap water that comes from the Oum Er-Rbia river, itself fed by Atlas snowmelt and a dam system built to supply the coastal cities and the OCP phosphate processing plants simultaneously. She does not think about where the water comes from. She thinks about the price of bread, which has risen because the wheat is imported — 60% of Morocco's wheat comes from abroad, mostly from France and the Black Sea, and the price follows global commodity markets she will never see.

In Tripoli, a father fills jerrycans from a neighbourhood standpipe connected to the Great Man-Made River. The water pressure has been low for three days — a pipeline section near Bani Walid is under repair again, damage from a militia skirmish in February. He remembers 2019, when the water stopped entirely for two weeks during the Tripoli siege. His children do not remember, but they know the drill: fill everything when the water flows, ration when it doesn't. The water beneath the Sahara that reaches his tap fell as rain before the last ice age. It will not fall again.

In the Tunisian Sahel, near Sfax, a farmer harvests olives by hand in November — beating the branches with a long stick, gathering the fruit on nets spread beneath ancient trees. His family has done this for four generations. The oil they press is exceptional, but the bulk buyer from a Sfax export house offers €2.50 a litre, and the farmer knows the same oil will appear in a Milan supermarket as "Italian extra virgin" at €12. He sells because he has no cold storage, no bottling line, and no label. The value leaves with the tanker truck. What stays is the work, the water stress, and the eroding hillside.

What Presses

The weight on Maghreb and Atlas

Draa-Tafilalet, Morocco — South of the Atlas Mountains, where the last of the snowmelt reaches the oasis towns before the Sahara begins, Amazigh farming communities watch the seguia channels run shorter each spring. The date palms in Zagora and Errachidia depend on this water — a system of communal irrigation managed by elected water masters who allocate shares based on landholding and need. The system worked for centuries because the supply, though scarce, was predictable. Now the snowpack is 30% lower than the 1980s baseline, and the industrial greenhouses on the Souss plain to the north are pumping the same deep aquifer. The oasis farmers have no legal priority over the greenhouse operators. The water table drops. The palms produce less. Young men leave for Casablanca or Tangier. The seguia still flows, but for fewer weeks, to fewer families, and no one can say how many seasons it has left.

Western Sahara (Occupied Territory) — Bou Craa is a mining town in the desert — a town that exists because phosphate exists beneath it. The Sahrawi people who lived here before the mine were pastoralists grazing camels across the hammada. Since Morocco's Green March in 1975, the territory has been under military administration, separated from Algeria by a 2,700-kilometre sand wall — the longest active military barrier in the world. The phosphate travels by conveyor belt to El Aaiún port and by ship to fertiliser plants in India, Brazil, and Indonesia. The Sahrawi population lives either under occupation or in refugee camps near Tindouf, Algeria, where they have been for fifty years. The UN Mission (MINURSO) monitors a ceasefire but has no mandate to monitor human rights. There is no mechanism to share the phosphate revenue. There is no date for the referendum.

Tripoli, Libya — The Great Man-Made River runs beneath 4,000 kilometres of desert, carrying water that last saw daylight during the Pleistocene. In Tripoli, the taps work most days — but "most days" is the achievement of a broken country. When militia fighting damaged the pipeline near Bani Walid in 2019, water stopped for two weeks. Families filled jerrycans from tanker trucks. The price of a cubic metre of water rose tenfold on the informal market. The pipeline has been repaired and damaged and repaired again, following the rhythm of a civil war that has no front line and no end date. Six million people depend on infrastructure that runs through territory controlled by armed groups with no interest in hydrology. Meanwhile, sub-Saharan migrants arrive daily — transiting to Europe, trapped in detention centres where the UN has documented forced labour, sexual violence, and conditions of modern slavery. The EU funds the coast guard that intercepts their boats and returns them to the centres they fled.

Morocco holds 70% of the world's phosphate reserves — the mineral that becomes the fertiliser that grows a third of the world's food. OCP Group, the state enterprise, mines it in Khouribga and in the occupied Western Sahara, where Sahrawi communities have been displaced since 1975. The phosphate travels by conveyor belt and ship to Brazil, India, and China. The fertiliser it becomes is in the food on your table. The Sahrawi people whose land produces it have no vote in how it is used, no share in the revenue, and no date for the self-determination referendum the UN promised 35 years ago.
Libya's Great Man-Made River is the largest engineering project in Africa — 4,000 kilometres of pipeline drawing 40,000-year-old fossil water from Saharan aquifers to supply coastal cities and farms. It sustains 6 million people. It is also non-renewable: the water will not refill in any human timeframe, and the infrastructure has been damaged repeatedly in a civil war that shows no sign of ending. The country's water stress stands at 817%. The pipeline is both lifeline and countdown.
An estimated 600,000 sub-Saharan migrants are trapped in Libya at any given time — in detention centres, in trafficking networks, in forced labour conditions the UN has documented as modern slavery. They are transiting to Europe, but the transit has become the destination: many never leave, caught between smugglers who demand payment and coast guard units that intercept boats with EU funding and return passengers to the detention centres they fled. The Mediterranean crossing that might drown them is preferable to the certainty of what they face on shore.

Four resource streams flow out of the Maghreb — phosphate, crude oil, olive oil, and migrant labour — worth billions at their destination markets. What does not come back is the water, consumed by mines and greenhouses and drawn from aquifers that predate human civilisation; and the dignity, denied to Sahrawi communities under occupation, to migrants in Libyan detention, and to Tunisian farmers whose oil is relabelled as someone else's. 32 killed (3 months); 32,791 displaced (total); migrant agricultural workers, wheat imports, fossil groundwater (Great Man-Made River) leaving; precipitation 51% of normal.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The phosphate in your food's fertiliser likely comes from Morocco — and possibly from occupied Western Sahara, where Sahrawi communities have been displaced since 1975. OCP Group controls 33% of global phosphate trade. The Western Sahara Resource Watch (wsrw.org) tracks which companies buy Western Sahara phosphate and which shipping lines carry it. If you eat food grown with synthetic fertiliser, you are connected to this place. Ask your government's position on Western Sahara — it is likely silent, and the silence is the policy.
If you buy olive oil labelled "Italian extra virgin," it may contain Tunisian oil bought in bulk at €2–3 a litre and relabelled at €10–12. Tunisia is the world's second-largest olive oil exporter, but most of it leaves without a Tunisian label. Look for oil labelled "Product of Tunisia" — it exists, and buying it directly shifts a few euros from an Italian bottler to a Tunisian farmer whose hillside is eroding and whose water is running out.
The EU funds Libyan coast guard units to intercept Mediterranean crossings and return migrants to detention centres that the UN has documented as sites of torture, forced labour, and sexual violence. This is funded by European taxpayers as migration policy. The European Council on Refugees and Exiles (ecre.org) tracks the agreements and their consequences. If you are a European citizen, this is done in your name.
