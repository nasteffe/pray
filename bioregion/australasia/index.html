What Presses

The weight on Australasia

From Australasia, thermal coal, iron ore, liquefied natural gas (LNG) flow outward — the cost borne by Gomeroi, Wiradjuri, and other First Nations (sacred site destruction), Karijini, Ngaanyatjarra, and other Pilbara First Nations, Yolngu, Martu, Bardi, and other Northern/Western Australian First Nations.

Australia exports the coal whose combustion warms the ocean that bleaches the reef that sustains the tourism economy that employs the coastal communities that vote for the governments that approve the coal exports. The loop is closed, the contradiction is visible, and the coal trains run anyway — 200 million tonnes a year through Newcastle alone, making it the world's largest coal port. The reef cannot survive what the port requires.
Rio Tinto destroyed the Juukan Gorge rock shelters — 46,000 years of continuous Aboriginal habitation, the oldest known evidence of human hair-braiding, DNA-linked to living Puutu Kunti Kurrama and Pinikura people — for an iron ore seam worth $135 million. The company's board resigned; its share price recovered within months; its Pilbara operations expanded. The shelters are now overburden. The iron is structural steel in Chinese construction. The legal framework that permitted the destruction has been reformed but not in ways that would prevent its repetition at scale.
Fonterra processes a third of New Zealand's GDP into milk powder for export while the rivers downstream of its supply farms exceed safe nitrate levels for infant formula preparation. The company reports on sustainability; the rivers report on chemistry. Māori freshwater rights under te mana o te wai are legally recognised and practically subordinated — the irrigation consent was granted before the Treaty claim was heard.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Firesticks Alliance Indigenous Corporation solidarity

Aboriginal-led organization reviving cultural burning practices across Australia. Trains Indigenous and non-Indigenous land managers in traditional fire knowledge that has sustained eucalypt landscapes for 65,000 years.

Directly addresses the atmospheric network crisis the briefing describes — megafires replacing the Aboriginal fire management that eucalypt forests evolved with. Cultural burning is the only demonstrated sustainable fire regime for these landscapes, and its suppression by colonial fire management created the fuel loads that drive Black Summer-scale events.

Murray Lower Darling Rivers Indigenous Nations (MLDRIN) advocacy

Confederation of First Nations peoples along the Murray-Darling river system, advocating for Aboriginal water rights and cultural flows. Represents the Traditional Owners whose water rights remain largely unfulfilled under the Basin Plan.

Represents the cost-bearers at the center of the water network crisis the briefing describes — Aboriginal nations whose cultural water rights are structurally subordinated to irrigator allocations in a river system where more water rights exist than actual water.

Te Kahui Wai Maori advocacy

Maori freshwater advisory body advocating for the implementation of te mana o te wai (the authority of the water) in New Zealand's resource management framework. Works to ensure Maori rights to freshwater are not subordinated to agricultural irrigation consents.

Directly addresses the water network crisis the briefing identifies in New Zealand — where dairy intensification (12,000 farms, 6.3 million cows) has polluted 60% of monitored rivers while Maori freshwater rights under the Treaty of Waitangi remain structurally subordinated to export-oriented agriculture.

Lock the Gate Alliance advocacy

Australian grassroots alliance of farming communities, Traditional Owners, and conservationists opposing destructive mining and gas extraction. Coordinates community campaigns against coal seam gas, open-cut mining, and sacred site destruction.

Works at the intersection of the mineral and carbon extractive flows the briefing traces — coal from Hunter Valley, iron ore from the Pilbara, LNG from the Browse Basin — where Gomeroi, Wiradjuri, Karijini, and other First Nations bear the cost of sacred site destruction and water contamination.

Inland Rivers Network investigation

Australian conservation organization focused on the health of inland river systems, particularly the Murray-Darling. Monitors water allocation compliance, environmental flow delivery, and the ecological condition of rivers and wetlands.

Monitors the water system the briefing centers — the Murray-Darling basin that produces a third of Australia's food from chronically over-allocated water. Tracks whether environmental flows actually reach downstream ecosystems and Aboriginal communities.
