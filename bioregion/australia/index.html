What Presses

The weight on Australia

Pilbara, Western Australia — The trains are 2.4 kilometres long and they run day and night, carrying iron ore from pits the size of suburbs to Port Hedland, where the red dust coats everything and the harbour water is rust-coloured. Karijini people have lived in this country for 40,000 years. The gorges that carry their name — Karijini National Park — are among the most beautiful landscapes on earth, but the pits are moving closer. Fly-in fly-out workers from Perth and Brisbane earn $150,000 a year and never visit Roebourne, where Aboriginal families live in public housing with mould problems and where the life expectancy gap with non-Indigenous Australians remains 8 years. After Juukan Gorge, the mining companies promised better consultation. The pits kept expanding.

Lower Murray-Darling, South Australia — At Menindee, where the Darling meets the lakes, Barkindji elder Uncle Badger Bates has photographed the same dry pools for twenty years. In 2019, a million native fish floated dead in water so oxygen-depleted it turned green — the result of upstream irrigation drawing the river below survival thresholds. The Murray-Darling Basin Plan was supposed to return 3,200 gigalitres to the environment. The buybacks stalled, the political compromises weakened the targets, and the river kept dropping. Aboriginal water rights — cultural flows for ceremony, connection, and ecological health — remain the last allocation to be honoured and the first to be cut.

Australia exports the coal whose combustion warms the ocean that bleaches the reef that sustains the tourism economy that employs the coastal communities that vote for the governments that approve the coal exports. The loop is closed, the contradiction is visible, and the coal trains run anyway — 200 million tonnes a year through Newcastle alone, making it the world's largest coal port. The reef cannot survive what the port requires.
Rio Tinto destroyed the Juukan Gorge rock shelters — 46,000 years of continuous Aboriginal habitation, the oldest known evidence of human hair-braiding, DNA-linked to living Puutu Kunti Kurrama and Pinikura people — for an iron ore seam worth $135 million. The company's board resigned; its share price recovered within months; its Pilbara operations expanded. The shelters are now overburden. The iron is structural steel in Chinese construction.

Three resource streams leave Australia annually — thermal coal, iron ore, liquefied natural gas — measured in hundreds of millions of tonnes and tens of billions of dollars at destination. What does not return is the Juukan Gorge, the coral that bleached, or the 46,000 years of habitation that Rio Tinto removed in an afternoon. The extraction is legal, profitable, and ongoing. thermal coal, iron ore, liquefied natural gas (LNG) leaving.

The dashboard reads green. No one is dying in the way that dashboards measure dying. But the reef is bleaching, the river is emptying, the gorge is gone, and the coal train runs day and night. This is what structural harm looks like from inside the structure.

Seeds of Hope

Barkindji elder Uncle Badger Bates has spent decades fighting for the Darling River at Wilcannia — photographing dry pools where pelicans once nested, testifying to the Murray-Darling Basin Authority, refusing to accept that upstream cotton allocation is more important than downstream life. His advocacy helped trigger the South Australian Royal Commission into the Murray-Darling Basin Plan (2019), which found systemic over-allocation and political interference.

Looking Ahead

  • Australia's fire season runs October through March and is expanding into September and April.
  • The next El Niño event will bring drought to the Murray-Darling basin, intensify bushfire conditions, and accelerate reef bleaching.
  • The structural drivers are not changing: coal and iron ore exports are at record volumes, no new water has been added to the Murray-Darling system, and the Great Barrier Reef has had no recovery window between bleaching events.
  • Murray-Darling Basin Plan water buyback decisions -- the Australian government's commitment to recover 450 gigalitres for the environment faces irrigator opposition and determines river health
  • Australian bushfire season November-March -- the 2019-20 Black Summer burned 18.6 million hectares; each El Nino year raises the risk of repeat catastrophic fire across southeast Australia
  • Rio Tinto, BHP, and Fortescue Pilbara expansion approvals -- each new iron ore pit requires Traditional Owner consent after the Juukan Gorge destruction; watch for cultural heritage protection decisions

Take Action

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Firesticks Alliance Indigenous Corporation solidarity

Aboriginal-led organization reviving cultural burning practices across Australia. Trains Indigenous and non-Indigenous land managers in traditional fire knowledge that has sustained eucalypt landscapes for 65,000 years.

Directly addresses the atmospheric network crisis — megafires replacing the Aboriginal fire management that eucalypt forests evolved with. Cultural burning is the only demonstrated sustainable fire regime for these landscapes.

Murray Lower Darling Rivers Indigenous Nations (MLDRIN) advocacy

Confederation of First Nations peoples along the Murray-Darling river system, advocating for Aboriginal water rights and cultural flows. Represents the Traditional Owners whose water rights remain largely unfulfilled under the Basin Plan.

Represents the cost-bearers at the center of the water network crisis — Aboriginal nations whose cultural water rights are structurally subordinated to irrigator allocations in a river system where more water rights exist than actual water.

Lock the Gate Alliance advocacy

Australian grassroots alliance of farming communities, Traditional Owners, and conservationists opposing destructive mining and gas extraction. Coordinates community campaigns against coal seam gas, open-cut mining, and sacred site destruction.

Works at the intersection of the mineral and carbon extractive flows — coal from Hunter Valley, iron ore from the Pilbara, LNG from the Browse Basin — where Gomeroi, Wiradjuri, Karijini, and other First Nations bear the cost.

Inland Rivers Network investigation

Australian conservation organization focused on the health of inland river systems, particularly the Murray-Darling. Monitors water allocation compliance, environmental flow delivery, and the ecological condition of rivers and wetlands.

Monitors the Murray-Darling basin that produces a third of Australia's food from chronically over-allocated water. Tracks whether environmental flows actually reach downstream ecosystems and Aboriginal communities.
