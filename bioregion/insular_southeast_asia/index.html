Lives Lived Here

On Bangka Island, a young man descends into a tin pit dug by hand into white sand. The tunnel is braced with coconut-wood poles. He earns more in a week than his father makes fishing in a month — the fishing has collapsed since the offshore dredges arrived. He knows the tunnel may collapse. He calculates the risk each morning and goes down anyway. The tin he digs will travel through a local smelter to an export terminal to a factory floor in Shenzhen, where it becomes the solder that holds your phone's circuit board together. For the gifts of this place…

For the gifts of these islands — reefs and rainforests, a thousand peoples and tongues, the trading crossroads of half the world. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what our comfort costs…

For the palm oil in half of what we eat and wash with, and the nickel in our batteries — grown and dug where the forest used to stand, where the haze now chokes the sky and the smelters foul the fishing grounds. Teach us to trace the label back to the cleared peat and the poisoned bay.

For the forest and fishing peoples…

For the Dayak and other Indigenous peoples of Borneo and Sumatra, pushed off ancestral land for the plantations; and for the coastal fishing communities beside the nickel smelters, whose catch and whose water are going. That those who kept these islands are not sacrificed to power them.

For holding both…

For eyes to see one region holding gleaming cities and cleared forests in the same frame; and for love that does not enjoy the cheap and the convenient without asking what it cost the islands that made it.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Palm oil appears in roughly half of all packaged supermarket products — bread, margarine, biscuits, shampoo, lipstick, detergent. Indonesia is the world's largest producer. Wilmar International (Singapore) and Golden Agri-Resources (Sinar Mas Group) are the dominant traders. The Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) certifies some production, but certification does not cover peatland drainage or historical land seizure. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) would require supply chain traceability; its enforcement has been delayed. You can check whether the products you buy contain palm oil — the ingredient list is required to name it. What it is not required to name is the forest that was cleared to produce it.
The nickel in EV batteries may come from Morowali, Central Sulawesi, where Tsingshan Holding Group operates the Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park. The batteries supply CATL and BYD. If you drive or plan to buy an electric vehicle, ask the manufacturer: where is the nickel from, and what are the environmental discharge standards at the smelter? BHRRC (business-humanrights.org) maintains a tracker of nickel and human rights allegations searchable by company name.
Sasi laut — the traditional rotational reef closure practised by communities in Maluku — is one of the oldest marine management systems still in active use. Communities ban harvest from reef sections for months or years, allowing fish stocks and coral to recover. It works. It has worked for centuries. It requires no technology, no enforcement budget, and no international agreement — only a community that agrees to wait. Supporting organisations that defend customary marine tenure (like KNTI) is one of the most cost-effective forms of marine conservation that exists.
