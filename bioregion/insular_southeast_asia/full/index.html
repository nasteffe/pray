Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Riau, a Melayu farmer harvests oil palm fruit — the red-orange bunches that must reach the mill within 24 hours or their acid content rises and the price drops. She is a smallholder: 40% of Indonesia's palm oil comes from farmers like her, but the mill sets the price, and the price has been falling. She took a loan from the mill to plant; the loan compounds; the fruit is her only means of repayment. Her grandmother grew rice and kept a kitchen garden in the peat forest. The peat forest is gone.

In a longhouse in West Kalimantan, a Dayak elder traces the boundary of the community's customary forest on a hand-drawn map. The boundary has not changed in his lifetime, but the forest inside it has. The concession company cleared the north side five years ago; the south side is in court. His grandson studies law in Pontianak — the first in the family to attend university — specifically to argue the land case. The case has been postponed four times.

On Bangka Island, a young man descends into a tin pit dug by hand into white sand. The tunnel is braced with coconut-wood poles. He earns more in a week than his father makes fishing in a month — the fishing has collapsed since the offshore dredges arrived. He knows the tunnel may collapse. He calculates the risk each morning and goes down anyway. The tin he digs will travel through a local smelter to an export terminal to a factory floor in Shenzhen, where it becomes the solder that holds your phone's circuit board together. He has never seen a smartphone.

In Morowali, Central Sulawesi, a Bajo woman mends nets on a platform house built over the reef. The water below her is cloudy — red-brown, since the nickel smelter began operating. Her husband fishes further out now, burning more fuel to reach clean water, catching less. The smelter employs men from her village, but not her husband — he is too old, they said. The nickel becomes battery-grade material for electric vehicles. The vehicles are advertised as saving the planet.

What Presses

The weight on Insular Southeast Asia

Riau, Sumatra — The peat forest that once covered Riau province is 80% gone, replaced by palm oil plantations stretching to every horizon. Melayu Riau farming families who grew rice and tapped rubber in the forest now work as plantation labourers or tend smallholder palm plots on land they once managed through diverse agroforestry. Every dry season, the drained peat ignites. The smoke is acrid, chemical, inescapable — it enters houses through every gap, settles in children's lungs, and does not lift for weeks. Schools close. Clinics fill. The fires are set by companies and smallholders clearing land, but the smoke does not discriminate: it falls on the people who lit it and the people who did not, on the plantation worker and the grandmother who remembers the forest.

Morowali, Central Sulawesi — Five years ago, the coast at Morowali was a fishing village. Now it is an industrial zone. The Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park — backed by Tsingshan Holding Group (China) — operates nickel smelters that run day and night, producing the battery-grade material that powers the global EV transition. The Bajo sea-nomad families who fished this reef for generations watch the water change colour. Red-brown plumes from smelter discharge spread across the bay. Fish kills have been documented. Workers — Indonesian and Chinese — have died in smelter explosions. The villagers who were not hired fish further out, burning more diesel to reach water that is still clear, catching less each trip.

Mimika, Papua — The Amungme people called it Nemangkawi — the place of the ancestors, the mountain where the spirits lived. Freeport-McMoRan called it the Ertsberg, and then the Grasberg, and hollowed it from the inside. The Kamoro people downstream watched the Aikwa River turn grey, then white, then stop flowing as tailings filled the floodplain. Two hundred and thirty square kilometres of lowland — their fishing grounds, their sago gardens, their children's swimming places — buried under mine waste. The Indonesian military provides perimeter security. Journalists and human rights observers have restricted access. The mine operates under a contract of work that runs to 2041.

Palm oil is the most invisible extractive commodity on earth: present in half of all supermarket products, yet the 26 million hectares of forest destroyed to produce it — and the Dayak, Melayu Riau, and Batak communities displaced from that forest — appear on no label. Wilmar International (Singapore) and Golden Agri-Resources (Sinar Mas) control supply chains that connect Indonesian peatland destruction to breakfast tables in Mumbai, Shanghai, and London. The EU Deforestation Regulation was designed to break this invisibility; Indonesia is lobbying to prevent its enforcement.
Indonesia's nickel boom is the green transition's extraction frontier. Tsingshan Holding Group's smelter complex at Morowali processes ore for CATL and BYD — the batteries that power electric vehicles marketed as the solution to climate change. The Bajo sea-nomad communities whose reef fisheries receive the smelter's waste are not consulted about the energy transition they are subsidising with their food supply. The cost of decarbonisation is not distributed; it is deposited, in red mud, on a reef.
The Grasberg mine has operated for over fifty years, extracted over $100 billion in copper and gold, and deposited 300,000 tonnes of tailings per day into the Aikwa River. The Amungme people's sacred mountain is gone. The Kamoro people's lowland fishery is buried. The Indonesian military provides security. Freeport-McMoRan (Phoenix, Arizona) retains 49% ownership; PT Inalum (Indonesian state) holds 51%. The tailings will remain in the Aikwa floodplain for geological time. This is not a crisis that can be resolved; it is a landscape that has been permanently remade to serve a supply chain.

Driven by: We must transform the palm-oil industry to NDPE compliance through our supply-chain leadership

The trap: Each tier of supplier mapping and each grievance-response cycle improves what is visible in the chain Wilmar buys from while leaving untouched the demand structure that makes 26 million hectares of plantation profitable in the first place. The Dayak forest livelihoods displaced before the policy began remain displaced; the smallholder schemes folded into NDPE compliance remain dependent on Wilmar's mill-gate price. The chain becomes more transparent as the conditions it documents become more entrenched. The traceability framework's function is to convert visible non-compliance into manageable risk — not to alter the demand that produces the non-compliance.

Mining Industry Indonesia (MIND ID) · Majority state stakes in PT Freeport Indonesia (Grasberg copper/gold, 51% IDN), PT Inalum (aluminium), PT Aneka Tambang (nickel/gold), PT Bukit Asam (coal), PT Timah (tin); Raw-mineral export ban revenues channelled into domestic smelting capacity; Indonesian sovereign debt issuance and PLN/state-bank credit lines; Joint ventures with Chinese smelter operators (Tsingshan, GEM, Huayou) for downstream nickel and cobalt processing

Driven by: We must capture the full value chain of Indonesian minerals for the Indonesian people

The trap: The export ban on raw nickel and the divestment of Freeport's Grasberg stake were emancipatory acts in resource-nationalism grammar — but the smelters that absorb the resulting ore feed are Chinese-financed, Chinese-operated, and powered by Indonesian coal subsidised by the same state asserting sovereignty. The downstream integration the policy demands is built on a coal-and-Chinese-capital scaffolding the sovereignty narrative cannot acknowledge; the Amungme and Kamoro communities at Grasberg bear the same tailings whether the majority owner is American or Indonesian. The sovereign ownership change converts the structural extraction into a national project — but the substance of the extraction, and who bears its costs, is unchanged.

Tsingshan Holding Group, Indonesia operations (IMIP / IWIP) · Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park (Sulawesi, since 2013) — world's largest nickel-laterite smelter cluster; Indonesia Weda Bay Industrial Park (Halmahera, since 2018) — HPAL battery-precursor capacity; Off-take agreements with Tesla, BYD, CATL, LG Energy Solution, Ford battery supply chains; Captive coal-fired power generation (~2 GW IMIP, ~1 GW IWIP)

Driven by: We must scale Indonesian nickel processing to supply the global energy transition

The trap: The same battery chemistry that displaces internal-combustion emissions in Hamburg or San Francisco generates HPAL tailings discharged into Bajo sea-nomad fishing grounds, captive-coal CO2 that exceeds the emissions the EV is supposed to prevent, and SO2 emissions blanketing villages whose residents will not drive an EV. The 'transition mineral' framing transfers the moral premium of decarbonisation onto an extractive regime whose worker fatalities, captive coal plants, and reef-system poisoning would be unacceptable in any other industrial context. The fantasy is that the EV's tailpipe-emission absence is the relevant emission boundary; the structural function of the framing is to render Morowali and Weda Bay outside the boundary the buyer's accounting registers.

Six resource streams leave these islands every day — palm oil for your kitchen and your cosmetics, nickel for the batteries in electric vehicles, tin for the solder in your phone, copper and gold from a sacred mountain that no longer exists, fish from reefs that are being blasted apart. What does not leave is the smoke from the peat fires, the red mud in the reef water, the collapsed tunnel with a young man inside, the Dayak elder waiting for a court date that keeps being postponed. The distance between your supermarket shelf and these islands is shorter than it appears. 69 killed (3 months); 360,097 food insecure (current); crude palm oil, nickel (laterite ore, nickel pig iron, nickel matte), tin leaving.

The Bajo build their houses on stilts over the reef because that is where the fish are. The smelter was built on the same coast because that is where the port is. The fish and the nickel cannot coexist in the same water, and everyone involved knows it. The question is whose knowledge counts.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Palm oil appears in roughly half of all packaged supermarket products — bread, margarine, biscuits, shampoo, lipstick, detergent. Indonesia is the world's largest producer. Wilmar International (Singapore) and Golden Agri-Resources (Sinar Mas Group) are the dominant traders. The Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) certifies some production, but certification does not cover peatland drainage or historical land seizure. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) would require supply chain traceability; its enforcement has been delayed. You can check whether the products you buy contain palm oil — the ingredient list is required to name it. What it is not required to name is the forest that was cleared to produce it.
The nickel in EV batteries may come from Morowali, Central Sulawesi, where Tsingshan Holding Group operates the Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park. The batteries supply CATL and BYD. If you drive or plan to buy an electric vehicle, ask the manufacturer: where is the nickel from, and what are the environmental discharge standards at the smelter? BHRRC (business-humanrights.org) maintains a tracker of nickel and human rights allegations searchable by company name.
Sasi laut — the traditional rotational reef closure practised by communities in Maluku — is one of the oldest marine management systems still in active use. Communities ban harvest from reef sections for months or years, allowing fish stocks and coral to recover. It works. It has worked for centuries. It requires no technology, no enforcement budget, and no international agreement — only a community that agrees to wait. Supporting organisations that defend customary marine tenure (like KNTI) is one of the most cost-effective forms of marine conservation that exists.

WALHI (Wahana Lingkungan Hidup Indonesia — Friends of the Earth Indonesia) advocacy

Indonesia's largest environmental organization, campaigning against palm oil deforestation, nickel smelter expansion, and peatland drainage. Provides legal support to Indigenous communities facing land seizures for plantation and mining concessions.

Confronts the palm oil expansion the briefing documents — 26M+ hectares of converted forest and peatland in Kalimantan and Sumatra — and the new wave of Chinese-backed nickel smelters in Sulawesi dumping waste into coastal fisheries for EV battery supply chains.

Forest Peoples Programme solidarity

Supports Indigenous Dayak, Batak, and other forest-dependent communities in asserting land rights against palm oil and mining concessions. Documents violations of free, prior, and informed consent across Indonesia and Malaysia.

Defends the Dayak, Melayu Riau, and Batak communities the briefing identifies as displaced from ancestral agroforestry (tembawang gardens, rubber, fruit, and medicinal plants) by Wilmar International, Golden Agri-Resources, and Asian Pulp & Paper's plantation expansion.

JATAM (Jaringan Advokasi Tambang — Mining Advocacy Network) investigation

Indonesian mining watchdog investigating the Grasberg mine, Bangka-Belitung tin mining, and Sulawesi nickel operations. Documents tailings disposal, worker deaths, and community displacement from mining mega-projects.

Investigates the Grasberg mine the briefing describes — where 300,000 tonnes/day of tailings have buried the Aikwa River floodplain (230 km²), destroying the Kamoro people's fishing and farming livelihood while extracting $100B+ in gold and copper for Freeport-McMoRan.

Greenpeace Southeast Asia investigation

Campaigns against peatland burning, palm oil deforestation, and industrial overfishing in the Coral Triangle. Deploys field teams to document illegal fires during haze season and IUU fishing by foreign fleets.

Documents the peatland fire crisis the briefing identifies — where fires set for land clearing ignite drained peat across Kalimantan and Sumatra, burning underground for weeks, causing 100,000+ premature deaths in 2015, and releasing more CO2 than the entire EU transport sector.

Coral Triangle Initiative on Coral Reefs, Fisheries and Food Security advocacy

Intergovernmental partnership of 6 nations working to protect the Coral Triangle — 76% of global coral species — from blast fishing, climate bleaching, and coastal development. Supports community-managed marine protected areas.

Addresses the Coral Triangle reef degradation the briefing identifies as threatening food security for 275M Indonesians who depend on fish for 54% of animal protein — where blast and cyanide fishing destroy the reef nurseries while industrial trawlers and IUU fleets deplete stocks.

Business & Human Rights Resource Centre — Transition Minerals investigation

Tracks corporate accountability for nickel smelter operations in Sulawesi and tin mining on Bangka-Belitung, documenting worker deaths, environmental damage, and community complaints against Chinese and multinational operators.

Monitors the EV supply chain extraction the briefing describes — Chinese-backed nickel smelters (Tsingshan at Morowali) supplying Tesla and BYD's battery chains while dumping waste into Bajo sea-nomad fishing waters, and Bangka-Belitung tin miners dying in collapses to supply smartphone solder.
