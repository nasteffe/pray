Lives Lived Here

At four in the morning in Al Wakrah, Qatar, a Bangladeshi construction worker queues for the bus to the North Field Expansion site at Ras Laffan, sixty kilometres north. He will spend ten hours welding LNG pipe fittings in conditions that routinely exceed the wet-bulb temperature at which the human body can no longer cool itself. The gas he is helping to pipe will be liquefied at minus 162 degrees, loaded onto Q-Max tankers — the largest LNG carriers ever built — and shipped through the Strait of Hormuz to terminals in Yokohama, Incheon, and Rotterdam. In January, Qatar exported 6.5 million tonnes of LNG. The welder earns 1,500 Qatari riyals a month — about £315 at current rates, above Qatar's legal minimum of QAR 1,000/month (introduced March 2021) — before the deductions his sponsor takes for accommodation and food in the labour camp. He owes a recruitment agency in Dhaka the equivalent of fourteen months' wages.

In a glass tower in the Dubai International Financial Centre, a fund manager allocates sovereign wealth across global markets. The Abu Dhabi Investment Authority manages over $900 billion — drawn from oil revenue, invested in real estate in London, technology in California, farmland in Australia. The fund manager may drive past the Sonapur labour camp on Sheikh Mohammed bin Zayed Road, where 150,000 workers live in shared rooms twelve to a unit, but the camp is screened from the highway. The connection between the sovereign wealth and the labour camp is not hidden; it simply does not require acknowledgement.

In a kitchen in Manchester, a family turns on the gas hob. The methane that lights the flame may have originated in Qatar's North Field — the same field the Bangladeshi welder is extending. The family pays their energy bill, which includes a wholesale price set partly by Qatari production decisions. They will never know the welder's name. The distance between the hob and the labour camp is not geographical; it is structural. The gas flows because the welder works. The welder works because he cannot leave.

What Presses

The weight on Tethys Gulf

Ras Laffan, Qatar — Sixty kilometres north of Doha, the world's largest LNG processing facility operates around the clock. Qatar is expanding North Field production from 77 million to 126 million tonnes of LNG per year by 2027. The expansion requires tens of thousands of additional construction workers, recruited under the same kafala system that produced documented deaths during World Cup stadium construction. Nepali workers — roughly 400,000 in Qatar — arrive carrying recruitment debts of $2,000 to $7,000. The reformed labour laws Qatar announced in 2020 have not ended the kafala system; they have renamed parts of it. Workers still cannot leave the country without employer permission in practice. Heat-related deaths are classified as "cardiac arrest" on death certificates, making the true toll uncountable. The families in Kathmandu and Janakpur who sent their sons receive a body and no explanation.

Dubai DMCC Gold Souk — Seventy-five billion dollars in gold passes through Dubai's refining and trading hub annually. Global Witness has traced conflict gold from RSF-controlled mines in Darfur — where farming communities were displaced and miners work under armed guard — through air freight to Dubai, where it enters DMCC-registered refineries (Kaloti, Emirates Gold) and exits as certified bullion for Swiss, Indian, and Chinese markets. The DMCC's due diligence requirements exist on paper. The enforcement gap is the product. Every gold bar that passes through Dubai without rigorous origin verification is a financial instrument that funds the next season of war in Sudan.

Ruwais Industrial Zone, Abu Dhabi — Two hundred kilometres west of Abu Dhabi city, the Ruwais refinery complex processes 900,000 barrels of crude per day. The workers — predominantly Indian and Pakistani — live in company compounds adjacent to the refinery stacks. Respiratory illness rates among refinery-adjacent migrant communities have not been independently studied because access is restricted. The same ADNOC revenue that funds the $900 billion ADIA sovereign wealth fund pays the wages — when they are paid — of the workers who inhale what the refinery emits. The compound is not a neighbourhood; it is a labour deployment site from which workers cannot freely depart.

The kafala system is not a labour market — it is a legal architecture that produces fifteen million people who build and maintain entire nations but cannot become part of them. Indian construction workers in the UAE (~3.5 million), Nepali workers in Qatar (~400,000), Filipino domestic workers (~700,000 in UAE alone) — each arrives carrying debt from recruitment fees, each is tied to a single employer who controls their visa, each can be deported for complaint. The system is not an aberration within Gulf prosperity; it is the mechanism by which Gulf prosperity is produced.
Dubai's DMCC gold hub connects the jewellery in a London shop window to the displacement of Darfuri farming communities by RSF-controlled mining in Sudan. The connection is not metaphorical — Global Witness has traced specific gold shipments from Khartoum to Dubai to Swiss refineries. The $75 billion annual throughput is large enough to absorb conflict gold without noticing, and the due diligence regime is designed to permit this absorption. Every pension fund and central bank that holds gold-backed assets is a downstream participant.
The Gulf's water economy is a closed loop with no exit: desalination requires fossil fuel energy, which produces revenue to fund more desalination, which discharges brine that degrades the Persian Gulf, which is the only water source for desalination. The shallow enclosed sea — averaging 35 metres depth — cannot dilute what five littoral states discharge simultaneously. The economy that depends on this water is destroying the water it depends on, and there is no alternative source within a thousand kilometres.

There is no river here. There has never been a river here. Everything that sustains human life in these cities — water, food, the buildings themselves — was brought from somewhere else or manufactured from the ground beneath them. What to bring with you from this briefing

The gas that heats your home or powers your cooker may originate in Qatar's North Field, where Nepali and Bangladeshi workers weld pipe fittings in temperatures that exceed the human body's ability to cool itself. Qatar's post-World Cup labour reforms did not end the kafala system — they modified its language. The International Labour Organization maintains a Qatar country office monitoring implementation. Their reports are public. Read them. If your energy supplier sources LNG from Qatar — most major European utilities do — their modern slavery statement is a legally required public document. Ask whether it names the kafala system.
If you own gold jewellery, hold a gold-backed ETF, or have a pension fund that includes gold reserves, you are a downstream participant in Dubai's $75 billion annual gold market — a market that Global Witness has documented receiving conflict gold from RSF-controlled mines in Darfur. The DMCC's responsible sourcing guidelines exist. Their enforcement does not. The Swiss refineries downstream from Dubai — Valcambi, PAMP, Metalor — are subject to the Swiss Responsible Business Initiative's due diligence provisions. Jewellers can source from refineries with chain-of-custody certification. Ask yours whether they do.
Remittances from Gulf migrant workers account for 25% of Nepal's GDP and significant shares in Bangladesh, the Philippines, and India. The families who depend on this money cannot advocate for labour reform without risking the income stream. This is not a personal failing; it is a structural trap. Organisations working on this include Pravasi Nepali Coordination Committee (Nepal), Migrants' Rights Platform (Bahrain), and the Migrant Forum in Asia. They accept solidarity and attention. The workers cannot organise collectively in the Gulf; the organising must happen from outside.
