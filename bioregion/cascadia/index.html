Lives Lived Here

In April, when the river is cold and the snowmelt is just beginning, a Yakama Nation fisher drives to the confluence of the Klickitat and Columbia before dawn — the same confluence her family has fished since before the dams, at a place where the treaty of 1855 guarantees the right to fish "at all usual and accustomed places." She dips her net. The run is thin. What returns from the Pacific each spring is a fraction of what ran here when the river was whole. The fish she catches will feed her family and her elders. The fish that don't return leave a gap in the forest — marine nitrogen that the trees needed, calories that the bears and eagles needed, the pulse that held this whole system together.

In Vancouver, a software engineer checks the air quality app before her morning run. It says 42 — acceptable. She runs. She passes a Starbucks, a Tesla showroom, a Whole Foods. The city feels clean and prosperous and green. What she doesn't see is the Trans Mountain Pipeline that came online last year, running through Wet'suwet'en country two provinces east, carrying Alberta bitumen to the tanker terminal twelve kilometres from where she's running. Her city's prosperity is downstream of that extraction. She knows this, dimly, the way one knows things one prefers not to examine too closely.

In Smithers, BC, a Wet'suwet'en land defender sits in her kitchen reading the court documents in the latest challenge to Coastal GasLink. The injunction was lifted. The pipeline is built. The gas is flowing. The hereditary chiefs who held the line for years are still holding it legally, but the physical fact of the pipeline through their territory is irreversible. She makes tea and goes back to the documents. The legal work continues because the legal work is what there is left.

What Presses

The weight on Cascadia

Columbia River, Oregon/Washington — The river is the archive of everything this bioregion has done to itself. Fourteen dams on the main stem, 90+ on the tributaries — a hydroelectric staircase that generates clean electricity for cities that grew up alongside it and killed 90% of the wild salmon that once ran here. The fish that remain — spring Chinook, diminished; sockeye, functionally extinct in some tributaries — swim past turbines and through heated reservoir water to spawning gravel that receives fewer of them each year. This March the snowpack that feeds their summer cold-water refuge is at half its normal depth. The Yakama Nation has treaty rights to these fish. The fish are nearly gone.

Wet'suwet'en Territory, British Columbia — The Wet'suwet'en hereditary chiefs hold title over 22,000 square kilometres of unceded territory in north-central BC — territory the Supreme Court of Canada recognised in 1997. The Coastal GasLink pipeline runs through it anyway, approved by elected band councils whose authority the hereditary chiefs contest, built under RCMP enforcement that removed land defenders from the path. The pipeline crosses headwaters that drain toward salmon rivers. The conflict fatalities registered in this data — 16 in three months — are almost certainly concentrated in these zones where extraction and treaty rights meet at gunpoint.

Portland, Oregon — The city has been under wildfire smoke advisories for cumulative weeks in each of the last five summers. The AQI readings during the worst events — above 400, beyond the scale's "hazardous" category — kept children indoors, forced emergency room visits for those with compromised lungs, and blanketed the city in an orange haze that blocked the mountains. The fires that generated the smoke were burning in forests where fire suppression for a century had built fuel loads that the climate now ignites. The smoke doesn't discriminate by income, but the ability to stay indoors does.

The Columbia River system generates 40% of US Pacific Northwest electricity from 90+ dams that have eliminated 90% of its wild salmon — the fish that carried marine nutrients into the forests, fed the bears that spread those nutrients further, and sustained Coast Salish, Yakama, and Nez Perce food sovereignty for thousands of years. The dams are kept because the electricity is politically easier to replace than the infrastructure. The salmon are not kept because their loss is distributed across species, ecosystems, and Indigenous communities that have less political weight than ratepayers.
The Trans Mountain Pipeline Expansion was built through Wet'suwet'en unceded territory over the objection of its hereditary chiefs — whose title the Supreme Court of Canada recognised in 1997 — using RCMP enforcement to remove land defenders. The pipeline is now operational. The legal challenges continue. This is the pattern: recognition of Indigenous title in law, extraction proceeding in fact, litigation as the only remaining terrain. The 16 conflict fatalities in three months are the human cost of that gap between the law on paper and the pipe in the ground.

Oil sands bitumen, shale gas, and softwood lumber leave this bioregion worth hundreds of billions annually — carried through pipelines that cross treaty lands whose holders were never asked. What does not come back is the wild salmon runs that 90 dams eliminated, the snowpack that climate change is thinning, or the 170 years of old-growth forest carbon that a century of industrial logging put into the atmosphere. 16 killed (3 months); shale oil and natural gas, corn, soybeans, and dairy products, bitumen crude oil (oil sands) leaving; precipitation 49% of normal; 176 fires (7 days).

The salmon carry the ocean into the mountains — marine nitrogen in their bodies becomes old-growth forest. When the salmon are gone, the forest slowly forgets the sea. We are watching this forgetting happen, and we know how to stop it, and we have not stopped it.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The four lower Snake River dams — Ice Harbor, Lower Monumental, Little Goose, Lower Granite — are the single intervention most likely to recover Columbia Basin Chinook salmon. Their removal has been recommended by NOAA Fisheries, supported by tribal nations, and blocked by agricultural irrigation and navigation interests in Idaho. The Columbia Basin Partnership and Save Our Wild Salmon coalition maintain public dashboards on the removal process. Idaho's congressional delegation and Berkshire Hathaway subsidiary PacifiCorp are the primary institutional obstacles. These positions are public record.
The Wet'suwet'en Nation's legal challenge to Coastal GasLink is active in BC courts. The hereditary chiefs' position — that the pipeline crosses unceded Wet'suwet'en territory without their free, prior, and informed consent — has been consistent since 2010. Coastal GasLink is owned by TC Energy (listed on NYSE: TRP). The company's FPIC commitments are in its sustainability reports; the gap between those commitments and the Wet'suwet'en situation is documented by the Business and Human Rights Resource Centre at business-humanrights.org.
Bristol Bay, Alaska, sustains the world's largest remaining sockeye salmon run — 50 million fish annually — and the Yup'ik and Dena'ina subsistence economies that depend on it. The Pebble Mine proposal (Northern Dynasty Minerals, listed on NYSE American: NAK) was vetoed by the EPA in 2023 under Clean Water Act authority. The company is pursuing new permits under the current administration. The EPA's veto rationale and the Northern Dynasty permit reapplication are both public documents. This is one of the cases where a legal framework held — and may not hold again.
