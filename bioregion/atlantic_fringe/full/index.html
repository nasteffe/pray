Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in County Cork, a dairy farmer walks to the parlour to begin the first milking. Her herd of 120 Friesians graze year-round on grass that grows because the Gulf Stream keeps this coast mild — the same latitude as Labrador, but green in January. The milk goes to the Glanbia tanker at the gate, then to the processing plant in Dungarvan, then as infant formula to China and as cheddar to the UK — a supply chain built on the assumption that Irish grass will always grow. The nitrogen she spreads to boost that grass runs into the Blackwater River, where salmon numbers have halved in twenty years. She knows this. The fertiliser bill and the fish kill are both real, and they come from the same field.

In Grimsby, a former fish processor now works the night shift at a supermarket distribution centre. The building stands where the ice factory used to be, where his father unloaded cod from Hull trawlers. The fish now arrives frozen from Iceland and Norway, landed at Immingham container port three miles away. He never sees a boat. His daughter is training as a wind turbine technician at Grimsby Institute — the offshore wind farms that have replaced the fishing grounds need maintenance crews, and the money is better than anything the town has offered in thirty years. The turbines stand in water where his grandfather fished. He is glad for her, and he does not romanticise what was lost, but he notices that the new jobs serve a different master.

In Paris, a civil servant approves the quarterly payment to Orano for uranium procurement. France's 56 reactors keep the lights on at a cost per kilowatt-hour that makes German industry envious. She has never been to Arlit, the mining town in northern Niger where the yellowcake originates — a town where Tuareg communities report elevated cancer rates and where the water table has dropped 75 metres since mining began in the 1970s. The connection between her light switch and the Arlit aquifer is real, but it has no name in her daily life.

What Presses

The weight on Atlantic Fringe

Grimsby, England — The ice factory is a supermarket warehouse now. The docks that processed 50,000 tonnes of cod a year handle container freight. The town's food bank usage has doubled since 2019. The average house price is GBP 120,000 — a third of the national median — and the young people who leave for university do not come back. Three miles offshore, the world's largest offshore wind farm cluster generates electricity for millions of homes. The construction and maintenance crews are based partly in Grimsby, and this is real work, genuinely needed. But the technicians are trained specialists, often recruited from elsewhere; the fish processors and net-menders and ice-factory workers did not become them. The transition is happening to the town, not through it.

Arlit, Niger — Eight hours north of Niamey by road, in the Saharan edge of the Air Mountains, Orano has operated uranium mines since 1971. The yellowcake travels by convoy to the port of Cotonou in Benin, then by ship to France, where it fuels the reactors that keep French electricity the cheapest in Western Europe. In Arlit, the water table has dropped 75 metres. Tuareg communities downwind of the tailings piles report rates of lung disease and cancer that local health workers have documented but that have never been the subject of an independent epidemiological study funded by Orano or the French state. The mine employs local workers, and the wages are better than anything else in the region — which is the point: dependency and extraction are not opposites but partners.

County Waterford, Ireland — The Suir runs through Waterford city to the sea. Upstream, the dairy herd has grown every year since quotas were abolished in 2015. The river is monitored by the EPA, which records declining water quality, rising phosphorus levels, and fewer salmon each survey. Downstream, a mussel farmer watches his harvest shrink as nutrient loading changes the estuary chemistry. He knows exactly where the nitrogen comes from. He also knows his neighbours — the dairy farmers are not villains but people responding to the same price signals, the same processor demands, the same subsidy structure that rewards volume over care. The harm is systemic, and the people doing it know it is harm, and they do not know what else to do.

The City of London and Dublin's IFSC form a two-node financial architecture that enables wealth extraction from the global south. London processes the capital; Dublin routes the corporate profits. The the people who bear the cost are not in either city — they are in Niger (foregone uranium revenue), in Nigeria (stolen oil wealth), in developing countries whose tax base is eroded by structures registered in Dublin and cleared through London. The compliance industry that monitors this system employs tens of thousands and catches a fraction.
France's 56 nuclear reactors produce 70% of national electricity at the lowest cost in Western Europe. The uranium comes from Niger, where Orano has mined since the 1970s. Arlit's water table has dropped 75 metres. Tuareg communities report elevated cancer rates. France gets energy security; Niger gets a hole in the ground and a depleted aquifer. The connection is direct, documented, and almost never discussed in French energy policy as a cost borne by someone.
Irish dairy intensification after the abolition of EU milk quotas in 2015 increased the national herd by 40%. The nitrogen from that herd flows into rivers where salmon populations have halved. The dairy cooperatives (Glanbia, Kerry) report record profits; the river communities downstream report fish kills. The same EU Common Agricultural Policy that subsidises the intensification funds the water quality monitoring that documents the damage.

Who Sustains This

His Majesty's Government (UK) / The Crown · Climate Change Act 2008 net-zero-by-2050 statutory framework + Sixth Carbon Budget; North Sea Transition Authority (NSTA) licensing rounds (Rosebank 2023, Jackdaw 2022, ongoing fields); Offshore wind contract-for-difference auctions (45GW target by 2030); Treasury revenue from petroleum revenue tax + energy-profits levy on North Sea producers

Driven by: We must lead the world to net zero while protecting British energy security and prosperity

The trap: The Net Zero Strategy and continued North Sea licensing are not contradictions in policy; they are co-functions of a state that needs the licensing revenue and the political coalition that fossil-fuel-producing regions provide. The AMOC weakening that climate science warns against is structurally produced by the same energy production the licensing rounds extend. The Crown's climate leadership and the Crown's licence to produce are the same instrument under different framings, in front of different audiences, with each framing's audience presupposing it does not see the other framing.

Bord Bia / Origin Green (Irish Food Board) · Irish Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine appropriation; Industry-levy funding from Irish dairy, meat, seafood, and horticulture exporters; Co-funding from Bord Bia member companies (Glanbia, Kerry, Dairygold, ABP, Lakeland Dairies); EU Common Agricultural Policy promotion-fund channeling

Driven by: We must position Ireland as the world's most sustainable food producer

The trap: Origin Green certification covers >90% of Irish food exports while the same dairy economy whose certification is the brand's value has missed its emissions targets every year and accelerated nitrate leaching across catchments the Water Framework Directive flags as deteriorating. The sustainability marketing is structurally what permits the herd expansion the marketing's commitments would otherwise constrain. The brand is not a commitment the industry will be held to; the brand is the device that allows the industry to continue while sustainability is asserted.

TotalEnergies SE · Global upstream oil and gas production (~2.5 mboe/day) across Africa, Atlantic deepwater, Middle East, Russia (pre-2022); Mozambique LNG (paused 2021 after Cabo Delgado insurgency; force-majeure ongoing); Atlantic deepwater development (Suriname Block 58, Namibia Venus, Brazil pre-salt); Integrated renewable-power generation portfolio (~22 GW gross, target 100 GW by 2030); European refining, marketing, and B2B chemicals (Antwerp, Normandy, La Mède biorefinery)

Driven by: We must lead the energy transition while continuing to supply the world with the energy it needs

The trap: The 'energy company, not oil company' rebrand is the political instrument that allows TotalEnergies to continue Atlantic deepwater development (Suriname, Namibia, GranMorgu) and Mozambique LNG while branding the upstream cash flow as the financing of the renewable transition. Each Atlantic licence round is justified as transition finance; each transition project depends on the upstream economics of the same hydrocarbons the transition claims to replace. The French state's historical-lineage connection with the company makes the contradiction sovereign as well as corporate — a national champion whose national-champion status absorbs the moral problem its operations would otherwise pose.

Six resource streams flow outward from the Atlantic Fringe — oil, uranium, dairy, wine, illicit capital, corporate tax revenue — worth hundreds of billions at their destination markets. What does not come back is the water quality in Irish rivers, the livelihoods in North Sea fishing towns, the health of Tuareg miners in Arlit, the tax revenue that might have funded hospitals in Letterkenny or schools in Lagos. The distance between extraction and cost is the system's essential feature. illicit financial flows, oil and gas, dairy products leaving; precipitation 32% of normal.

The harm is quiet here. No one is fleeing. The rivers are not drying up — they are turning green, slowly, from nitrogen that makes the grass grow faster so the milk can be cheaper so the farm can survive another year. The fisherman's daughter trains for the wind turbines. The compliance officer files the suspicious activity report. The uranium arrives on schedule. The system works, for now, for most, and the cost is elsewhere — in Arlit, in Lagos, in the river where the salmon used to run. Prayer for this place is not prayer for victims at a distance. It is prayer for the water under your own feet.
