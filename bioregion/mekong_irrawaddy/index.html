Lives Lived Here

In Dak Lak province, in Vietnam's Central Highlands, a Degar farmer tends coffee seedlings on land his family has worked for generations — land that the state reclassified as a coffee estate concession in the 1990s, pushing his community to the margins of their own territory. His coffee will be bought by a Vietnamese trader at $1.80 a kilogram and sold to Nestle at $3.50. The Nestle instant coffee made from his beans retails at the equivalent of $25 a kilogram in a Tokyo convenience store. He has never tasted instant coffee. He cannot afford it.

For the Rohingya…

For the Rohingya, stateless and confined — those in the vast camps of Bangladesh with no way home, and those still in Rakhine under what the world has named a genocide. That they are not forgotten in their long waiting.

For the ethnic hills…

For the Kachin, Karen, Shan and other peoples of the highlands, whose jade and timber and land have funded every side of Myanmar's wars while they are displaced from them. For their homes, and for a just place in whatever peace comes.

For the trafficked…

For those held in coercion across this region — the men enslaved on the fishing boats, the young lured into the scam compounds and unable to leave. For their release, and for the breaking of the trade in people.

For the wounded river…

For the great rivers, the Mekong and the Irrawaddy — dammed upstream, mined for sand, their fisheries and their floods that fed millions faltering. For the communities on their banks whose lives the rivers still hold.

For those who carry each other…

For the underground clinics and teachers, the cross-border aid workers, the monks and volunteers who shelter the displaced. That their quiet defiance outlasts the generals.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The shrimp in your supermarket freezer may have been peeled by a trafficked worker from Myanmar or Cambodia, held on a Thai fishing boat or in a Samut Prakan processing plant with his passport confiscated. The Environmental Justice Foundation (ejfoundation.org) documents these supply chains and publishes the names of companies. Thailand's seafood exports are worth $6 billion a year. The EU threatened trade sanctions over forced labour in 2015 and partially lifted them in 2019 after legislative reform. The reform is incomplete. The shrimp is still cheap. Ask your supermarket: where does your shrimp come from, and under what labour conditions was it processed?
The garments in your wardrobe were likely sewn in Cambodia or Vietnam. Cambodian garment workers earn $204 a month minimum wage (2024) for sixty-hour weeks; Vietnamese workers earn $200-250. The brands — H&M, Zara, Nike, Uniqlo — set the price per piece. The Clean Clothes Campaign (cleanclothes.org) tracks factory conditions and wage theft across Southeast Asian supply chains. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive requires companies to identify and address labour rights risks in their supply chains. It applies now. Ask the brands you buy from: what is the lowest wage in your supply chain, and who decided it?
The Mekong River Commission (mrcmekong.org) publishes data on dam impacts, sediment loss, and fishery decline across the basin. Eleven mainstream dams now control the river that feeds sixty million people. The dam operators are Chinese state enterprises. The electricity buyers are Thai industrial consumers. The cost bearers are Cambodian fishers and Vietnamese rice farmers. The MRC has no authority to compel China to release water or sediment. What exists is monitoring, data, and the slow accumulation of evidence that the river is being taken. That evidence is public. It names what is happening.
