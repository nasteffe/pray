Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on Tonle Sap, a Cambodian fisher checks his nets in the half-light. The lake is lower this year — the flood pulse came late and left early, the Chinese dams upstream holding back water for turbines in a dry month. His catch is smaller than his father's was, and his father's was smaller than his grandfather's. He sells what he has at the floating market to a trader who drives it to Phnom Penh, where a garment worker buys dried fish with part of the $204 she earned (2024 minimum wage) this month sewing fast fashion for H&M. She sends the rest home to her mother in Kampong Chhnang, who uses it to buy fertiliser — Syngenta brand, imported — for the rice paddy that no longer floods deeply enough to deposit its own nutrients.

In Samut Prakan, south of Bangkok, a Burmese migrant peels shrimp in a processing plant. He crossed the border two years ago after his village in Mon State was shelled. His passport is with his employer. The shrimp he processes will be frozen, boxed, and shipped to a supermarket in London or Atlanta, where it will cost less per kilo than the rice his wife grows back home — rice she irrigates from a tributary of the Mekong that runs lower each dry season because Laos sold the water rights to a Chinese dam company. She does not know where the water goes. He does not know where the shrimp goes. The supply chain connecting them passes through five countries and none of them can see it whole.

In Dak Lak province, in Vietnam's Central Highlands, a Degar farmer tends coffee seedlings on land his family has worked for generations — land that the state reclassified as a coffee estate concession in the 1990s, pushing his community to the margins of their own territory. His coffee will be bought by a Vietnamese trader at $1.80 a kilogram and sold to Nestle at $3.50. The Nestle instant coffee made from his beans retails at the equivalent of $25 a kilogram in a Tokyo convenience store. He has never tasted instant coffee. He cannot afford it.

The Mekong is being converted from a living river system into a hydroelectric supply chain. Eleven mainstream dams — built by Chinese state enterprises (CWE, POWERCHINA) and financed by Chinese state banks — trap the sediment that downstream rice paddies need, flatten the flood pulse that Tonle Sap's fishery depends on, and sell the electricity to Thailand's EGAT under contracts that transfer the river's productive capacity from sixty million subsistence farmers and fishers to industrial consumers who will never see the water. The Mekong Delta is sinking because the sediment that built it is now behind a wall in Yunnan.
Myanmar's jade trade connects Kachin indigenous dispossession to Chinese luxury consumption through a single supply chain worth $31 billion a year. The military extracts the jade; the jade funds the military; the military burns the villages; the villagers flee to the mines. Global Witness documented this cycle in 2015 and nothing changed, because the jade crosses the border before any accountability mechanism can touch it, and the consumer in Guangzhou or Hong Kong has no reason to know where the stone came from.
The clothes in your wardrobe and the shrimp in your freezer are connected by the same labour geography. Cambodian women sew garments for H&M at $204 a month (2024 minimum wage); Burmese men peel shrimp for Tesco at nothing a month. Both are sustained by the same structural condition: a surplus of displaced and desperate people whose only alternative is worse. The brands that profit from this labour set the price per piece low enough that the factory must compress the wage, and the worker absorbs the difference in her body — repetitive strain, respiratory illness, exhaustion. The supply chain is designed so that the cost is invisible at the point of purchase.

Myanmar military coup (2021) -> civil war across Kachin, Rakhine, Sagaing, Chin -> 3.5M displaced, 2,237 killed in 3 months, 1,873 healthcare attacks -> jade/timber extraction funds military operations -> displacement feeds labour migration to Thai fishing and processing -> trafficked workers supply cheap seafood exports to US/EU/Japan

Ten resource streams flow out of Southeast Asia — jade, shrimp, sand, garments, coffee, bauxite, nickel, bananas, timber, hydropower — worth tens of billions at their destination markets. What does not come back is the sediment, trapped behind dams that sell electricity to countries that did not lose a river. What does not come back is the labour, consumed in processing plants and fishing boats and garment factories at rates the human body was not designed to sustain. The river still reverses. Each June, the Mekong pushes backward into Tonle Sap, and the lake fills, and the fish come. It has done this for longer than any dam has stood. The question is not whether the river remembers. The question is whether we will let it.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The shrimp in your supermarket freezer may have been peeled by a trafficked worker from Myanmar or Cambodia, held on a Thai fishing boat or in a Samut Prakan processing plant with his passport confiscated. The Environmental Justice Foundation (ejfoundation.org) documents these supply chains and publishes the names of companies. Thailand's seafood exports are worth $6 billion a year. The EU threatened trade sanctions over forced labour in 2015 and partially lifted them in 2019 after legislative reform. The reform is incomplete. The shrimp is still cheap. Ask your supermarket: where does your shrimp come from, and under what labour conditions was it processed?
The garments in your wardrobe were likely sewn in Cambodia or Vietnam. Cambodian garment workers earn $204 a month minimum wage (2024) for sixty-hour weeks; Vietnamese workers earn $200-250. The brands — H&M, Zara, Nike, Uniqlo — set the price per piece. The Clean Clothes Campaign (cleanclothes.org) tracks factory conditions and wage theft across Southeast Asian supply chains. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive requires companies to identify and address labour rights risks in their supply chains. It applies now. Ask the brands you buy from: what is the lowest wage in your supply chain, and who decided it?
The Mekong River Commission (mrcmekong.org) publishes data on dam impacts, sediment loss, and fishery decline across the basin. Eleven mainstream dams now control the river that feeds sixty million people. The dam operators are Chinese state enterprises. The electricity buyers are Thai industrial consumers. The cost bearers are Cambodian fishers and Vietnamese rice farmers. The MRC has no authority to compel China to release water or sediment. What exists is monitoring, data, and the slow accumulation of evidence that the river is being taken. That evidence is public. It names what is happening.
