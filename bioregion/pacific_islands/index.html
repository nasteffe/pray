What Presses

The weight on Pacific Islands

Gold from Papua New Guinea's Porgera mine in Enga Province flows through Lae port to international refineries, enriching Barrick Gold and Zijin Mining while Ipili landowners receive inadequate compensation and communities downstream absorb mine tailings in their water. Tropical hardwood logs from the Solomon Islands' Western Province, Guadalcanal, and Isabel are shipped through Honiara port to China — 85% of all log exports — by Malaysian-linked logging companies that manipulate customary landowners into agreements that strip their forests and silt their rivers. Across the Pacific, distant-water fishing fleets from China, Taiwan, Japan, Korea, the Philippines, and the EU harvest tuna from the exclusive economic zones of Kiribati (3.5 million square kilometres), Tuvalu, the Marshall Islands, and the Federated States of Micronesia. These tuna licenses are often the primary government revenue for nations whose entire landmass could fit inside a single Australian cattle station. The fishing fleets take the pelagic stocks; coastal communities fish the depleted nearshore reefs. Tuvalu — 12,000 people on 26 square kilometres of atoll — earns revenue from its .tv internet domain, sold to streaming platforms, while the sea rises to erase the nation the domain is named for.

Seeds of Hope

Palau has closed 80% of its exclusive economic zone as a National Marine Sanctuary — 500,000 square kilometres of ocean protected despite the foregone licensing revenue, a sovereign act of ecological resistance by a nation of 18,000 people. Across Micronesia and Melanesia, customary reef tenure systems persist: village communities manage their own reef fisheries through traditional governance that has sustained stocks for millennia. The Parties to the Nauru Agreement (PNA) Vessel Day Scheme — designed by Pacific Island nations themselves — is the most significant attempt by small states to collectively control access to their own marine resources.
