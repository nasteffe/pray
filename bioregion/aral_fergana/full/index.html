Lives Lived Here

In the Fergana Valley, before the heat of the day, a farmer in Andijan opens the sluice gate on a canal branch that has not been maintained since independence. The concrete is cracked, the seepage wastes a third of the water before it reaches his cotton field, but the water still comes — snowmelt from the Tien Shan that fell as snow eight months ago on peaks he can see from his doorstep. His cotton will be ginned in a state facility outside Fergana city, baled, and railed to Tashkent. From Tashkent it travels by road or rail to textile mills in Bangladesh, Turkey, and China, where it becomes the fabric of shirts sold in shopping centres that have never heard of the Fergana Valley. His wife buys flour milled from Kazakh wheat that grew on the northern steppe — wheat irrigated by the same river system, six hundred kilometres upstream.

In Zhanaozen, on the Caspian shore, a Kazakh oil worker clocks on at the Tengiz field. The crude he pumps flows through the CPC pipeline — fifteen hundred kilometres across Kazakhstan and southern Russia to the Black Sea port of Novorossiysk — where tankers carry it to refineries in Italy and the Netherlands. Chevron holds fifty percent of the Tengiz consortium; the Kazakh state holds twenty. In 2011, oil workers in this town struck over wages and conditions. Police opened fire. Sixteen were killed. The square where they fell has been paved over. The pipeline still runs.

In Moynaq, a town that was once Uzbekistan's busiest fishing port, a Karakalpak grandmother hangs laundry on a line that faces the desert where the harbour used to be. The boats are rusting monuments on dry ground, photographed by tourists who drive in from Khiva. She does not fish. No one fishes. Her grandson has a cough that will not clear — the dust that blows off the former seabed carries salt and DDT residue and settles on everything: the laundry, the vegetables, the schoolyard. The nearest specialist clinic is in Nukus, forty minutes away if the shared taxi comes. She has been three times this year.

What Presses

The weight on Aral-Fergana Basin

Cotton, natural gas, uranium, gold, crude oil leave the region — Farming families (state-mandated production quotas until 2020, below-market prices), Agricultural workers (forced mobilisation for harvest documented by ILO until 2021), Aral Sea communities (Karakalpakstan — irrigation diversion destroyed the sea).

The same irrigation infrastructure that sustains thirty million livelihoods in the Fergana Valley and beyond destroyed the Aral Sea — and continues to operate without fundamental reform. The canals lose up to forty percent of their water to seepage; the cotton fields are salinising; the river no longer reaches the sea. The system is failing and indispensable at the same time. No country can afford to shut it down; no country can afford to keep it running as designed.
Kazakhstan produces forty-three percent of the world's uranium and billions of barrels of Caspian oil, yet the communities closest to extraction — Atyrau Region oil workers, Kyzylkum steppe communities near ISL wellfields, the population of Semey (formerly Semipalatinsk, where the Soviet Union conducted 456 nuclear tests and exposed 1.5 million people) — receive almost none of this wealth. The resource curse is not a metaphor in Kazakhstan; it is a postal code.
Migrant labour is the Fergana Valley's largest unofficial export. Over two million Central Asians — predominantly Uzbek, Kyrgyz, and Tajik men — work on Russian construction sites, sending remittances that account for up to thirty percent of Tajikistan's GDP. Their labour subsidises Russian infrastructure at wages Russian workers will not accept, while their absence restructures family life at home — women managing farms, children growing up with fathers who return once a year, if they return. When Russia's economy contracts or anti-migrant sentiment rises, the remittance flow drops and entire communities lose their primary income overnight.

Cascade Chains

Soviet irrigation diversion → Aral Sea desiccation → Exposed seabed releases toxic dust → Karakalpak respiratory illness, anaemia, cancer clusters → Children stunted and immunocompromised (UZB — 1.8M people in dust zone; 60%+ anaemia in women of childbearing age; TB rates among highest in former Soviet Union)

Upstream hydropower release in winter → Downstream flooding of fallow fields → Summer irrigation shortage → Cotton and wheat yield decline → Food import dependency (KGZ-TJK upstream vs UZB-KAZ-TKM downstream; Toktogul reservoir release schedule unresolved since 1991)

Glacial retreat on Tien Shan and Pamir → Peak water approaching (brief abundance, then permanent decline) → Irrigation system built for Soviet-era flow volumes becomes oversized → Five countries competing for a shrinking resource with no binding agreement (60M+ people dependent on glacial-fed rivers; 30% glacier mass loss since 1960s)

Who Sustains This

Uzpakhtasanoat / Uzbek cotton-textile cluster system · Vertically-integrated cotton clusters (territorially-allocated public-private holdings replacing the pre-2017 state monopoly) controlling ginning, spinning, weaving, garment; Domestic textile industry exports (~$3B+ annually) replacing raw-cotton exports as the value-capture point; World Bank and EBRD agricultural-reform lending channelled through cluster operators; State-allocated land-use rights and water quotas to cluster anchor enterprises

Driven by: We must transform Uzbekistan from raw-cotton exporter to value-added textile producer

The trap: The cluster reform ended ILO-flagged forced labour and built domestic textile capacity — both real, both important — while preserving the cotton-on-the-irrigation-grid land-use that drained the Aral in the first place. Karakalpak communities still breathe pesticide-salt dust from the exposed seabed regardless of who owns the gin. The sovereign-textile narrative redistributes value within the cotton economy; the water debt that economy owes remains unpaid because no one inside the cluster system has the standing to charge it. The reformed industry's profitability depends on the same water budget the unreformed industry could not pay.

Rogun HPP / Barki Tojik (Tajik state hydropower) · Soviet-era construction restart since 2008; sovereign-debt issuance backed by future electricity-export revenue; Domestic electricity tariffs (Tajik households + TALCO aluminium smelter, the dominant industrial offtaker); Anticipated CASA-1000 cross-border power exports to Afghanistan and Pakistan; World Bank Phase I disbursements (~$240M) and broader international financing envelope (~$5B remaining estimated need)

Driven by: We must complete Rogun to deliver energy independence and lift Tajikistan out of energy poverty

The trap: The dam that ends Tajik winter electricity rationing also gives Tajikistan unilateral control over the Amu Darya fill rate that Karakalpakstan, Khorezm, and Dashoguz farmers depend on for spring irrigation. Each Tajik household added to reliable electricity is paid for by water-allocation discretion exercised over populations downstream of a border that did not exist in Soviet hydrology. The energy-poverty framing the project's legitimacy rests on cannot acknowledge that the same hydrological pulse the dam captures is the pulse the downstream irrigation system was built around. The sovereignty being asserted is also the asymmetric power that sovereignty was supposed to escape.

Kazatomprom (Kazakh National Atomic Company) · In-situ recovery (ISR) uranium mining across 13 mines in central and southern Kazakhstan (~43% of global U3O8 supply); Kazakhstan Stock Exchange / London Stock Exchange dual listing; Long-term offtake contracts with US, French, Chinese, Indian, Korean, UAE reactor operators; Samruk-Kazyna sovereign-wealth fund majority shareholding

Driven by: We must supply the world's nuclear renaissance with the responsibly produced uranium it needs

The trap: In-situ leach acidifies aquifers that pastoralist communities and the Aral-Caspian basin depend on; the Soviet-era Semipalatinsk and Mayak legacies remain the ambient health context in which 'responsible' production is performed. The nuclear-renaissance demand structure converts Kazakh sovereign-resource policy into a price-taker role inside a Western/Russian/Chinese reactor economy the sovereignty was supposed to escape. Civil-nuclear's climate-credential function transfers moral premium to extraction whose costs accrue to steppe communities outside the climate accounting. The sovereignty is real and the climate moral framing is real; both operate downstream of the same steppe.

Five resource streams flow out of the Aral-Fergana Basin — cotton, crude oil, natural gas, uranium, gold — worth billions at their destination. What does not flow back is the water, consumed by irrigation that drained a sea; the health, degraded by dust and radiation and hydrogen sulphide; or the labour, sent as migrant workers to Russian construction sites because the basin''s own economy cannot employ its young. cotton, natural gas, uranium, gold leaving.

The Aral Sea did not die suddenly. It took forty years of cotton harvests, each one taking a little more water than the rivers could replace. The people who grew the cotton did not choose to drain the sea — they were told to plant, and they planted. The dust that now rises from the seabed and settles on their children is the repayment of a debt they did not incur. To pray for Karakalpakstan is to sit with the knowledge that slow destruction is still destruction, and that the ordinary way of things — growing fibre, wearing clothes, turning on the lights — has consequences that arrive as illness in a child who lives where a harbour used to be.

For the vanished sea...

For the people of Karakalpakstan and the old fishing town of Moynaq, whose sea drained away within a lifetime, leaving rusting boats on a poisoned seabed and dust storms that carry salt and pesticide into every breath and lung. For the children born into that air.

For those who pick the cotton...

For the farming families still bound to the cotton the water was drained to grow, and for the long legacy of forced labour in these fields. For work that is freely chosen and fairly paid.

For the labour migrants...

For the millions who leave these valleys to work in Russia and beyond, holding whole villages together with what they send home, and bearing suspicion and danger far from their families. For their dignity, and for economies at home that could keep them.

For the crowded valley...

For the peoples of the Fergana Valley packed against hard borders and competing for the same shrinking water, under governments that leave little room to speak. For water shared rather than fought over, and for room to breathe.

For those who plant against the dust...

For those replanting the dry Aral bed and reviving the old orchards and canals. That the slow work of healing a poisoned land is given time and help.

Looking Ahead

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The cotton in an ordinary T-shirt may have been grown in the Fergana Valley with water that would otherwise have reached the Aral Sea. Uzbekistan's cotton reforms since 2017 are real — the ILO verified the end of state-organised forced labour in 2022 — but the water cost remains: every hectare of irrigated cotton in the basin consumes water from rivers that no longer reach the sea. Ask where your cotton comes from. The Better Cotton Initiative and Cotton Campaign (cottonCampaign.org) track sourcing and reform progress.
Kazakhstan supplies forty-three percent of the world's uranium — the fuel in nuclear reactors that may generate your electricity. Kazatomprom, the state-owned producer, is listed on the London Stock Exchange. Its annual reports disclose production volumes but not community health data from the steppe towns near in-situ leaching operations, and they do not mention the 1.5 million people exposed by 456 Soviet nuclear tests at Semipalatinsk. The connection between your light switch and the Kyzylkum steppe is real, and it has a ticker symbol: KAP.
Over two million Central Asian migrant workers — many from the Fergana Valley — build Russia's roads, apartments, and stadiums at wages Russian workers will not accept. Their remittances sustain families across Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, and Tajikistan (up to 30% of Tajikistan's GDP). When anti-migrant sentiment rises in Russia, these families lose their income. The migrant labour circuit connects Central Asian poverty to Russian construction to the buildings and infrastructure that host international events. The connection is not abstract; it is structural.

Monitored and campaigned against state-imposed forced labor in Uzbekistan's cotton harvest for over a decade. Documented the system of compulsory mobilization of public sector workers, teachers, and students to pick cotton under threat of dismissal.

Crude Accountability investigation

Environmental and human rights organization focused on the Caspian region and Central Asia. Documents impacts of oil, gas, and uranium extraction on communities and ecosystems, including Kashagan field pollution and Navoi uranium mining.

Aral Sea Foundation solidarity

International Water Management Institute (IWMI) — Central Asia investigation
