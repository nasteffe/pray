Lives Lived Here

In Lunda Norte, a garimpeiro from Kasai-Central in DRC washes gravel through a sluice box at the edge of an Endiama concession. He has been expelled twice in the last three years and came back both times, because $8 a day in the gravels is more than he earns in Kasai. The diamond he finds today will reach Antwerp in three weeks. He will not know its price.

Stakeholder trap

Movimento Popular de Libertação de Angola

Driven by: We must preserve the continuity; the party that ended the colonial war and the civil war cannot now be the obstacle, and structural critique becomes a question of loyalty

The trap: The MPLA's legitimacy rests on having ended the colonial extractive economy, yet the post-war order — a narrow coastal apparatus exporting subsoil wealth and importing food, with the interior depopulated and its original cultivators landless — has the same structural shape the colonial economy produced.

Material role: Ruling party continuously since 1975 independence; political infrastructure through which oil and mining concession allocations, banking access, import licences, and security appointments are distributed; Post-2002 reconstruction economy financed by oil-backed Chinese credit lines and oil-collateralised infrastructure contracts (Eximbank, CDB); Party-state apparatus governing Sonangol, Endiama, the FAA, and the major banks through overlapping appointments rather than separate institutional tracks

Stakeholder trap

Sonangol E.P.

Driven by: We must keep production flowing; the debt service, the budget, and the diversification plan all depend on the next barrel

The trap: Sonangol is positioned as the vehicle by which Angola will free itself from oil dependency, yet the institution's survival — its payroll, its pre-financed loans, the fiscal floor it provides the state — requires the dependency to continue.

Material role: State concessionaire holding the Angolan side of every offshore licence (Block 0 / Block 17 / Block 32 partnerships with Chevron, TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, BP); ~1.1M barrels/day national crude production routed through Sonangol off-take; ~95% of merchandise exports and ~60% of fiscal revenue dependent on the company's flow; Cabinda enclave fields (~50% of national crude) operated under Sonangol concession while the province itself remains among the country's poorest; Pre-financed crude oil sales to Chinese state banks (Eximbank, CDB) servicing legacy reconstruction debt

Take Action

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

HALO Trust — Angola Programme solidarity

The largest humanitarian mine clearance organization operating in Angola, clearing an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 landmines across 1,100+ minefields on the central plateau. Enables Ovimbundu farming families to return to productive land.

Directly addresses the soil network blockage the briefing describes — landmines prevent agricultural return on the Planalto, trapping rural communities in urban poverty. Each cleared field reconnects a family to the land that sustained them before the civil war.

MAG (Mines Advisory Group) — Angola solidarity

Conducts mine clearance alongside HALO Trust in Angola's eastern provinces, focusing on community liaison and risk education. Works field by field to open land for farming in Lunda Norte, Moxico, and Bié provinces.

Partners with HALO Trust in the demining work the briefing's resistance narrative centers — Ovimbundu women planting maize and cassava in fields cleared meter by meter. Also works in the Lunda diamond provinces where artisanal miners face militarised concession security.

Kimberley Process Civil Society Coalition investigation

Coalition of NGOs monitoring the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme, which certifies diamonds as conflict-free. Advocates for expanding the definition of 'conflict diamond' to include state violence against mining communities.

Directly challenges the accountability gap the briefing identifies — Angola's artisanal diamond sector, which saw mass expulsion of Congolese miners in 2024, produces KP-certified diamonds because the KP definition excludes state violence. The diamonds reaching Antwerp and Dubai are 'certified' but not clean.

OMUNGA (Angolan Human Rights Organization) advocacy

Angolan civil society organization documenting human rights conditions in Cabinda Province and the Lunda diamond provinces, including forced evictions from mining concessions and environmental contamination from oil extraction.

Works in the two extraction zones the briefing describes — Cabinda (where Chevron has operated since 1966 under opaque Production Sharing Agreements) and the Lunda diamond gravels (where tens of thousands of garimpeiros work under militarised security).

Global Witness — Angola Oil and Diamond Transparency investigation

Investigates opaque revenue flows from Angola's oil and diamond sectors, tracking how extraction revenues are distributed and whether they reach public services. Has published landmark reports on Angola's missing oil money.

Follows the money the briefing traces — Chevron, TotalEnergies, BP extracting 1.1 million barrels/day while Angola imports more than half its food. Documents the gap between extraction wealth and the conditions faced by communities in Cabinda and the diamond provinces.
