Lives Lived Here

Before the rains fail completely, an Ovimbundu farmer in Bié province follows the demining team's cleared path into a field that her mother farmed before the war. The team marked safe ground with stakes; she plants maize between them, knowing the next field over is still mined and her neighbour won't be planting this season. The maize she grows will feed her household and sell at the Kuito market — some of it travelling five hours by road to Luanda, where her cousin works as a domestic worker in Talatona, the neighbourhood where oil company staff live behind walls.

In Lunda Norte, a garimpeiro from Kasai-Central in DRC washes gravel through a sluice box at the edge of an Endiama concession. He has been expelled twice in the last three years and came back both times, because $8 a day in the gravels is more than he earns in Kasai. The diamond he finds today will reach Antwerp in three weeks. He will not know its price.

The compound has a generator, a canteen, and a swimming pool. Her school has none of these. The reading is lower than last year, which was lower than the year before. What Presses

The weight on Angolan Escarpment and Central Plateau

Bié Plateau, Angola — Five hours east of Luanda at 1,500 metres, where demining teams have cleared a path through fields that the civil war seeded with landmines, the wet season is failing in its final weeks. Precipitation is running at less than a quarter of normal — the rain that should have saturated the soil, secured the harvest in April, and filled the canals feeding the Kwanza River has not come. An Ovimbundu farming family who returned to this land after the war, planted their first crops on demined fields over three years, and survived on those yields is watching the maize die in the ground. The landmines blocked their return for twenty years. The rain is blocking it now.

Lunda Norte, Angola — The Lunda provinces sit on some of the world's richest diamond gravels, and tens of thousands of garimpeiros work them under militarised concession security. The diamonds reach Antwerp and Dubai certified under the Kimberley Process as conflict-free — a certification that covers armed rebel groups but does not cover state violence against mining communities. Human Rights Watch documented extrajudicial killings, torture, and mass expulsion of tens of thousands of Congolese migrant miners in 2024. The miners return after each expulsion because subsistence in the Lunda gravels, under the guns, is still better than the alternative.

Cabinda, Angola — Cabinda is an oil-rich exclave separated from mainland Angola by a strip of DRC territory, producing roughly half of Angola's crude oil through Chevron's offshore operations that have run continuously since 1966. Cabinda Province's poverty rate is among the highest in the country. The FLEC separatist movement — which has argued for sixty years that Cabinda's resources should benefit Cabinda's people — is militarily suppressed. The distance between the offshore platform lights visible from the coast and the school without reliable electricity three hundred metres inland is not geographical. It is a decision.

Angola exports $30 billion in oil annually and imports more than half its food. The civil war destroyed the agricultural base; oil revenue replaced it; the revenue services Chinese-backed debt that predates the current government's ability to negotiate; and the 1.3 million food-insecure Angolans on the continent's most fertile plateaux are invisible to the economy that runs on their subsoil.
The Lobito Corridor — built by colonial forced labour, destroyed in the civil war, and now rebuilt with $1.6 billion in US and EU development finance — runs through Huambo and Moxico, Angola's most food-insecure provinces, carrying DRC cobalt and Zambian copper to the Atlantic. The corridor communities bear construction disruption, land acquisition, and unexploded ordnance risk. The mineral rents do not stay.

Oil, diamonds, and transit mineral rents flow outward from Angola worth tens of billions annually — 95% of export revenue from a single commodity employing less than 1% of the population. What does not flow back is demining funding for 1,100+ minefields, investment in the food systems that once fed this country from its own highlands, or any binding claim on the water Angola generates for four of its neighbours' river systems. 7 killed (3 months); 1.3M food insecure (current); crude oil and LNG, alluvial and kimberlite diamonds, headwater river flow (Cubango-Cuito system) leaving; precipitation 24% of normal.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Chevron has operated in Cabinda since 1966 under Production Sharing Agreements whose terms are among the most favourable to operators on the African continent. The FLEC movement's core demand is that Cabinda's resources should benefit Cabinda's people. The case is before the African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights (ACHPR). Chevron's Cabinda operations are described in its annual SEC filings, which are public.
The Kimberley Process Certification Scheme certifies diamonds as conflict-free — meaning free from armed rebel group financing. Angola's artisanal diamond sector, where Human Rights Watch documented extrajudicial violence and forced mass expulsions of Congolese miners in 2024, produces Kimberley Process-certified diamonds. The KP definition of conflict diamond does not cover state violence against mining communities.
The Lobito Corridor is funded by the US International Development Finance Corporation and EU Global Gateway as strategic infrastructure — explicitly framed in US government documents as countering Chinese influence in African critical minerals. The development case documents and the strategic competition documents are both public. The communities in Huambo and Moxico through which the corridor runs are mentioned in neither.
