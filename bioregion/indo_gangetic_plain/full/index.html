Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Sundarbans — the vast mangrove delta where Bangladesh meets the Bay of Bengal — a Munda honey collector prepares to enter tiger territory. He will spend three weeks in the forest, smoking bees from hives he has known since his father showed them to him, returning with honey that sells for less each year as the mangrove retreats and the saltwater advances. His village has lost ground to every cyclone since Sidr in 2007. His daughter works in a garment factory in Gazipur, stitching collars for a brand whose name she knows but whose clothes she could not afford. She sends money home before Eid.

In southern Punjab, a cotton farmer rises to check the canal. The Indus water that should be flowing has been allocated upstream — Punjab province takes its share before Sindh, and within Punjab, large estates take theirs before smallholders. His cotton will go to Faisalabad's textile mills and then to European retailers, but the price he receives barely covers the pesticide he applied last month. His nephew left for Dubai three years ago and works construction twelve hours a day. The remittance arrives monthly, minus the recruitment agent's fee that took two years' wages to repay. Without it, the farm would have been sold.

In Bihar, a Dalit agricultural labourer harvests rice on land she will never own. The paddy was planted when the monsoon arrived in June and will be threshed by December. Her daily wage is set by the landowner. Her son attends government school when he is not needed in the fields, which is to say he attends intermittently. The rice she harvests enters the Public Distribution System — India's vast subsidised food network — but the ration she receives from it arrives irregularly and never in full. She is simultaneously the producer and the inadequately served consumer of the same grain.

What Presses

The weight on Indo-Gangetic Plain

Ready-made garments, ganges-brahmaputra-meghna river system, indus river water, cotton textiles leave the region — 4 million garment workers (80% women, $95/month minimum wage), workers in unsafe factories (Rana Plaza precedent), communities near tanneries (toxic waste). 25.8 million cannot meet daily food needs. 1,618 killed in three months of conflict.

Pakistan withdraws 110 percent of its renewable water supply. The Indus canal system — the world's largest contiguous irrigation network — serves Punjab first and Sindh last, large estates before smallholders, commercial crops before subsistence farming. The engineering that made the Indus basin productive also encoded who would bear the cost of scarcity: Sindhi haris, Indus delta fishers, and the communities downstream of every barrage. When climate change intensifies both drought and flood, the same infrastructure that distributes water distributes harm.
The shirt on your back likely passed through Bangladeshi hands. Four million garment workers produce for brands that publish sustainability reports while paying wages that the Asia Floor Wage Alliance calculates at one-fifth of a living wage. The Rana Plaza collapse killed 1,134 people and changed building codes but not the pricing model. The women who sew are often themselves climate migrants — displaced from rural Bangladesh by the same monsoon intensification that this briefing describes. They are not a metaphor for global inequality; they are the mechanism of it.
The stubble fires in Punjab and the asthma inhalers in Delhi are connected by a two-week window. After rice harvest, wheat must be sown. Burning is the only method most farmers can afford to clear fields in time. The smoke drifts 300 kilometres southeast and settles over 600 million people. The farmers are blamed; the children are hospitalised; the window remains two weeks. No one in this chain chose the situation — but the costs fall on those who can least avoid them: children in Delhi's poorer neighbourhoods, without air purifiers or sealed classrooms.

Cascade Chains

Water stress in Pakistan's Indus basin (110% withdrawal vs renewable supply) reduces agricultural yields in Sindh, where tail-end farmers receive water last. Crop failure compounds existing food insecurity (7.5 million in IPC 3+). Bonded tenant farmers cannot relocate — their debts tie them to the land. When the monsoon overcompensates with extreme flooding, as in 2022, the same communities absorb the surplus that the deficit denied them: drought and flood are not opposites here but phases of the same dispossession.

Himalayan glacier retreat reduces dry-season river flow in the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Indus systems. Groundwater pumping intensifies to compensate (India's Punjab aquifer drops 1 metre per year). Deeper wells cost more, excluding smallholders. Women and girls walk further for water. Farmer debt rises with well depth; in India's 'suicide belt' (Maharashtra, Telangana, Karnataka), the connection between failed wells, unpayable loans, and farmer suicide is documented but unbroken.

Global fast-fashion demand drives garment production volume in Bangladesh. Brands compete on price, compressing factory margins. Factory owners compress wages (currently ~$113/month minimum, set December 2023). Workers — predominantly women from flood-affected rural districts — accept conditions because the alternative is subsistence farming on land that floods more frequently. Climate displacement feeds the labour supply that makes the price point possible.

Indian Security Forces · state budget (world's 4th largest military expenditure)

Driven by: We must defend the nation from Pakistan, China, and internal threats

The trap: Securing Kashmir through occupation that delegitimizes the claim. India asserts Kashmir is an integral part of the nation, yet governs it through emergency powers, internet shutdowns, and mass detention that no other state would apply to its own territory. The security apparatus designed to prove sovereignty demonstrates its absence.

Rapid Action Battalion · state budget; garment industry protection rackets (alleged)

Driven by: We must eliminate crime, drugs, and extremism by any means

The trap: Fighting lawlessness through extrajudicial killings that embody it. RAB was created to combat organized crime but became a mechanism for political repression and extortion. The US sanctions for human rights abuses against the unit that claims to uphold order expose the contradiction: the cure reproduces the disease.

Six resource streams flow outward from South Asia — garments, cotton textiles, steel from scrapped ships, rice, wheat, and human labour itself — worth tens of billions at destination markets. What does not come back is the water, consumed by agriculture and industry faster than the monsoon replaces it; and the health, absorbed by shipbreaking yards and garment factories and Gulf construction sites where the workers' bodies are the cheapest input in the chain. 1,618 killed (3 months); 25.8M food insecure (current); ready-made garments, Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna river system, Indus river water leaving.

The monsoon does not negotiate. It arrives, and everything downstream responds — the river rises or falls, the paddy fills or fails, the char island appears or dissolves. What the monsoon cannot do is decide who stands in the flood and who stands on higher ground. That decision was made by canal engineers, by landlords, by factory owners, by the recruitment agents who arrange passage to the Gulf. The water is not the problem. The problem is who gets to be dry.

For those who sew the world's clothes...

For the garment workers of the great river-delta cities — millions of them, most of them women — sewing for the world for a wage that barely feeds them, in buildings that have collapsed before. For safe walls, a living wage, and respect.

For the flood plain...

For the heat...

For the shared and contested water...

For the farmers at the tail ends of the Indus and Ganges canals, last in line for water drawn off upstream, and for wisdom among nations that share these rivers. That the water is shared as a gift, not hoarded as a weapon.

For the union organizers, the flood-shelter volunteers, the women's self-help groups across this vast plain. That the patient strength of ordinary people is honoured and answered.

Seeds of Hope

In Bangladesh, garment workers have organised repeatedly despite legal and physical intimidation — the 2023-24 wage protests that won a minimum wage increase from ~$75 to ~$113 per month (12,500 taka, effective December 2023 — a 56% increase, still below any calculation of a living wage) involved hundreds of thousands of workers walking out of factories that supply the world's largest fashion brands.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Check the label on your shirt. If it says Made in Bangladesh, it was likely stitched by a woman earning less than $100 a month in a factory in the Dhaka-Gazipur corridor. The Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety (now the International Accord) is a legally binding agreement between brands and trade unions — you can check whether the brands you buy from are signatories at internationalaccord.org. If they are not, ask them why. This is not abstract: the Accord exists because 1,134 people died in the Rana Plaza collapse, and the brands whose clothes were found in the rubble initially denied responsibility.
The remittances that sustain tens of millions of South Asian households begin with a recruitment fee. Workers bound for Gulf construction or domestic work pay agents the equivalent of one to two years' wages for the job placement — arriving in debt, under kafala sponsorship that ties their visa to their employer. The ILO's Fair Recruitment Initiative documents these practices and names the companies involved. If your country hosts South Asian migrant workers, the labour protections they receive (or do not receive) are a matter of domestic policy, not distant charity.
India's 2020-21 farmer protests — 250,000 people camped at Delhi's borders for thirteen months — succeeded in repealing three agricultural reform laws that would have exposed smallholders to corporate price-setting. The Samyukta Kisan Morcha (United Farmers' Front) that organised the protests remains active and documents ongoing agrarian crisis. Their work is visible at aikscc.com. The protest was covered as disruption in international media; for the farmers, it was the alternative to losing their land.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Clean Clothes Campaign advocacy

Global alliance dedicated to improving working conditions in the garment industry. Campaigns for living wages, union rights, and corporate accountability in Bangladesh, India, and Pakistan garment factories supplying European and American brands.

Targets the garment labor flow the briefing traces — where 4 million mostly women workers in Dhaka's factories produce fast fashion under conditions that the Rana Plaza collapse exposed, connecting brand purchasing practices to wage theft, building safety, and suppression of organizing.

Bangladesh Environmental Lawyers Association (BELA) advocacy

Public interest law organization litigating environmental cases in Bangladesh, including tannery pollution in Hazaribagh, ship-breaking contamination in Chittagong, and river encroachment by industrial estates. Files constitutional writ petitions on behalf of affected communities.

Litigates at the nodes where the briefing's industrial flows produce environmental sacrifice zones — Chittagong shipbreaking yards where workers dismantle tankers containing asbestos and heavy metals, and tannery effluent from Dhaka's leather industry poisoning the Buriganga River.

South Asian Network for Development and Environmental Economics (SANDEE) investigation

Research network producing evidence on environmental-economic linkages across South Asia, including monsoon variability impacts on rice yields, flood damage costs, and the economics of groundwater depletion in the Indo-Gangetic plain.

Quantifies the climate-agriculture nexus the briefing documents — how monsoon shifts alter rice production for 500 million people, how groundwater pumping for irrigation is depleting aquifers across Punjab and Bihar, and the economic losses from increasingly erratic flooding in Bangladesh.

National Fishworkers' Forum (NFF) India solidarity

Federation of fish worker trade unions across India representing 3.5 million artisanal and small-scale fishers. Advocates against industrial trawling, coastal zone encroachment, and port expansion that displaces traditional fishing communities along the Ganges delta and Bay of Bengal.

Represents the Ganges fishery cost-bearers the briefing identifies — artisanal fishers whose livelihoods are destroyed by industrial pollution, dam-altered sediment flows, and coastal aquaculture expansion, connecting upstream infrastructure decisions to downstream livelihood collapse.

FIDH (International Federation for Human Rights) — South Asia investigation

Documents human rights violations linked to economic exploitation across South Asia, including bonded labor in brick kilns, land grabs for special economic zones, and worker deaths in shipbreaking. Publishes field investigation reports with survivor testimony.

Investigates the labor exploitation the briefing traces across multiple flows — bonded labor in India's brick kilns feeding urban construction, migrant worker deaths in Chittagong's shipbreaking yards, and garment factory conditions that treat workers as disposable inputs to global supply chains.

International Collective in Support of Fishworkers (ICSF) solidarity

International organization supporting small-scale fishworker rights, based in Chennai. Documents how coastal industrialization, blue economy policies, and climate change impact fishing communities across the Bay of Bengal, Arabian Sea, and inland waterways.

Connects the Ganges fishery and coastal livelihoods the briefing documents — where Sundarbans fishing communities face simultaneous pressures from cyclone intensification, mangrove destruction for shrimp aquaculture, and upstream pollution, naming the people whose food sovereignty depends on these flows.
