What Presses

The weight on Mediterranean Fire-Water

Water is the primary extraction — aquifers in southern Spain, Morocco, and Tunisia are pumped beyond recharge to irrigate export crops (strawberries, tomatoes, citrus) destined for northern European supermarkets, employing migrant farmworkers from sub-Saharan Africa at wages that would be illegal in the destination countries. Tunisia imports wheat through Sfax port because subsidised European grain undercuts domestic production — the same EU agricultural policy that pays French farmers also destroys Tunisian ones. The Mediterranean's fish stocks have been depleted by industrial trawling, with 62% of stocks overfished according to FAO, while small-scale fishers in Morocco, Greece, and Tunisia lose access to waters they have fished for generations.

Northern European supermarket demand for year-round strawberries and tomatoes drives greenhouse construction in southern Spain and Morocco that pumps aquifers below recharge — the same supply chain that makes fresh produce cheap in London and Berlin makes water scarce in Almería and Agadir.
Tunisia's wheat dependency is not a natural outcome of drought but of EU Common Agricultural Policy subsidies that make European grain cheaper than what Tunisian farmers can grow — a trade structure that turns a food-producing nation into a food-importing one, then blames climate for the deficit.

Seeds of Hope

Moroccan argan oil cooperatives, run entirely by women, have become a model of community resource governance — protecting the trees that hold soil on Atlas slopes while providing income independent of male-controlled agriculture. In Spain, small-scale farmers in Andalusia challenge the water permits that allow industrial greenhouses to drain the Doñana wetlands, and Greek fishing cooperatives on the Aegean islands maintain traditional practices that keep inshore stocks viable where industrial fleets have stripped them. Tunisian seed-saving networks preserve local wheat varieties adapted to drought conditions that industrial monocultures cannot survive.

Looking Ahead

The Mediterranean summer dry season (June-September) approaches with aquifers already depleted and precipitation at 22% of normal. Fire risk is extreme — the conditions that produced Greece's 2023 megafires and Portugal's 2024 season are present again. Tunisia's fiscal fragility (wheat subsidy costs rise with global prices) creates food security risk if the next harvest fails.

The Mediterranean basin invented agriculture. That it is now importing the grain it taught the world to grow — because the water that grew it has been pumped into plastic greenhouses for export — is not irony but consequence.
