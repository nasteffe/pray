Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in a shanty settlement outside El Ejido, Almería, a Senegalese man named Moussa — his real name withheld because he has no papers — walks two kilometres to the greenhouse where he will spend ten hours picking peppers in 45-degree heat under plastic. He earns thirty-two euros for the shift, when the contractor pays. The peppers will be in a Dutch supermarket by Thursday. Moussa sends two hundred euros a month to his family in Tambacounda. He has been in Spain for four years and has never seen a doctor. His settlement has no running water. The peppers he picks are washed, graded, and packed in a facility with climate control and employee canteen three hundred metres from where he sleeps.

On the hill above Jaén, María tends sixty olive trees that her family has worked for four generations. The trees are ancient — thick-trunked, low-canopied, adapted to the thin soil and the summer heat that has always defined this landscape. Last year the rain did not come until December, a month late, and the olives shrivelled on the branch. She produced 800 litres of oil instead of 2,400. The cooperative paid her three euros sixty a litre — double the price of two years ago, but on a third of the volume, she earned less than before. The super-intensive plantation in the valley below, owned by a Portuguese investment fund, produced at full capacity because it irrigates from a borehole 200 metres deep. María's well is 40 metres. She can hear the difference.

In Lampedusa, Ferruccio has fished these waters for thirty years. He remembers when the swordfish came in July and the nets came up full. Now the industrial trawlers from further north have taken the offshore stocks, and what remains nearshore is fought over by a shrinking fleet. What Ferruccio pulls from the water now, more often than he will say, are not fish. The island received 7,000 people in a single day in September 2023. Ferruccio's wife Maria serves meals at the parish hall three days a week to whoever arrives. They have been doing this for ten years. No one from Rome has asked them how.

What Presses

The weight on Western Mediterranean

Almería, Spain — Behind the coast, where the tourist brochures show white villages and blue sea, 30,000 hectares of plastic greenhouses cover the ground like a second skin. Inside, the temperature reaches 50 degrees in summer. Moussa and 80,000 other migrant workers — a significant number without documents — pick peppers, tomatoes, and strawberries for four euros an hour when the contractor pays, which is not always. Their settlements cluster at the edges of the greenhouses: structures of cardboard, plastic sheeting, and salvaged wood, without running water, without sanitation, without addresses. A Spanish labour inspector in 2019 called these conditions "the worst in Europe." The produce they pick reaches German and Dutch supermarkets within 48 hours. Moussa has never been inside one.

Lampedusa, Italy — The island is closer to Tunisia than to Sicily. Its 6,000 residents have watched the central Mediterranean migration corridor pass through their harbour for twenty years. In September 2023, 7,000 people arrived in a single day on an island with capacity for 400. The parish of San Gerlando opened its doors. The women of the island cooked. Ferruccio and the other fishermen brought blankets from their own homes. Rome sent a minister for a press conference and then left. The bodies that wash ashore — and they still wash ashore — are buried in a cemetery at the edge of town. The graves have numbers, not names. The islanders tend them anyway.

Rural Alentejo, Portugal — In the hills east of Évora, cork oak forests that sustained communities for centuries are being replaced by super-intensive olive plantations backed by Spanish and Portuguese investment funds. The new plantations use irrigation at a scale the region has never seen, drawing from an aquifer that was stable for generations. Cork harvesters who earned five to seven euros a day stripping bark in summer heat are losing work as the oaks are cleared. The Iberian lynx — recovered from 94 animals (2002) to over 2,000 by 2023–24 — still loses scrubland each season to monoculture. The villagers are old. Their children live in Lisbon. The investment fund is registered in a jurisdiction that does not require them to report water use.

The food on northern European tables is produced by a labour system that would be illegal if applied to citizens. Almería's greenhouses and Italy's tomato fields depend on undocumented workers whose lack of legal status makes exploitation not a risk but a certainty — wage theft, unsafe conditions, no healthcare, housing in structures built from agricultural waste. The price of a punnet of Spanish strawberries in a Berlin supermarket reflects the cost of seed, fertiliser, plastic, transport, and retail margin. It does not reflect the cost of Moussa.
The Mediterranean is the deadliest migration corridor on earth — over 30,000 drowned since 2014 — and the same countries whose agricultural sectors depend on migrant labour fund the border enforcement that makes the crossing lethal. Italy needs the workers who arrive on Lampedusa. It does not want them to arrive on Lampedusa. This is not a policy contradiction that can be resolved by better policy. It is the structure of the system: cheap labour requires vulnerable people, and vulnerable people require dangerous journeys.
The olive oil crisis and the wildfire crisis are the same crisis seen from different angles. When smallholders abandon groves because drought and price competition make them unviable, the terraces that held the hillside together for centuries collapse. Brush fills the gaps. The fire comes. After the fire, rain hits bare soil and floods the valley. The cycle does not reset — each turn leaves less soil, fewer farmers, more fuel. The industrial plantation in the valley, irrigated from a deep borehole, survives every iteration. The landscape around it does not.

Cascade Chains

Aquifer depletion for greenhouse agriculture → groundwater decline → smallholder wells go dry first → farm abandonment → fuel load accumulation on unmanaged land → megafire risk (ESP — Almería consuming fossil groundwater; 40% rural population loss since 1950 creating unmanaged fire fuel)

Rural depopulation → abandoned terraces and firebreaks → 60 years of fuel accumulation → megafire exceeding suppression capacity → bare soil → intense rainfall on burned slopes → flooding and erosion → further depopulation (PRT/GRC — 400,000+ ha burned in Greece 2023; 117 killed in Portugal 2017 fires)

Northern European supermarket price pressure → greenhouse operators cut labour costs → undocumented migrant workers accept below-minimum wages → shanty town settlements with no water or sanitation → health crises invisible to consumers (ESP/ITA — caporalato controls 400,000 workers in Italian agriculture; Almería settlements lack running water)

Mediterranean crossing → arrival on Lampedusa or Aegean islands → overwhelmed reception → EU relocation failures → irregular onward movement → exploitation in agricultural labour circuits → the same workers harvesting the food the same Europe eats (30,000+ drowned since 2014; Rosarno riots 2010; Foggia ghetto fires killing workers in their sleep)

Who Sustains This

Coexphal (Almería Producer-Exporter Association) · Member cooperatives across the Almería 'mar de plástico' (~32,000 ha of plastic greenhouse covering Campo de Dalías and Campo de Níjar); Northern European supermarket contracts (Aldi, Lidl, Tesco, Carrefour, Edeka) — winter produce destination; EU Common Agricultural Policy producer-organisation funds (Regulation 1308/2013)

Driven by: We must remain the year-round vegetable garden of northern Europe

The trap: The cooperative model defends the small Almería grower against the supermarket chain while binding that grower to the chain's price expectations. Each certification, each cold-chain investment, each photovoltaic upgrade is financed against a forward contract whose price has been set by a buyer whose own consumer demand structure presupposes the labour and water arrangements the certification claims to address. The longer the supply relationship endures, the more groundwater has been drawn and the more Senegalese, Malian, and Moroccan harvest labour has been housed in greenhouses' lee — both invisible to the consumer the cooperative ultimately serves.

Coldiretti (Italian National Confederation of Farmers) · Membership dues from ~1.6 million Italian farm enterprises (largest agricultural organisation in Europe); Service-bureau revenue from accounting, payroll, and CAP-subsidy advisory to member farms; EU CAP support flowing through member operations (~€7B/year to Italy)

Driven by: We must defend Italian agriculture, Italian terroir, the Italian table

The trap: The federation defends the symbolic value of Italian agriculture — the terroir, the family farm, the Sunday table — through legal and regulatory frameworks whose underlying economic operation depends on a labour force the symbolic framework cannot acknowledge. Each Made-in-Italy campaign, each DOP defense, each anti-synthetic-food press release reproduces the consumer fantasy of an Italian harvest whose actual hands are those of Senegalese, Burkinabé, and Tunisian workers housed in baraccopoli the same federation's electoral allies treat as a security problem. The longer the symbolic defense succeeds, the more entrenched the labour regime that funds it becomes.

Frontex (European Border and Coast Guard Agency, Central Mediterranean operations) · EU general budget — €845M operational budget (2024), tripled since 2019; Standing Corps (~10,000 border guards planned by 2027, ~5,500 deployed); Asset pool from member states (vessels, aircraft, drones — including Libya-coast surveillance under JO Themis)

Driven by: We must save lives at sea and we must protect Europe's external borders

The trap: The agency's dual mandate — saving lives at sea and securing the external border — operates a single physical apparatus that produces both effects simultaneously. Each vessel, drone, and member-state asset deployed to reduce migrant deaths also closes the channels through which migrants could safely arrive; each maritime rescue feeds intercepted persons into the Libyan and Tunisian detention systems whose conditions are the proximate driver of the next attempt. The Standing Corps' growth is framed as a humanitarian instrument, but the structural function of the border it patrols is to ensure that the migrant economy serving southern European agriculture remains an informal one — admitted enough to harvest, excluded enough to be unprotected.

TotalEnergies SE · Global upstream oil and gas production (~2.5 mboe/day) across Africa, Atlantic deepwater, Middle East, Russia (pre-2022); Mozambique LNG (paused 2021 after Cabo Delgado insurgency; force-majeure ongoing); Atlantic deepwater development (Suriname Block 58, Namibia Venus, Brazil pre-salt); Integrated renewable-power generation portfolio (~22 GW gross, target 100 GW by 2030); European refining, marketing, and B2B chemicals (Antwerp, Normandy, La Mède biorefinery)

Driven by: We must lead the energy transition while continuing to supply the world with the energy it needs

The trap: The 'energy company, not oil company' rebrand is the political instrument that allows TotalEnergies to continue Atlantic deepwater development (Suriname, Namibia, GranMorgu) and Mozambique LNG while branding the upstream cash flow as the financing of the renewable transition. Each Atlantic licence round is justified as transition finance; each transition project depends on the upstream economics of the same hydrocarbons the transition claims to replace. The French state's historical-lineage connection with the company makes the contradiction sovereign as well as corporate — a national champion whose national-champion status absorbs the moral problem its operations would otherwise pose.

Olive oil, tomatoes, wine, cork, lithium, garments, strawberries — each leaves the Western Mediterranean carrying a cost that arrives nowhere. The migrant worker's wages do not appear on the label. The aquifer drawdown does not appear in the price. The drowned do not appear in the trade statistics. What leaves is value. What remains is thinner soil, lower water tables, emptier villages, and the people who cannot afford to leave. global merchant shipping, cork bark, lithium leaving; precipitation 51% of normal.

The olive tree does not know that the aquifer beneath it is falling. It reaches deeper, as it always has, finding what the rock offers. The people who planted it eight centuries ago could not have imagined a borehole two hundred metres deep draining the water table for an investment fund registered in Lisbon. But the tree is still standing. The question the tree asks, by standing, is whether the landscape it holds together — the soil, the insects, the shade, the rain it draws by breathing — will outlast the economy that is draining it. The tree does not ask this in words. It asks it by fruiting, or by failing to fruit, which is the same question.

For the gifts of this place...

For the gifts of this sea and its shores — for olive and vine and citrus, for old cities and long warm light, for a middle sea that has carried trade and pilgrims and ideas for three thousand years. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what our comfort costs elsewhere...

For the hands behind our ease: the migrant workers bent double in the tomato fields of the south and the plastic greenhouses of Almería for a few euros an hour, the seafarers from the Philippines and Indonesia crewing the ships that bring us everything. Forgive the ease of not asking. Teach us to trace what we hold back to the people who bear its cost.

For those who do not all arrive...

For those who come to this sea from the south and the east and do not all reach the far shore — the ones who drown in the crossing, the ones held in island camps, the ones we would rather not see. That this coast, which has always taken in the shipwrecked, does not forget how.

For quietness...

This week, this place asks of us not alarm but attention. For the discipline to pray without inventing an emergency; for gratitude that does not curdle into complacency; and for love that looks outward from a safe place toward the places — and the boats — that are not safe.

Seeds of Hope

In Rosarno, Calabria, African harvest workers organised a street protest in 2010 after years of wage theft and violence — the Rosarno riots became the first time Italian media covered the caporalato system as a systemic problem rather than an isolated incident. The workers were beaten, and many were deported, but the legal and journalistic attention they created led to Italy's anti-caporalato law (Law 199/2016), which for the first time criminalised the labour intermediaries who control the harvest workforce.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The olive oil in your kitchen almost certainly comes from Spain or Italy. Spain produces half the world's supply. When drought halved the 2022–23 harvest, supermarket prices doubled — but the smallholders of Jaén earned less, not more, because they had less to sell. The industrial operators with deep boreholes earned more. Next time you buy olive oil, look for cooperative or PDO (Protected Designation of Origin) labels: these indicate smallholder production. The price is higher. The difference is the cost of the aquifer and the farmer.
The central Mediterranean migration corridor has killed over 30,000 people since 2014. The International Organization for Migration's Missing Migrants Project tracks every recorded death (missingmigrants.iom.int). The people who drown are not statistics — they are named, from specific towns, missed by specific families. IOM publishes their data openly. If your government has a position on Mediterranean migration policy, this is the cost it is managing or failing to manage.
Italy's Law 199/2016 criminalises the caporalato system — the illegal labour intermediaries who control agricultural workers' access to harvest work across southern Italy. The law was won by Rosarno workers who protested in 2010 and Manolada workers who took Greece to the European Court of Human Rights in 2013. Enforcement remains weak: the Ferrante Ferranti Foundation and the FLAI-CGIL agricultural workers' union document ongoing violations. Their reports are public. Supporting agricultural labour rights organisations in southern Europe is as concrete a form of solidarity as exists.
