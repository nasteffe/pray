Lives Lived Here

In Lampedusa, Ferruccio has fished these waters for thirty years. He remembers when the swordfish came in July and the nets came up full. Now the industrial trawlers from further north have taken the offshore stocks, and what remains nearshore is fought over by a shrinking fleet. What Ferruccio pulls from the water now, more often than he will say, are not fish. The island received 7,000 people in a single day in September 2023. Ferruccio's wife Maria serves meals at the parish hall three days a week to whoever arrives. They have been doing this for ten years. No one from Rome has asked them how.

For Prayer

Last updated on 2026-07-20.

For the gifts of this place…

For the gifts of this sea and its shores — for olive and vine and citrus, for old cities and long warm light, for a middle sea that has carried trade and pilgrims and ideas for three thousand years. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what our comfort costs elsewhere…

For the hands behind our ease: the migrant workers bent double in the tomato fields of the south and the plastic greenhouses of Almería for a few euros an hour, the seafarers from the Philippines and Indonesia crewing the ships that bring us everything. Forgive the ease of not asking. Teach us to trace what we hold back to the people who bear its cost.

For those who do not all arrive…

For those who come to this sea from the south and the east and do not all reach the far shore — the ones who drown in the crossing, the ones held in island camps, the ones we would rather not see. That this coast, which has always taken in the shipwrecked, does not forget how.

For quietness…

This week, this place asks of us not alarm but attention. For the discipline to pray without inventing an emergency; for gratitude that does not curdle into complacency; and for love that looks outward from a safe place toward the places — and the boats — that are not safe.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The olive oil in your kitchen almost certainly comes from Spain or Italy. Spain produces half the world's supply. When drought halved the 2022–23 harvest, supermarket prices doubled — but the smallholders of Jaén earned less, not more, because they had less to sell. The industrial operators with deep boreholes earned more. Next time you buy olive oil, look for cooperative or PDO (Protected Designation of Origin) labels: these indicate smallholder production. The price is higher. The difference is the cost of the aquifer and the farmer.
The central Mediterranean migration corridor has killed over 30,000 people since 2014. The International Organization for Migration's Missing Migrants Project tracks every recorded death (missingmigrants.iom.int). The people who drown are not statistics — they are named, from specific towns, missed by specific families. IOM publishes their data openly. If your government has a position on Mediterranean migration policy, this is the cost it is managing or failing to manage.
Italy's Law 199/2016 criminalises the caporalato system — the illegal labour intermediaries who control agricultural workers' access to harvest work across southern Italy. The law was won by Rosarno workers who protested in 2010 and Manolada workers who took Greece to the European Court of Human Rights in 2013. Enforcement remains weak: the Ferrante Ferranti Foundation and the FLAI-CGIL agricultural workers' union document ongoing violations. Their reports are public. Supporting agricultural labour rights organisations in southern Europe is as concrete a form of solidarity as exists.
