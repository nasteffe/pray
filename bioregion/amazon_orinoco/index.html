The Amazon breathes. Every day, the canopy of the world's largest rainforest exhales 20 billion tonnes of water into the atmosphere — more than the Amazon River itself pours into the Atlantic. These flying rivers travel west until they strike the Andes, fall as rain, and return through a thousand tributaries to begin again. The Yanomami, who have lived in the northern Amazon for at least a thousand years, call the forest urihi a — the living land.

Lives Lived Here

In Boa Vista, Roraima, a Yanomami woman walks four hours to reach the nearest health post with her two-year-old son, who has had diarrhoea for six days. The garimpeiros upstream use mercury to separate gold from sediment; the mercury enters the river, enters the fish, enters the child. The gold travels through buying houses in Boa Vista to formal refineries in São Paulo, where it is laundered into the legitimate market and shipped to jewellers and electronics manufacturers in Europe and North America. Her son's illness and the gold in a smartphone share a supply chain. She does not know this. She knows her son is sick and the river tastes wrong.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

RAISG (Red Amazónica de Información Socioambiental Georreferenciada) investigation

Network of civil society organizations from all nine Amazonian countries producing georeferenced maps of deforestation, mining, oil concessions, and Indigenous territories. Publishes the definitive atlas of pressures on the Amazon basin.

Maps the exact gold mining frontiers and deforestation arcs the briefing traces — illegal garimpo sites in Yanomami territory, soy expansion corridors, and oil blocks overlapping Indigenous reserves. Makes visible the spatial overlap between extractive concessions and community lands.

Instituto Socioambiental (ISA) advocacy

Brazilian organization supporting Indigenous peoples' territorial rights through legal advocacy, policy analysis, and satellite monitoring of deforestation and illegal mining. Maintains the most comprehensive database of Indigenous lands in Brazil.

Works directly at the intersection of gold extraction and Indigenous land rights — monitoring mercury contamination in Yanomami rivers, tracking illegal airstrips supplying garimpo operations, and supporting community-led territorial surveillance against timber and mining invasions.

Amazon Watch solidarity

International organization partnering with Indigenous and environmental organizations in the Amazon Basin to protect rainforest and advance Indigenous rights. Campaigns against oil drilling, mining, and dam construction.

Amplifies the cost-bearers the briefing identifies — communities displaced by oil extraction in Ecuador's Yasuní, gold mining in Peru's Madre de Dios, and hydroelectric dams on the Xingu. Channels international pressure onto the commodity supply chains driving deforestation.

Rainforest Foundation US solidarity

Supports Indigenous and traditional forest communities in securing their land rights through community mapping, legal support, and advocacy. Works in Brazil, Peru, Guyana, and Panama.

Funds community-based mapping that counters the land grabs the briefing documents — helping communities produce counter-maps that make visible the forest areas under pressure from cattle ranching, soy monoculture, and illegal timber extraction along the deforestation arc.

COICA (Coordinadora de las Organizaciones Indígenas de la Cuenca Amazónica) advocacy

Umbrella organization representing Indigenous peoples across all nine Amazon basin countries. Advocates for territorial rights, free prior and informed consent, and Indigenous-led forest governance at international forums.

Represents the communities whose livelihoods — fisheries, agroforestry, subsistence farming — are disrupted by the extractive flows the briefing traces. Their territorial governance proposals offer alternatives to the deforestation-driven commodity model.
