Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the Solimões River, a Ribeirinho fisherman poles his canoe into the flooded forest. It is February — high water — and the tambaqui have moved into the igapó to feed on fallen fruit. He has fished this way since his father taught him at seven years old, reading the water level against marks his grandfather carved into a Brazil nut tree. His wife will smoke the catch on a rack behind their stilt house and carry it by boat to the Manaus market, where it sells for R$15 a kilo. The dried fish feeds families across Amazonas state. The river that feeds the fish is fed by rain that the forest makes.

Twelve hundred kilometres south, in Mato Grosso, a soy farmer watches a GPS-guided combine harvest 3,000 hectares of soybeans. The land was cerrado savanna fifteen years ago — cleared, burned, planted. His crop will travel by truck to the port of Santos and by bulk carrier to Dalian, China, where it becomes feed for pigs that become pork that reaches Shanghai supermarkets. He has never met the Ribeirinho fisherman, but they share a water cycle: the moisture that his cleared land no longer transpires is moisture that no longer falls as rain over the fisherman's river. Neither man chose this connection. Both live inside it.

In Boa Vista, Roraima, a Yanomami woman walks four hours to reach the nearest health post with her two-year-old son, who has had diarrhoea for six days. The garimpeiros upstream use mercury to separate gold from sediment; the mercury enters the river, enters the fish, enters the child. The gold travels through buying houses in Boa Vista to formal refineries in São Paulo, where it is laundered into the legitimate market and shipped to jewellers and electronics manufacturers in Europe and North America. Her son's illness and the gold in a smartphone share a supply chain. She does not know this. She knows her son is sick and the river tastes wrong.

In Cúcuta, on the Colombian-Venezuelan border, a Venezuelan nurse who once earned $300 a month now earns nothing — PDVSA's collapse took the economy with it. She crosses the bridge to Colombia each morning to sell arepas and phone credit, carrying medicine back for her mother in the evening. She is one of 7.7 million Venezuelans who have left. Western Union takes 7% of whatever her cousin in Lima sends home. She does not think of herself as displaced. She thinks of herself as surviving.

What Presses

The weight on Amazon-Orinoco Lowlands

Yanomami Territory, Roraima, Brazil — In the northernmost Amazon, where the Serra da Neblina rises to nearly 3,000 metres, 30,000 Yanomami live in a territory the size of Portugal. Since 2018, an estimated 20,000 garimpeiros have invaded — arriving by river, by clandestine airstrip, by foot. They dredge riverbeds with hydraulic pumps, use mercury to amalgamate gold, and leave behind cratered moonscapes where forest stood. The mercury enters the fish, the fish enters the children. In 2023, the Brazilian government declared a health emergency after 570 Yanomami children under five died over the preceding four years (2019–2022) from malnutrition, malaria, and waterborne disease. The army was sent; some miners left; most returned within months. Yanomami leader Davi Kopenawa has called the garimpo a "machine of death." The gold leaves through Boa Vista buying houses, is refined in São Paulo, and enters the global market unlabelled.

Putumayo and Nariño, Colombia — In southern Colombia, where the Andes descend into the Amazon basin and the Pacific slope, FARC dissidents and the ELN control territory by controlling coca. Campesino farmers grow coca not because they choose it but because nothing else pays — $1.50 a kilogram of coca leaf against debts that compound monthly, under armed men who tax the harvest and kill those who try to switch crops. Colombia's 7.1 million displaced people are concentrated in host communities whose water and sanitation infrastructure was built for a fraction of the current population. In 2025, every single hour, the life of a student or teacher was placed in jeopardy. The UNFPA reports that the crisis is "protracted, complex, and rapidly intensifying" — driven by armed conflict, social violence, floods, and forced displacement simultaneously. The funding gap is 83%.

Madre de Dios, Peru — In Peru's southeastern Amazon, illegal gold mining has deforested over 100,000 hectares — visible from space as a scar of bare earth and toxic ponds spreading along river systems. Organized crime networks from Peru and Brazil control the mining zones. Indigenous Huitoto and Matsiguenka communities downstream watch their fish stocks collapse as mercury concentrations in river water exceed WHO safe limits by 5-10 times. The gold is worth $10 billion annually at global prices. It passes through formal refineries that do not ask where it came from. The mercury stays in the water table. It will be there for decades after the last miner leaves.

The Amazon is not just a forest — it is a water pump. The canopy transpires 20 billion tonnes of water daily into atmospheric rivers that travel west, strike the Andes, and return as rainfall across the continent. At 17% deforestation, the system is approaching the 20-25% threshold beyond which the self-watering cycle collapses irreversibly. The soy and cattle that replaced the trees are themselves dependent on the rainfall the trees once generated. JBS, Marfrig, Bunge, and Cargill are profiting from a supply chain that is destroying its own water supply — and São Paulo, 2,000 kilometres away, will pay the price in drought.
Gold mining in the Amazon operates as a perfect closed loop of harm: garimpeiros invade Indigenous territory, poison rivers with mercury to extract gold, the gold is laundered through formal refineries into the global market, and the mercury remains in the water table for generations. In Yanomami territory, 570 children under five died over the four years 2019–2022 from the consequences — malnutrition, malaria, contaminated water — while the gold entered jewellery stores and electronics supply chains without any label of its origin.
Venezuela's 7.7 million displaced people and Colombia's 7.1 million are not separate crises. They are the same crisis with different entry points: oil collapse in one country, armed-group territorial control in the other, and in both cases the departure of people from their homes serves the interests of those who want the land — for coca, for cattle, for mining, for oil. Displacement is not a side effect of the economy. It is how the economy works.

Cascade Chains

Deforestation for soy/cattle → Loss of transpiration → Weakening of flying rivers → Reduced rainfall over southern Brazil and cerrado → Agricultural stress in regions that depend on Amazon-origin moisture (BRA — 17% of forest cleared, approaching 20-25% tipping point; São Paulo receives 70%+ of rainfall from Amazon moisture transport)

Illegal gold mining invasion → Mercury contamination of rivers → Fish stock collapse → Indigenous malnutrition and child mortality → Health emergency without sustained response (BRA — Yanomami territory, 570 children under five dead 2019–2022; 20,000 garimpeiros; mercury in river systems for decades)

Venezuelan economic collapse (PDVSA production down 77%) → 7.7M displaced across South America → Host community strain on water, sanitation, employment → Xenophobia and informal exploitation → Survival trading economy at borders (VEN/COL/PER/ECU/BRA — largest displacement crisis in Latin American history; bachaqueros crossing Cúcuta bridge daily)

Who Sustains This

Bolivarian National Armed Forces · PDVSA oil revenue (declining); gold mining (illegal); drug trafficking (alleged, sanctioned individuals)

Driven by: We must defend the revolution against imperialism and the oligarchy

The trap: Defending the revolution of the poor through policies that impoverish them. The Bolivarian project aimed to redistribute oil wealth to the marginalized, but PDVSA's collapse, hyperinflation, and military corruption have driven 7.7 million Venezuelans into exile. The revolution that promised dignity produced the largest displacement crisis in Latin American history.

Brazilian Police and Paramilitary Militias · State police budgets; extortion (milícias); illegal real estate; clandestine services (gas, internet, transport)

Driven by: We must bring order to spaces the state has abandoned

The trap: Imposing order through extralegal violence that is itself criminal disorder. Brazilian police kill more than any force globally (~6,000/year) while milícias — founded by off-duty police — run extortion rackets identical to the drug factions they claim to oppose. The line between law enforcement and organized crime disappears while the ideology of 'order' persists.

Peruvian Mining-Security Complex · State budget; police-mining company private contracts (convenios)

Driven by: We must protect investment and growth from anti-mining radicals

The trap: Developing communities through extraction that destroys their livelihoods. Peruvian mining promises development for the poorest regions but contaminates the water and land that sustain existing livelihoods. Communities resist not because they oppose development but because the development on offer requires the destruction of what they already have.

FARC Dissidents (Estado Mayor Central) · Coca taxation in Cauca, Nariño, Putumayo; illegal gold mining; extortion of rural communities; cocaine trafficking

Driven by: We must continue the struggle against the oligarchic state

The trap: Claiming revolutionary liberation while operating as a narco-trafficking enterprise. The FARC dissidents invoke Bolivarian liberation ideology to justify territorial control, but their revenue depends entirely on the coca economy — which requires the poverty and state absence they claim to oppose. Campesino families produce under coercion, receiving $0.50-1.50/kg while cocaine retails at $100+/gram. Liberation has become its own form of extraction.

Thirteen resource streams flow out of the Amazon-Orinoco lowlands — soy, beef, iron ore, timber, crude oil, coal, cocaine, coffee, shrimp, bananas, copper, gold, lithium — worth tens of billions at destination markets. What does not come back is the forest, which once cleared does not regrow on any human timescale; the water, which the forest generated and which no longer falls as rain over the rivers that fed the fisheries; and the people, 7.7 million Venezuelans scattered across a continent and 7.1 million Colombians displaced within their own country, whose absence creates the vacancy extraction requires. 7.1M displaced (total); soy, beef, animal products, iron ore, illegal timber (hardwoods) leaving.

The forest breathes out and the rain falls. The rain falls and the river rises. The river rises and the fish feed. The fish feed and the children eat. Remove one step and the whole sequence fails — but the failure takes years to arrive, and by the time it is visible, the forest is already gone. The Yanomami knew this. We are only now learning what they meant.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The beef and soy in European and North American supermarkets may come from cleared Amazon forest. JBS — the world's largest meat company — has been repeatedly documented purchasing cattle from illegally deforested land in Pará and Mato Grosso. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), delayed to December 2025, would require importers to prove their soy and beef are deforestation-free. Ask your supermarket: where does your beef come from, and can you trace it to the farm? The traceability technology exists. The question is whether the market will use it before the tipping point is crossed.
The gold in your electronics or jewellery may come from Yanomami territory or Madre de Dios, laundered through formal refineries that do not track origin. The London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) certifies "responsible" gold, but NGOs including Global Witness have documented how illegally mined Amazon gold enters LBMA-certified supply chains. Demand that your electronics manufacturer and jeweller disclose their gold sourcing. The Dodd-Frank Act Section 1502 requires US-listed companies to report on conflict minerals — gold is one of them.
You are breathing Amazon air. The forest produces roughly 20% of the world's oxygen and cycles water across a continent. Its survival is not a Brazilian domestic issue — it is a planetary system. Indigenous territorial management is the most effective form of forest protection: deforestation rates inside demarcated Indigenous territories are 80% lower than outside them. Supporting Indigenous land rights — through organisations like APIB (Articulação dos Povos Indígenas do Brasil) or the Hutukara Yanomami Association — is not charity. It is the cheapest and most effective climate policy available.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

RAISG (Red Amazónica de Información Socioambiental Georreferenciada) investigation

Network of civil society organizations from all nine Amazonian countries producing georeferenced maps of deforestation, mining, oil concessions, and Indigenous territories. Publishes the definitive atlas of pressures on the Amazon basin.

Maps the exact gold mining frontiers and deforestation arcs the briefing traces — illegal garimpo sites in Yanomami territory, soy expansion corridors, and oil blocks overlapping Indigenous reserves. Makes visible the spatial overlap between extractive concessions and community lands.

Instituto Socioambiental (ISA) advocacy

Brazilian organization supporting Indigenous peoples' territorial rights through legal advocacy, policy analysis, and satellite monitoring of deforestation and illegal mining. Maintains the most comprehensive database of Indigenous lands in Brazil.

Works directly at the intersection of gold extraction and Indigenous land rights — monitoring mercury contamination in Yanomami rivers, tracking illegal airstrips supplying garimpo operations, and supporting community-led territorial surveillance against timber and mining invasions.

Amazon Watch solidarity

International organization partnering with Indigenous and environmental organizations in the Amazon Basin to protect rainforest and advance Indigenous rights. Campaigns against oil drilling, mining, and dam construction.

Amplifies the cost-bearers the briefing identifies — communities displaced by oil extraction in Ecuador's Yasuní, gold mining in Peru's Madre de Dios, and hydroelectric dams on the Xingu. Channels international pressure onto the commodity supply chains driving deforestation.

Rainforest Foundation US solidarity

Supports Indigenous and traditional forest communities in securing their land rights through community mapping, legal support, and advocacy. Works in Brazil, Peru, Guyana, and Panama.

Funds community-based mapping that counters the land grabs the briefing documents — helping communities produce counter-maps that make visible the forest areas under pressure from cattle ranching, soy monoculture, and illegal timber extraction along the deforestation arc.

COICA (Coordinadora de las Organizaciones Indígenas de la Cuenca Amazónica) advocacy

Umbrella organization representing Indigenous peoples across all nine Amazon basin countries. Advocates for territorial rights, free prior and informed consent, and Indigenous-led forest governance at international forums.

Represents the communities whose livelihoods — fisheries, agroforestry, subsistence farming — are disrupted by the extractive flows the briefing traces. Their territorial governance proposals offer alternatives to the deforestation-driven commodity model.
