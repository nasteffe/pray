Lives Lived Here

In Duisburg, where the Rhine meets the Ruhr, a barge pilot checks the water level at the Kaub gauge on his phone before loading. The number determines everything: how deep his hull can sit, how many tonnes of coal he can carry, whether the BASF plant in Ludwigshafen gets its feedstock this week. In 2022, when the gauge read 30 centimetres, he carried a third of his usual load and the chemical plant ran emergency protocols for the first time since the war. His grandfather ran the same route carrying the same cargo. His grandfather never checked the water level.

In Aalsmeer, south of Amsterdam, 20 million flowers cross the auction floor before breakfast on a Tuesday morning. Roses from Kenya, chrysanthemums from Ethiopia, tulips from the reclaimed polders outside. A buyer for a German supermarket chain bids by the trolley. The Kenyan roses flew overnight from Nairobi — grown on the shore of Lake Naivasha by women earning three euros a day, sprayed with pesticides banned in the EU, packed in a cold chain powered by diesel generators. The buyer does not know this. He knows the price per stem and the margin per bunch.

In Zug, a town of 30,000 people in central Switzerland, commodity traders turn over more revenue than most countries produce. Glencore, Trafigura, Vitol — their Zug offices set the price of copper from Katanga, oil from the Niger Delta, grain from Ukraine. A trader watches copper futures on three screens. The copper will travel from a DRC mine where children sort ore by hand, through a Chinese smelter, into a German wire harness, into a car assembled in Wolfsburg and sold in Munich. The trader has not been to Katanga. He has been to the LME.

In a reception centre outside Vienna, a Syrian family waits for their asylum interview. They crossed from Hungary on a route that traces the Danube — the same corridor that carries grain barges and rail freight. Austria processed approximately 108,000 asylum applications in 2022, more per capita than Germany. The family does not know that the arms in the war they fled were partly manufactured in this country — Glock pistols, Steyr rifles, Austrian-made components in systems sold to coalition partners who used them in the conflict. They know only the waiting.

What Presses

The weight on Rhine-Danube Karst

Groningen, Netherlands — In the villages of northern Groningen, the cracks run through bedroom walls, kitchen ceilings, load-bearing foundations. Since NAM — the Shell-ExxonMobil subsidiary — began extracting gas from Europe's largest onshore field in 1963, over a thousand induced earthquakes have shaken this province, the largest reaching magnitude 3.6 in 2012 at Huizinge. Property values collapsed 20 to 30 percent in the earthquake zone. Insurance claims exceeded a billion euros. Children grew up in houses that moved. The government chose gas revenue — the field earned hundreds of billions over six decades — over the structural safety of its own citizens. Groningen residents organised, sued, and eventually won a halt to extraction by 2024. But the houses are still cracked. The compensation process is still grinding. The lesson is this: even in one of the world's wealthiest democracies, extraction communities are sacrificed when the resource is valuable enough.

Antwerp, Belgium — Eighty percent of the world's rough diamonds pass through Antwerp's diamond district, a few blocks near the central station where billions of dollars change hands each week. The stones arrive from Botswana, from Russia, from the DRC, from the Central African Republic. The Kimberley Process is meant to certify them as conflict-free. It does not. Investigative reporting has documented stones from artisanal mines in Kasai province — where communities were displaced for concessions, where miners work in conditions that meet international definitions of forced labour, where mercury is handled without protection — arriving in Antwerp with clean paperwork. The diamond on your hand may be clean. It may not. The system is designed so that you will never know.

Zug, Switzerland — A town smaller than most London boroughs hosts Glencore, Trafigura, Vitol, and dozens of smaller commodity trading houses whose combined annual revenues exceed the GDP of most African countries. These firms set the global price of copper, cobalt, oil, grain, and coal. They do not mine, farm, or drill — they buy from those who do, sell to those who need, and keep the margin. The margin is calculated in Zug. The cost is borne in Katanga, in the Niger Delta, in the wheat fields of Ukraine. Swiss banking secrecy and Zug's tax arrangements provide the financial infrastructure that makes this arbitrage possible. The trading floor is quiet. The mine is not.

Swiss commodity trading houses — Glencore, Trafigura, Vitol, Cargill — operate from Zug and Geneva with annual revenues exceeding those of most countries, setting global prices for minerals, oil, and grain while the communities that produce these commodities have no seat at the table. The financial architecture of Swiss banking secrecy and Zug's preferential tax regime enable an opacity that makes accountability nearly impossible: the gold refined at Valcambi arrives certified and anonymous, the copper priced on the LME carries no memory of the child who sorted it.
The green transition reproduces colonial extraction patterns in clean-energy packaging. German automakers — Volkswagen, BMW, Mercedes — source cobalt from DRC and lithium from the Atacama for batteries marketed as climate solutions. The water cost at the mine, the child labour in the sorting, the community displacement for the concession — these are not acknowledged in the vehicle's carbon footprint, which begins at the factory gate, not at the pit face. The EV in your driveway is not clean. It is clean here.
The Royal FloraHolland cooperative (centred on Aalsmeer, Amsterdam) handles approximately 60% of the global cut flower trade — making the Netherlands the chokepoint through which the beauty industry passes. Kenyan flower workers earn three euros a day, handle pesticides banned in the EU, and have no meaningful access to union representation. Lake Naivasha, whose water feeds the farms, has dropped two metres and its fisheries have collapsed. The flowers arrive fresh at European supermarkets every morning without a trace of what they cost.

What flows out is manufactured goods, financial instruments, and arms. The exchange is not trade. The Rhine carries everything — chemicals, coal, grain, cars, sewage — and asks for nothing except enough water to keep moving. When the water drops below the navigation line at Kaub, the factories stop, the coal stays in the hold, and for a few weeks this corridor remembers what it means to depend on something you cannot manufacture. Seeds of Hope

In Groningen province, citizens whose homes were cracked by gas-extraction earthquakes — over a thousand induced seismic events since 1995 — organised, litigated, and won. The Dutch government agreed to halt Groningen gas extraction by 2024, a rare case where an extraction community in the Global North used democratic institutions to end the damage. The victory cost decades of structural damage, billions in unresolved claims, and a generation of children who grew up sleeping in cracked bedrooms.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Glencore, headquartered in Baar near Zug, is listed on the London Stock Exchange. It operates mines in the DRC (cobalt), Peru (copper), Colombia (coal), and dozens of other countries. Its annual reports, filed with the LSE, describe its operations. The Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org) indexes community complaints, litigation, and shareholder resolutions by company name. The company's operations at Cerrejon (Colombia) displaced 2,000 Wayuu families; at Las Bambas (Peru), 69 people were killed in community protests. These are matters of public record. You can read them. You can ask your pension fund whether it holds the stock.
The EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive requires companies operating in this corridor to disclose supply chain impacts beginning in 2026. This is new. It means that for the first time, the Belgian chocolate manufacturer, the German automaker, the Dutch flower importer will be legally required to name their sources and their costs. Whether the disclosures are honest, whether enforcement follows, whether the directive survives industry lobbying — these are open questions. But the legal framework now exists. Civic organisations including SOMO (Netherlands), Germanwatch (Germany), and Public Eye (Switzerland) monitor corporate compliance and publish accessible reports.
The Aalsmeer flower auction is the single largest chokepoint in the global cut flower trade — ~60% of the world's flowers pass through Dutch Royal FloraHolland auctions. The Fairtrade Foundation certifies some flowers for labour and pesticide standards; certified stems carry a price premium. You can ask your florist whether their roses are Fairtrade-certified. Most are not. The difference is three euros a day for a Kenyan worker, and whether she handles pesticides banned in the country where you will put them on your kitchen table.

European Environmental Bureau (EEB) advocacy

Europe's largest network of environmental citizen organizations, advocating for stronger EU regulation on industrial emissions, agricultural pollution, and water framework directive enforcement across the Rhine-Danube basin.

Campaigns on the industrial and agricultural flows the briefing documents — CAP subsidy-driven intensive dairy overloading nitrogen on Dutch and German soils, PFAS contamination from chemical plants along the Rhine, and Alpine hydropower conflicts between energy generation and river ecology.

Via Campesina Europe solidarity

European coordination of La Via Campesina representing small-scale farmers, defending peasant agriculture against consolidation by industrial dairy and agribusiness. Advocates for food sovereignty, seed rights, and fair milk pricing.

Represents the small-scale farmers displaced by the intensive dairy system the briefing describes — where EU subsidy structures favor industrial operations that overproduce milk while bankrupting family farms, connecting CAP policy to rural depopulation and nitrogen pollution across the heartland.

Danube Environmental Forum (DEF) investigation

Network of NGOs across the Danube basin monitoring dam impacts, wetland loss, and industrial contamination from Romania to Germany. Documents how navigation infrastructure and hydropower alter sediment transport and floodplain ecology.

Tracks the Danube waterway infrastructure the briefing documents — where locks, canals, and dredging for barge navigation destroy wetland habitat that buffers floods, and where upstream Alpine hydropower alters sediment flows that downstream agriculture depends on.

Alpine Convention — Water Management Working Group advocacy

International treaty framework protecting Alpine ecosystems across eight countries. Working group on water management addresses glacier retreat, hydropower conflicts, and drinking water supply for downstream urban populations.

Addresses the Alpine water crisis at the source of the briefing's flows — where glacial retreat reduces summer Rhine and Danube base flows that power industry, cool nuclear plants, and irrigate agriculture, connecting high-altitude ice loss to lowland economic disruption.

WECF (Women Engage for a Common Future) solidarity

Network of feminist organizations working on environmental health and sustainable development across Europe. Documents how chemical pollution from Rhine-corridor industry affects reproductive health in downstream communities, and supports community-led water monitoring.

Centers the health cost-bearers the briefing's industrial flows produce — communities downstream of chemical plants exposed to PFAS and endocrine disruptors in drinking water, and farm workers in intensive dairy regions exposed to pesticide drift, naming who physically absorbs industrial externalities.
