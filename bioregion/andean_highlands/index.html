Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Apurímac valley, a Quechua farmer opens the acequia that her community has maintained for six centuries to water potato terraces at 3,800 metres. Her potatoes will travel by truck to the Cusco market, where a miner's wife from the Las Bambas camp buys them alongside rice trucked from the coast — rice irrigated by the same glacial rivers that the mine is drawing down. The acequia runs for fewer weeks each year. The miner's wife notices but cannot name why.

In Miraflores, on the Pacific coast of Lima, a civil servant fills a glass at the kitchen tap and notices the pressure is lower than usual. It is July — the dry season, when the Rímac runs on glacier melt alone. She knows this abstractly: the SEDAPAL alerts on her phone, the news about monitoring stations recording historic lows, the 18% rise in her water bill last year. She also knows, less consciously, that three hours inland Las Bambas mine draws from the same highland water system — and that the mine's allocation is settled before the city's. She refills the glass and opens her laptop. The connection between her tap and the Apurímac aquifer is real, but it has no name in her daily life.

In the Chapare lowlands east of Cochabamba, a Bolivian coca farmer strips leaves that produce four harvests a year, selling to the legal market for coca tea and to the illegal one for cocaine precursor — he cannot tell his buyers apart, and it does not matter, because both pay more than the quinoa his father grew before the soil was exhausted. His federation negotiated the partial legalisation of coca cultivation and won; his son studies agronomy in Cochabamba on a scholarship the federation secured.

In Nariño, on Colombia's Pacific slope, a campesino family tends coffee seedlings while FARC dissidents patrol the road below, taxing every bag that leaves the valley — the family earns $5 a day picking coffee in a good season, less than the armed men earn in an hour. Their eldest daughter left for Bogotá last year. She works in a call centre and sends money home at the end of every month, which arrives via Western Union minus 7%.

What Presses

The weight on Andean Highlands and Pacific Slope

Apurímac, Peru — Five hours east of Lima at 3,800 metres, where Quechua farming communities have worked potato terraces for a thousand years, the river is lower this year. The acequia runs for fewer weeks, the potatoes go in later, the frost catches the ones planted too late. The glacier that fed the Rímac is not coming back, and what water remains arrives as a fight: the mine gets its allocation first, then the city, then the farm. In Apurímac province, communities have watched the water table drop as Las Bambas expanded — the same aquifer serves the copper flotation plant and the potato terraces, and the plant never runs dry.

La Guajira, Colombia — La Guajira is the northernmost tip of South America, a desert peninsula so dry that the Wayúu people built their entire culture around the scarcity of water. The dust from Cerrejón settles on everything here. Wayúu children in the resettlement camps downwind of the coal pit have respiratory illness rates three times the national average. The Ranchería River — the only permanent water source in the region — was diverted to wash coal. Over 2,000 families were moved to make room for the pit; the coal trains run to Puerto Bolívar day and night, 35 million tonnes a year for European power plants, and the families were promised houses and water that arrived late, or broken, or not at all.

Pacific Slope & Nariño — Where the Andes drop toward the Ecuadorean border and the Pacific slope begins, Nariño has been coca country, coffee country, and for the last decade, cartel country. On the coast below, where 80% of mangrove forest has been cleared for shrimp ponds, the fishermen who once lived from the estuary now work in the ponds — wading chest-deep in antibiotic-dosed water, earning less than they made from wild catch, with no storm protection for the village behind them. Inland, campesino families grow coca because nothing else pays — $1.50 a kilogram against debts that compound monthly, under armed men who tax the harvest and shoot those who try to switch to coffee. The violence is not incidental to the economy; it is the economy.

Copper and lithium extraction in Peru and Bolivia consume the same glacial meltwater that terrace farmers and Lima's 10 million residents depend on — and the glaciers are not being replaced. The green transition's demand for battery minerals reproduces colonial metabolic patterns in clean-energy form: the water cost and the displacement cost are borne at 4,000 metres by Quechua and Aymara communities while the benefit accrues in car showrooms that will never see the dry riverbeds.
Glencore operates both the Cerrejón coal mine — which displaced 2,000 Wayúu families and diverted their only river for European power plants — and the Las Bambas copper mine, whose tailings contaminate Quechua irrigation water for Chinese smelters. A single Swiss corporation holds the thread connecting Indigenous dispossession in two countries to the energy and electronics supply chains of two continents.
Colombia's 7.1 million displaced people are not a byproduct of conflict — they are its product. Armed groups need depopulated territory for coca cultivation and transshipment. Each family that flees frees land for the next planting. The displacement is not collateral damage; it is the business model.

Nine resource streams flow out of the Andes annually — copper, lithium, coal, cocaine, shrimp, bananas, coffee, gold, crude oil — worth tens of billions at their destination markets. What does not come back is the water, consumed by mines and absent from retreating glaciers; and the people, 7.1 million of them displaced in Colombia alone, whose departure makes space for the next extraction. 755 killed (3 months); 7.1M displaced (total); copper, gold (artisanal/illegal), lithium carbonate/hydroxide leaving; precipitation 365% of normal; 247 fires (7 days).

What to bring with you from this briefing

Glencore operates both the Cerrejón coal mine (La Guajira, Colombia — 2,000 Wayúu families displaced, Ranchería River diverted) and the Las Bambas copper mine (Apurímac, Peru — 69 killed in community protests 2022–23). Glencore is listed on the London and Johannesburg stock exchanges. Shareholders submitted Indigenous rights resolutions at the 2023 and 2024 AGMs. These filings are public record at the Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org), searchable by company name.
The lithium in your phone or EV battery may come from Bolivia's Salar de Uyuni, where Aymara pastoral communities lose groundwater to operations that consume 65% of the region's freshwater. Joint venture partners include Ganzizhou Liyuan (China) and CITIC. Ask your device or car manufacturer: where is your lithium from, and what community accountability framework governs its extraction? Supply chain disclosure is now a legal requirement in the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.
Peru's Quechua Potato Park (Parque de la Papa, Pisac, Cusco) preserves 3,000 Andean potato varieties in living practice — women farmers selecting, saving, and exchanging seed each harvest against the seed law reforms that would require them to buy patented varieties instead. GRAIN's February 2026 report documents the legislative mechanism. The park receives visitors and accepts solidarity from abroad. Their work is what food sovereignty looks like when it is not a slogan.
