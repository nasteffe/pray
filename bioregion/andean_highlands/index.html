For ten thousand years, Quechua and Aymara farmers have opened the acequia — the stone-lined canal — before dawn to water potato terraces that feed entire civilisations. The varieties they grow, over 3,000 of them, exist nowhere in the world except in those hands and in those hills: purple-fleshed, frost-resistant, drought-adapted, each one a conversation between a seed and a mountain perfected over generations of women who understood the soil at 3,800 metres.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Apurímac valley, a Quechua farmer opens the acequia that her community has maintained for six centuries to water potato terraces at 3,800 metres. Her potatoes will travel by truck to the Cusco market, where a miner's wife from the Las Bambas camp buys them alongside rice trucked from the coast — rice irrigated by the same glacial rivers that the mine is drawing down. The acequia runs for fewer weeks each year. The miner's wife notices but cannot name why.

Peruvian Mining-Security Complex

Driven by: We must protect investment and growth from anti-mining radicals

The trap: Developing communities through extraction that destroys their livelihoods. Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Business & Human Rights Resource Centre — Mining in Latin America investigation

Tracks corporate accountability allegations against mining companies operating in the Andes, including Glencore's Las Bambas and Cerrejon operations. Documents community complaints, company responses, and legal proceedings.

Directly monitors the copper and coal extractive flows described in the briefing — Las Bambas (400,000 tonnes/year copper to China) and Cerrejon (coal from La Guajira). Names cost-bearers and tracks whether companies respond to allegations.

Parque de la Papa (Potato Park) solidarity

Quechua community-governed biocultural heritage area in Pisac, Cusco, preserving 3,000+ native potato varieties through traditional agricultural knowledge. A living counter-model to extractive monoculture.

Directly counters the soil network crisis described in the briefing — while mining operations contaminate highland aquifers, Parque de la Papa demonstrates Indigenous food sovereignty through terraced agriculture that has sustained Andean communities for millennia.

CooperAccion advocacy

Peruvian civil society organization monitoring extractive industries, providing legal support to mining-affected communities, and advocating for environmental regulation. Documents social conflicts around Las Bambas, Tia Maria, and other contested mines.

Works at the nodes where copper extraction meets community resistance — the Apurimac-Cusco mining corridor and the community blockades the briefing documents. Tracks the 69 killed in Las Bambas protests and ongoing state-of-emergency declarations.

OCMAL (Observatorio de Conflictos Mineros de America Latina) investigation

Latin American mining conflict observatory mapping active disputes between extractive companies and communities across the Andes. Maintains a database of mining conflicts with community testimony.

Maps the same extractive corridors the briefing traces — copper, gold, and lithium flows from highland mines to global markets — but from the perspective of affected communities rather than commodity markets.

CEDHA (Centro de Derechos Humanos y Ambiente) advocacy

Argentine human rights and environment organization working on lithium extraction accountability in the Lithium Triangle (Salar de Atacama, Salar de Uyuni, Hombre Muerto). Documents water depletion from brine extraction.

Addresses the lithium triangle extraction the briefing identifies — where brine pumping for EV batteries depletes aquifers that sustain Indigenous pastoral communities on the Altiplano, connecting green-transition demand in Europe and China to water loss in the Andes.
