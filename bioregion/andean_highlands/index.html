Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Apurímac valley, a Quechua farmer opens the acequia that her community has maintained for six centuries to water potato terraces at 3,800 metres. Her potatoes will travel by truck to the Cusco market, where a miner's wife from the Las Bambas camp buys them alongside rice trucked from the coast — rice irrigated by the same glacial rivers that the mine is drawing down. The acequia runs for fewer weeks each year. The miner's wife notices but cannot name why.

For Prayer

Last updated on 2026-07-20.

For the farmers of the high country…

For the Quechua and Aymara families working terraces their ancestors cut a thousand years ago, as the glaciers that feed their canals retreat and the rains arrive wrong. For the potato and the alpaca, and for the young deciding whether to stay.

For those beside the mines…

For the villages ringed by the great copper and lithium works — Las Bambas, the salt flats of Uyuni — whose water the mines drink and whose protests are answered with states of emergency. That the metals the world calls clean are not counted clean at their source.

For the coca-caught…

For the farming families of the eastern slopes held in the coca economy, growing the leaf because nothing else pays, living between the violence of the trade and the violence of its eradication. For a way out that is not simply another poverty.

For the miners…

For the miners of the Andes, from the silver mountain of Potosí that has swallowed lives for five centuries to the artisanal gold pits, breathing dust for wages that never match the wealth they bring up.

For the cities that receive them…

For those who leave the highlands for the edges of Lima and La Paz and Bogotá, and for the ones who go further still — that they carry their mountains' dignity with them and are met with welcome.

For those who govern by the old law…

For the rondas, the water committees, the Indigenous authorities defending land and water by custom and by court. That their patient governance outlasts the boom.

Directly counters the soil network crisis described in the briefing — while mining operations contaminate highland aquifers, Parque de la Papa demonstrates Indigenous food sovereignty through terraced agriculture that has sustained Andean communities for millennia.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Glencore operates both the Cerrejón coal mine (La Guajira, Colombia — 2,000 Wayúu families displaced, Ranchería River diverted) and the Las Bambas copper mine (Apurímac, Peru — 69 killed in community protests 2022–23). Glencore is listed on the London and Johannesburg stock exchanges. Shareholders submitted Indigenous rights resolutions at the 2023 and 2024 AGMs. These filings are public record at the Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org), searchable by company name.
The lithium in your phone or EV battery may come from Bolivia's Salar de Uyuni, where Aymara pastoral communities lose groundwater to operations that consume 65% of the region's freshwater. Joint venture partners include Ganzizhou Liyuan (China) and CITIC. Ask your device or car manufacturer: where is your lithium from, and what community accountability framework governs its extraction? Supply chain disclosure is now a legal requirement in the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.
Peru's Quechua Potato Park (Parque de la Papa, Pisac, Cusco) preserves 3,000 Andean potato varieties in living practice — women farmers selecting, saving, and exchanging seed each harvest against the seed law reforms that would require them to buy patented varieties instead. GRAIN's February 2026 report documents the legislative mechanism. The park receives visitors and accepts solidarity from abroad. Their work is what food sovereignty looks like when it is not a slogan.
