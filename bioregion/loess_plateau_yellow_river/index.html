For the gifts of this place…

For the gifts of this old and industrious land — for the Yellow River cradle of a civilization, for a technological genius that clothes and connects the world, for the long peace and plenty of its eastern rim. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what our comfort costs…

For the world's workshop that this region has become — the steel, the ships, the chips, the endless goods — and for the aquifer beneath the North China Plain pumped dry to sustain it, the rivers run low, the air thick over the factory towns. Teach us to trace the device in our hand back to the water and the workers spent to make it.

For those who bear the cost here…

For the Mongolian herders displaced by the rare-earth pits at Bayan Obo, for the workers on the long factory lines, for the farmers watching their wells go deep and then dry. That the makers of the world's things are not used up in the making.

For quietness, and watchfulness…

For gratitude that does not forget its makers; and, over this crowded and consequential region, for a peace held rather than broken — that the tensions of these seas and straits do not become another of the world's wounds.

What to bring with you from this briefing

TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) produces 90% of the world's most advanced chips. Its water consumption has forced direct reallocation from agriculture. TSMC publishes an annual Corporate Social Responsibility report that includes water usage data. Your phone, laptop, car, and medical devices almost certainly contain TSMC chips. Ask your device manufacturer about semiconductor supply chain water accountability. The EU Chips Act and US CHIPS Act both include supply chain provisions — these are public legislative documents, searchable by name.
The rare earths in your phone or electric vehicle likely passed through Bayan Obo (Inner Mongolia) or Jiangxi Province processing. China Northern Rare Earth Group is the world's largest producer. The environmental and displacement costs at Bayan Obo are documented by the Associated Press, Reuters, and the Chinese Academy of Sciences (search: "Bayan Obo tailings environmental impact"). No current certification scheme for rare earths matches the Kimberley Process for diamonds — the supply chain remains effectively unaccountable for community harm.
The Environmental Justice Foundation has documented forced labour conditions on Taiwanese-flagged distant-water fishing vessels, including debt bondage, passport confiscation, and wage theft affecting Indonesian, Filipino, and Thai migrant fishers. Reports are publicly available at ejfoundation.org. Taiwan's Fisheries Agency has introduced reforms; their effectiveness is contested. If you eat canned tuna or sushi-grade fish, the supply chain may include vessels operating under these conditions.
