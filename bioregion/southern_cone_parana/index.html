The Paraná runs 3,400 kilometres from the cerrado highlands to the Río de la Plata, and for most of that distance it carries grain. Barges loaded with soy and maize move south along the same river that artisanal fishermen in Corrientes and Entre Ríos have worked for generations — pulling dorado and surubí from currents that grow shallower each decade.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the pampas west of Rosario, a tenant farmer checks the moisture on his soybean crop — planted in November, it will be ready for harvest in March if the rain holds. His grandfather raised cattle on this land; his father switched to soy when Cargill built the elevator at the river. The soy pays better per hectare, but the soil is thinner each year and the herbicide bill grows as glyphosate-resistant weeds spread. At the Rosario terminals, grain-elevator workers load barges that will carry the meal downriver to ocean-going vessels bound for Rotterdam and Shanghai. They work twelve-hour shifts during harvest season. The soy will become pig and poultry feed in operations that could not exist at their current scale without it.

In Corrientes province, where the Paraná widens into braided channels, a fisherman sets his net for surubí before first light. His family has fished this stretch for four generations. The river is lower than it should be in March. He has heard about the Amazon — everyone has — but the connection between a burned hectare in Rondônia and the water level outside his door is not something the radio explains. His catch is smaller each year. The young men in his village work on soy farms now.

In Neuquén, a Mapuche woman watches a convoy of water trucks cross her community's land toward the Vaca Muerta wellpads. Each fracking operation consumes millions of litres of water in a region that receives less than 200mm of rain a year. Her community has filed legal challenges; the courts move slowly, the drilling does not. Her grandmother grazed goats on this land. The goats are gone.

On the Puna above Salta, at 3,700 metres, a Kolla herder leads his llamas past a lithium evaporation pond that did not exist five years ago. The pond is turquoise and perfectly still. His well, a kilometre away, has dropped by a third. He does not know the name of the company — Livent, based in Philadelphia — but he knows the water table. The lithium in the pond will become a battery cathode in a factory in Hefei or Ulsan. The herder will not own what his aquifer produced.

What Presses

The weight on Southern Cone and Paraná-Plata

Rosario, Argentina — Along seventy kilometres of Paraná River frontage, grain elevators rise above the water like industrial cathedrals. This is where 90% of Argentina's soy exports are loaded — soybean meal bound for pig farms in Guangdong and poultry operations in the Netherlands. The elevator workers do twelve-hour shifts during the March–April harvest peak. Upstream, tenant farmers on the pampas watch their soil test results decline: nitrogen levels dropping under continuous soy rotation, glyphosate-resistant weeds requiring stronger chemicals each season, the land producing more and giving less. West of the city, in the towns the elevators bypassed, campesino families who once grew mixed crops for local markets have been consolidated out — their plots absorbed into estates operated by firms that report to commodity exchanges in Chicago and São Paulo.

Salta Province, Argentina — At 3,700 metres on the Puna plateau, where the air is thin and the light is blinding, turquoise evaporation ponds spread across the salt flats. Livent Corporation, headquartered in Philadelphia, pumps lithium-rich brine from beneath the salar and leaves it to concentrate under the sun. A kilometre away, a Kolla herder measures the water in his well with a stick notched for each year — this year's notch is lower than any before it. He waters his llamas from a spring that has slowed to a trickle. The provincial government in Salta approved the concession without the free, prior, and informed consent that ILO Convention 169 requires. The lithium will become a battery cathode in a factory in Hefei. The herder does not know this. He knows the water.

Los Lagos, Chile — In the fjords south of Puerto Montt, salmon pens float in rows so dense the water between them turns opaque. Atlantic salmon — a species that does not exist naturally in the southern hemisphere — are raised here in concentrations that require constant antibiotic treatment: Chile uses more antibiotics per tonne of farmed fish than any country on earth. The waste falls to the fjord floor, smothering the benthic ecosystems that Kawésqar communities navigated for six thousand years. The Kawésqar fished these waters from bark canoes; their descendants watch the pens multiply and the wild fish disappear. The salmon reaches a plate in Tokyo or New York within forty-eight hours of harvest. The fjord does not recover on that schedule.

The Amazon-to-breadbasket atmospheric connection is the bioregion's most consequential and least visible vulnerability. More than 70% of the rainfall reaching the cerrado and pampas originates as water vapour transpired by Amazon trees. JBS, Bunge, and Cargill clear Amazon and cerrado forest for soy and cattle — and the same deforestation reduces the rainfall on the soy they have already planted further south. The 2021 Paraná crisis was not an anomaly: it was the system working as the physics predict when you remove the forest that generates the rain.
The lithium triangle (Argentina-Bolivia-Chile) holds more than half the world's known lithium reserves. The green transition's demand for battery minerals is accelerating extraction in one of South America's driest inhabited zones — the Puna plateau, where annual rainfall is under 200mm and every litre of brine pumped lowers the water table that Kolla and Atacameño herders depend on. The electric vehicle in a European driveway and the dry well on the Puna are connected by a supply chain that renders one invisible to the other.
Rosario's seventy-kilometre grain corridor is a chokepoint for global food supply. Ninety per cent of Argentina's soy exports pass through it, and the corridor functions only when the Paraná's water level permits barge navigation. The 2021 low-water event demonstrated that a hydrological disruption originating in the Amazon can halt food exports from a port 3,000 kilometres downstream — a cascade that connects deforestation policy in Brasília to soybean meal prices in Rotterdam.

Six resource streams flow out of the Southern Cone — soy and beef to feed China's and Europe's livestock, lithium for EV batteries, shale gas for global energy markets, iron ore for Chinese steel, farmed salmon for supermarket shelves in North America and Japan. What does not come back is the water, drawn down from Puna aquifers and Patagonian rivers; the soil, losing nitrogen under monoculture that has no fallow; and the atmospheric moisture, diminishing as the Amazon canopy that generates it is cleared by the same firms that need it. 1,415 killed (3 months); soy/soybean meal, lithium carbonate/hydroxide, shale gas (Vaca Muerta) leaving; precipitation 45% of normal; 173 fires (7 days).

The pampas were grassland before they were soybean fields. The cerrado was savanna before it was cattle pasture. The Pantanal was the world's largest wetland before a quarter of it burned. What these places were is still visible — in the gallery forest along the cerrado streams, in the surubí that still swim the Paraná, in the Mapuche grandmother who remembers the goats. The land is patient, but its patience is not infinite.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The soy in processed food, animal feed, and cooking oil is likely from the Southern Cone — Argentina and Brazil together produce more than half the world's supply. Bunge, ADM, Cargill, and JBS control the commodity chains. The Roundtable on Responsible Soy (RTRS) certifies a fraction of total production. Ask where the soy in your food chain originates: supermarkets and food manufacturers can trace it if pressed. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), entering enforcement in 2025, requires supply chain due diligence for soy imported into Europe.
The lithium in your phone, laptop, or electric vehicle battery may come from the Argentine Puna — Salta, Jujuy, or Catamarca provinces — where Livent (USA) and Albemarle (USA) extract brine from salt flats that Kolla and Atacameño communities depend on for water. Ask your device or car manufacturer for supply chain disclosure on lithium sourcing. ILO Convention 169 requires free, prior, and informed consent from indigenous communities before extraction on their territory — a standard that provincial concessions in Argentina have not consistently met.
The farmed salmon sold in supermarkets worldwide is largely raised in Norwegian-origin operations in Chile's Patagonian fjords — Mowi, Cermaq, AquaChile. Chile uses more antibiotics per tonne of farmed fish than any other country. The Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC) certifies some operations. Consumers can ask retailers whether their salmon carries ASC certification and what the antibiotic disclosure policy is for their supply chain.
