Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the pampas west of Rosario, a tenant farmer checks the moisture on his soybean crop — planted in November, it will be ready for harvest in March if the rain holds. His grandfather raised cattle on this land; his father switched to soy when Cargill built the elevator at the river. The soy pays better per hectare, but the soil is thinner each year and the herbicide bill grows as glyphosate-resistant weeds spread. At the Rosario terminals, grain-elevator workers load barges that will carry the meal downriver to ocean-going vessels bound for Rotterdam and Shanghai. They work twelve-hour shifts during harvest season. The soy will become pig and poultry feed in operations that could not exist at their current scale without it.

In Corrientes province, where the Paraná widens into braided channels, a fisherman sets his net for surubí before first light. His family has fished this stretch for four generations. The river is lower than it should be in March. He has heard about the Amazon — everyone has — but the connection between a burned hectare in Rondônia and the water level outside his door is not something the radio explains. His catch is smaller each year. The young men in his village work on soy farms now.

In Neuquén, a Mapuche woman watches a convoy of water trucks cross her community's land toward the Vaca Muerta wellpads. Each fracking operation consumes millions of litres of water in a region that receives less than 200mm of rain a year. Her community has filed legal challenges; the courts move slowly, the drilling does not. Her grandmother grazed goats on this land. The goats are gone.

On the Puna above Salta, at 3,700 metres, a Kolla herder leads his llamas past a lithium evaporation pond that did not exist five years ago. The pond is turquoise and perfectly still. His well, a kilometre away, has dropped by a third. He does not know the name of the company — Arcadium Lithium (formerly Livent, now owned by Rio Tinto) — but he knows the water table. The lithium in the pond will become a battery cathode in a factory in Hefei or Ulsan. The herder will not own what his aquifer produced.

What Presses

The weight on Southern Cone and Paraná-Plata

Rosario, Argentina — Along seventy kilometres of Paraná River frontage, grain elevators rise above the water like industrial cathedrals. This is where 90% of Argentina's soy exports are loaded — soybean meal bound for pig farms in Guangdong and poultry operations in the Netherlands. The elevator workers do twelve-hour shifts during the March–April harvest peak. Upstream, tenant farmers on the pampas watch their soil test results decline: nitrogen levels dropping under continuous soy rotation, glyphosate-resistant weeds requiring stronger chemicals each season, the land producing more and giving less. West of the city, in the towns the elevators bypassed, campesino families who once grew mixed crops for local markets have been consolidated out — their plots absorbed into estates operated by firms that report to commodity exchanges in Chicago and São Paulo.

Salta Province, Argentina — At 3,700 metres on the Puna plateau, where the air is thin and the light is blinding, turquoise evaporation ponds spread across the salt flats. Arcadium Lithium (formerly Livent, now acquired by Rio Tinto in January 2025), pumps lithium-rich brine from beneath the salar and leaves it to concentrate under the sun. A kilometre away, a Kolla herder measures the water in his well with a stick notched for each year — this year's notch is lower than any before it. He waters his llamas from a spring that has slowed to a trickle. The provincial government in Salta approved the concession without the free, prior, and informed consent that ILO Convention 169 requires. The lithium will become a battery cathode in a factory in Hefei. The herder does not know this. He knows the water.

Los Lagos, Chile — In the fjords south of Puerto Montt, salmon pens float in rows so dense the water between them turns opaque. Atlantic salmon — a species that does not exist naturally in the southern hemisphere — are raised here in concentrations that require constant antibiotic treatment: Chile uses more antibiotics per tonne of farmed fish than any country on earth. The waste falls to the fjord floor, smothering the benthic ecosystems that Kawésqar communities navigated for six thousand years. The Kawésqar fished these waters from bark canoes; their descendants watch the pens multiply and the wild fish disappear. The salmon reaches a plate in Tokyo or New York within forty-eight hours of harvest. The fjord does not recover on that schedule.

The Amazon-to-breadbasket atmospheric connection is the bioregion's most consequential and least visible vulnerability. More than 70% of the rainfall reaching the cerrado and pampas originates as water vapour transpired by Amazon trees. JBS, Bunge, and Cargill clear Amazon and cerrado forest for soy and cattle — and the same deforestation reduces the rainfall on the soy they have already planted further south. The 2021 Paraná crisis was not an anomaly: it was the system working as the physics predict when you remove the forest that generates the rain.
The lithium triangle (Argentina-Bolivia-Chile) holds more than half the world's known lithium reserves. The green transition's demand for battery minerals is accelerating extraction in one of South America's driest inhabited zones — the Puna plateau, where annual rainfall is under 200mm and every litre of brine pumped lowers the water table that Kolla and Atacameño herders depend on. The electric vehicle in a European driveway and the dry well on the Puna are connected by a supply chain that renders one invisible to the other.
Rosario's seventy-kilometre grain corridor is a chokepoint for global food supply. Ninety per cent of Argentina's soy exports pass through it, and the corridor functions only when the Paraná's water level permits barge navigation. The 2021 low-water event demonstrated that a hydrological disruption originating in the Amazon can halt food exports from a port 3,000 kilometres downstream — a cascade that connects deforestation policy in Brasília to soybean meal prices in Rotterdam.

Driven by: We must bring order to spaces the state has abandoned

The trap: Imposing order through extralegal violence that is itself criminal disorder. Brazilian police kill more than any force globally (~6,000/year) while milícias — founded by off-duty police — run extortion rackets identical to the drug factions they claim to oppose. The line between law enforcement and organized crime disappears while the ideology of 'order' persists.

Six resource streams flow out of the Southern Cone — soy and beef to feed China's and Europe's livestock, lithium for EV batteries, shale gas for global energy markets, iron ore for Chinese steel, farmed salmon for supermarket shelves in North America and Japan. What does not come back is the water, drawn down from Puna aquifers and Patagonian rivers; the soil, losing nitrogen under monoculture that has no fallow; and the atmospheric moisture, diminishing as the Amazon canopy that generates it is cleared by the same firms that need it. 1,078 killed (3 months); soy/soybean meal, lithium carbonate/hydroxide, shale gas (Vaca Muerta) leaving; precipitation 37% of normal; 155 fires (7 days).

The pampas were grassland before they were soybean fields. The cerrado was savanna before it was cattle pasture. The Pantanal was the world's largest wetland before a quarter of it burned. What these places were is still visible — in the gallery forest along the cerrado streams, in the surubí that still swim the Paraná, in the Mapuche grandmother who remembers the goats. The land is patient, but its patience is not infinite.

Seeds of Hope

The MST — Brazil's Landless Workers Movement — occupies unused estates across the cerrado and pampas to challenge the concentration of land ownership that leaves millions without access to soil while agribusiness exports feed other continents. Their settlements are not protest camps but functioning communities with schools, health clinics, and agroecological farming that demonstrates an alternative to monoculture.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The soy in processed food, animal feed, and cooking oil is likely from the Southern Cone — Argentina and Brazil together produce more than half the world's supply. Bunge, ADM, Cargill, and JBS control the commodity chains. The Roundtable on Responsible Soy (RTRS) certifies a fraction of total production. Ask where the soy in your food chain originates: supermarkets and food manufacturers can trace it if pressed. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), entering enforcement in 2025, requires supply chain due diligence for soy imported into Europe.
The lithium in your phone, laptop, or electric vehicle battery may come from the Argentine Puna — Salta, Jujuy, or Catamarca provinces — where Arcadium Lithium (formerly Livent, acquired by Rio Tinto 2025) and Albemarle (USA) extract brine from salt flats that Kolla and Atacameño communities depend on for water. Ask your device or car manufacturer for supply chain disclosure on lithium sourcing. ILO Convention 169 requires free, prior, and informed consent from indigenous communities before extraction on their territory — a standard that provincial concessions in Argentina have not consistently met.
The farmed salmon sold in supermarkets worldwide is largely raised in Norwegian-origin operations in Chile's Patagonian fjords — Mowi, Cermaq, AquaChile. Chile uses more antibiotics per tonne of farmed fish than any other country. The Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC) certifies some operations. Consumers can ask retailers whether their salmon carries ASC certification and what the antibiotic disclosure policy is for their supply chain.

FARN (Fundación Ambiente y Recursos Naturales) advocacy

Argentine environmental law organization litigating against Vaca Muerta fracking expansion, soy monoculture deforestation, and Paraná waterway dredging. Advocates for community consent and environmental impact enforcement.

Litigates at the extraction points the briefing traces — Vaca Muerta shale gas wells contaminating Mapuche water sources in Neuquén, and Paraná waterway deepening that accelerates soy export throughput while destroying wetland ecosystems that fishing communities depend on.

GRAIN investigation

International research organization documenting land grabbing, corporate concentration in agriculture, and seed privatization. Publishes investigations on soy frontier expansion in the Chaco, cattle ranching deforestation, and their connections to European feed markets.

Investigates the soy and cattle flows the briefing documents — how European livestock feed demand drives deforestation in the Argentine and Paraguayan Chaco, displacing Indigenous Wichí and Guaraní communities, and how commodity traders like Cargill and Bunge concentrate control over the Paraná grain corridor.

Fundación Plurales solidarity

Argentine organization supporting Indigenous and campesino communities in the Gran Chaco, particularly women-led water access and land tenure initiatives. Works with Wichí, Qom, and Guaraní communities facing soy frontier encroachment.

Supports the cost-bearers the briefing identifies at the soy frontier — Indigenous communities in Salta and Formosa whose forests are cleared for soy plantations, and who lose access to water sources contaminated by agrochemical runoff from monoculture expansion.

Observatorio Petrolero Sur (OPSur) investigation

Argentine observatory monitoring the oil and gas industry, with particular focus on Vaca Muerta unconventional extraction. Documents water contamination, seismic activity, and health impacts on Mapuche communities near fracking operations.

Monitors the Vaca Muerta shale flow the briefing traces — where hydraulic fracturing consumes scarce water in Patagonian semi-arid zones, produces toxic flowback that threatens the Neuquén River, and operates on contested Mapuche territory without adequate consultation or consent.

FOBOMADE (Foro Boliviano sobre Medio Ambiente y Desarrollo) advocacy

Bolivian environmental forum monitoring lithium extraction in the Salar de Uyuni, Amazon deforestation, and water conflicts. Advocates for community governance of natural resources and critiques extractivist development models.

Addresses the lithium extraction the briefing documents — where brine pumping from the Salar de Uyuni and Argentine salars for EV batteries depletes water tables that sustain high-altitude pastoralism, connecting green-transition demand in Europe and China to water loss in the Altiplano.

Taller Ecologista solidarity

Rosario-based environmental organization defending Paraná River wetlands against agricultural expansion, real estate development, and intentional burning. Supports island fishing communities and documents illegal fires in the Paraná Delta.

Defends the Paraná waterway ecosystem the briefing traces — where deliberate burning of delta islands for cattle grazing destroys wetlands that filter water for Rosario's 1.3 million residents, and where dredging for grain barge traffic alters the river ecology that sustains artisanal fishing communities.
