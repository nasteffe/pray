Lives Lived Here

Before the rains in the Sahel, everything moves. Fulani herders drive 800,000 cattle south from the Sahara margins through Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso, following corridors their families have walked for generations. They arrive in farming country around October, when the harvest is in. Hausa and Kanuri farmers need the manure their cattle leave behind; the herders need the crop stubble that remains in the fields. This exchange — dung for grazing, fertility for forage — is not a metaphor. It is the soil economy of the western Sahel, and when it works, millet yields rise and herds survive the dry months.

On the shores of Lake Chad, Buduma fishermen launch pirogues into water that covers a fraction of what it did sixty years ago. The lake shrank 90% between the 1960s and 1990s and has since stabilised at reduced levels, but the communities who depend on it — 30 million people across four countries — still organise their year around its seasonal flood pulse. As the water recedes from November to February, Kanuri farmers plant recession crops in the exposed lakebed: beans, maize, peppers. Pastoral households water their livestock at the retreating margins. The rhythm holds, but the margin is thinner than it was.

In village seed banks across the region, women select and store the short-cycle millet and sorghum varieties that can mature in 60 to 90 days — adapted over generations to a growing season that is shrinking. They plant in June or July and harvest in September or October. If the rains arrive two weeks late, yields drop sharply; if they fail entirely, there is nothing between the family and hunger except the dry-season migration south to Abidjan, Accra, or Lagos, where young men and women from farming households send remittances home until planting season calls them back — if it is safe to return.

What Presses

The weight on Western Sahel

Tillabéri and Tahoua, Niger — In the western regions where Niger meets Mali and Burkina Faso, the tri-border area has become the most dangerous intersection in the Sahel. ACAPS reports a marked escalation in violence against civilians through 2025 — not just more attacks, but more lethal ones, concentrated in communities that have no state presence and no way to leave. Tillabéri region alone holds over 300,000 displaced people. Fulani herders moving through this corridor are caught between JNIM checkpoints, ISGS raids, and Nigerien military operations that treat pastoral movement as suspicious. The herders cannot stay — there is no grazing in the dry season — and they cannot move safely. Uranium from the Arlit mines, 800 kilometres north, powered French nuclear plants for 53 years while the Tuareg communities around the mines lived without electricity. Niger's military government nationalised the operations in 2024. Whether the revenue reaches Tillabéri is another question.

Central Mali and the Mopti Corridor — The road from Mopti to Sévaré was once the spine of Malian transhumance — herds moving south through the Inner Niger Delta, the largest wetland in West Africa, where floodplain pasture sustained cattle through the dry months. Today the corridor is contested by JNIM, by military operations, and by the Volunteers for the Defence of the Homeland (locally called the Dana Ambassagou in Dogon country). WFP reports 415,000 internally displaced people in Mali, mostly women and children. Markets in Mopti that once anchored the regional livestock trade are functioning at reduced capacity — traders cannot move goods without armed escort, and the escort itself attracts attack. Barrick Gold and B2Gold operate industrial mines in Kayes and Sikasso to the southwest, producing gold for Dubai, Switzerland, and London, while artisanal mining sites further north fund the armed groups that control the corridor.

The Lake Chad Basin — Lake Chad lost 90% of its surface area between the 1960s and 1990s. It has stabilised since, but at a fraction of its former extent, and the 30 million people across four countries who depend on its seasonal pulse — fishermen, recession farmers, pastoral households — are compressed into a shrinking margin. In Chad, IOM reports that Chadian nationals displaced from Sudan are returning to communities that cannot absorb them. UNHCR tracks new arrivals at the border with changing movement profiles and increasing protection risks. The Doba oil fields in southern Chad, operated by CNPC and Glencore, pipe crude through a 1,070-kilometre pipeline to Cameroon's Atlantic coast. The Ngbaka and Gbaya communities around Doba lost land and water to the pipeline; the oil revenue funds the Déby government's military apparatus but does not reach the lake shore.

The gold mines and the insurgency feed each other. JNIM and ISGS tax artisanal mining sites across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger — gold worth over a billion dollars a year moves through informal channels to Dubai and Turkey, funding the armed groups whose territorial control makes the mining possible and the pastoral economy impossible. The same young men who might have farmed or herded are drawn into digging by the collapse of the agricultural economy, and their labour funds the groups that caused the collapse. The circuit is closed and self-sustaining.
Three military juntas — Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger — expelled France and formed the Alliance of Sahel States, winning popular support by breaking with a colonial patron. Each then invited Russian military contractors (Africa Corps, formerly Wagner Group), traded one external dependency for another, and saw security deteriorate. More territory has been lost to jihadist groups since the coups than before them. The sovereignty is genuine in sentiment and incomplete in practice — and the communities in the rural north, where the fighting is worst, experience neither French nor Russian nor junta protection.
The pastoral transhumance that once moved 800,000 cattle across four countries — sustaining soil fertility through manure exchange, connecting farming and herding economies, feeding millions — depends on corridors that are now controlled by armed groups. Every checkpoint, every tax, every attack on a herder or farmer tightens the knot. When the corridor closes, herders and farmers lose the symbiosis that kept the soil productive, and both fall into the food crisis that the armed groups then exploit for recruitment. The oldest economy in the Sahel is being strangled by the newest.

Five resource streams flow out of the western Sahel every day — gold to Dubai and Switzerland, uranium to France, crude oil through Cameroon to the Atlantic, cotton to Asian factories, manganese to Chinese and Indian steelworks. What does not come back is the water consumed by mining operations, the soil fertility lost to mercury contamination, the young men drawn from farming into artisanal digging, or the pastoral corridors closed by the armed groups that the gold economy funds. The extraction and the insecurity are not separate problems. The Sahel means shore — the shore of the desert, the edge where life is still possible. Ninety-two million people live on that edge. The question the Sahel asks is not whether the rains will come. It is whether, when they do, anyone will be allowed to plant.

Seeds of Hope

Fulani herders still walk the corridor. This is the first and most fundamental act of resistance in the western Sahel — that 800,000 cattle still move south in October and north in June, that the transhumance continues despite armed checkpoints, despite taxation by JNIM and counter-operations by national militaries, despite the fracturing of the farmer-herder compact that made the journey possible. The herders walk because there is no alternative, and because the walk itself is the economy.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Gold from Mali and Burkina Faso reaches Dubai, Switzerland, and London. Barrick Gold (Loulo-Gounkoto, Mali) and B2Gold (Fekola, Mali) are listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange. Artisanal gold worth over $1 billion a year moves through informal channels to UAE refineries with limited origin checks. The Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org) tracks allegations and company responses — search by company name. Ask your jeweller or electronics manufacturer where their gold is sourced, and whether their supply chain excludes conflict-affected artisanal mining zones in the Sahel.
Uranium from Niger powered French nuclear plants for 53 years. Orano (formerly AREVA) operated the Arlit mines from 1971 to 2024, producing roughly 4% of global supply. The surrounding Tuareg communities lived without electricity and documented radioactive contamination in their water (Greenpeace 2010, CRIIRAD 2009). Niger's military government nationalised the mines in 2024. If you live in France, your electricity has a material connection to Arlit. The CRIIRAD contamination reports are publicly available.
Cotton from Burkina Faso's western belt — grown by smallholder farmers earning below production costs — reaches Asian textile manufacturers through SOFITEX, the state cotton company, and eventually becomes clothing sold in European and American shops. Ask the brands you buy from whether they source Burkinabè cotton, and what price the farmers received. Fair trade certification exists but covers a small fraction of production.
