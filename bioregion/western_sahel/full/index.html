What Presses

The weight on Western Sahel

Gold, uranium, cotton, manganese leave the region — farming communities (land acquisition, water diversion for industrial mines), artisanal miners (mercury poisoning, child labor, low wages), women in mining areas (sexual exploitation, resource competition). 6.7 million cannot meet daily food needs. 3.2 million displaced from home.

The gold mines and the insurgency feed each other. JNIM and ISGS tax artisanal mining sites across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger — gold worth over a billion dollars a year moves through informal channels to Dubai and Turkey, funding the armed groups whose territorial control makes the mining possible and the pastoral economy impossible. The same young men who might have farmed or herded are drawn into digging by the collapse of the agricultural economy, and their labour funds the groups that caused the collapse. The circuit is closed and self-sustaining.
Three military juntas — Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger — expelled France and formed the Alliance of Sahel States, winning popular support by breaking with a colonial patron. Each then invited Russian military contractors (Africa Corps, formerly Wagner Group), traded one external dependency for another, and saw security deteriorate. More territory has been lost to jihadist groups since the coups than before them. The sovereignty is genuine in sentiment and incomplete in practice — and the communities in the rural north, where the fighting is worst, experience neither French nor Russian nor junta protection.
The pastoral transhumance that once moved 800,000 cattle across four countries — sustaining soil fertility through manure exchange, connecting farming and herding economies, feeding millions — depends on corridors that are now controlled by armed groups. Every checkpoint, every tax, every attack on a herder or farmer tightens the knot. When the corridor closes, herders and farmers lose the symbiosis that kept the soil productive, and both fall into the food crisis that the armed groups then exploit for recruitment. The oldest economy in the Sahel is being strangled by the newest.

Cascade Chains

Gold economy funds armed groups who close pastoral corridors — herders cannot reach dry-season grazing — livestock losses push families into displacement or artisanal mining — mining feeds more gold into the same armed economy (MLI, BFA, NER — artisanal gold generates $1B+/year across the region; JNIM and ISGS tax production at source)

Lake Chad variability (shrank 90% by the 1990s, partially recovered to ~24,500 km² by 2024) compresses approximately 50 million people into a contested margin — competition between fishers, recession farmers, and pastoral households intensifies — armed groups recruit from unemployed young men — Boko Haram and ISWAP establish control over lakeside communities — displacement further concentrates pressure on the remaining shoreline (TCD, NER — lake lost 90% of surface between 1960s and 1990s)

Military coups expel French and UN forces — security vacuum in rural areas widens — Russian military contractors arrive but concentrate in mining zones, not pastoral corridors — jihadist groups expand territorial control — humanitarian access collapses — displacement accelerates with no international monitoring (MLI, BFA, NER — Alliance of Sahel States formed 2023)

Who Sustains This

Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin · artisanal gold mining taxation; livestock trade taxation; kidnap ransoms

Driven by: We must establish God's rule where the state has failed and France has exploited

The trap: Offering governance through the destruction of governable space. JNIM claims to provide justice and order where the state abandoned communities, and its anti-colonial narrative resonates widely. Yet its attacks on schools, markets, and infrastructure make the territories it controls progressively unlivable—hollowing out the society it promises to govern.

Islamic State in the Greater Sahara · gold mining sites; livestock raiding; extortion

Driven by: We must wage uncompromising jihad; any accommodation is apostasy

The trap: Pursuing purity through violence that destroys community. ISGS rejects JNIM's pragmatism as compromise, insisting on total war. But its indiscriminate violence against civilians—including Muslim communities—alienates the population it needs to sustain a caliphate. Doctrinal purity requires a population that its methods depopulate.

Volunteers for the Defence of the Homeland · state stipends (minimal); community contributions

Driven by: We must protect our villages since neither army nor state will

The trap: Defending community through a militarization that transforms it. The VDP arose from genuine need—communities under attack with no state protection. But arming civilians has triggered ethnic reprisals, created parallel power structures, and drawn communities deeper into a war they sought to survive. The defense has become inseparable from the conflict it was meant to resist.

Patriotic Movement for Safeguard and Restoration · state budget; mining revenue renegotiations

Driven by: We must break with France and save the nation where democracy failed

The trap: Seeking sovereignty through new dependencies. The MPSR junta expelled France and won popular legitimacy by rejecting foreign interference. Yet it has invited Russian military contractors, restricted press freedom, and expanded executive power. The sovereignty achieved by rejecting one external patron depends on alignment with another, while the security it promises remains elusive—more territory has been lost to insurgents since the coup.

Five resource streams flow out of the western Sahel every day — gold to Dubai and Switzerland, uranium to France, crude oil through Cameroon to the Atlantic, cotton to Asian factories, manganese to Chinese and Indian steelworks. What does not come back is the water consumed by mining operations, the soil fertility lost to mercury contamination, the young men drawn from farming into artisanal digging, or the pastoral corridors closed by the armed groups that the gold economy funds. The extraction and the insecurity are not separate problems. They share a supply chain. 6.7M food insecure (current); 3.2M displaced (total); gold, uranium, gold leaving.

The Sahel means shore — the shore of the desert, the edge where life is still possible. Ninety-two million people live on that edge. The question the Sahel asks is not whether the rains will come. It is whether, when they do, anyone will be allowed to plant.

For the hungry...

For the millions across the Sahel who go hungry as the rains fail and the roads close — in Mali, in Burkina Faso, in Niger, in Chad. For the mother in a Djibo displacement site stretching the last of the millet, for the herder whose cattle died on the long dry route north. That food reaches the towns the fighting has cut off.

For the besieged...

For the towns held under blockade, their wells and markets ringed for months by armed groups; and for the villages caught between the jihadist factions, the national armies, and the village militias raised to fight them. For those killed, and for those who must bury them without ceremony.

For the displaced...

For the millions driven from home across the central Sahel, most of them women and children, sheltering in towns that were already poor. For safety on roads that are themselves dangerous, and for welcome at the end of them.

For those who dig the gold...

For the men, women, and children breaking gold from the ground by hand from Mali to Burkina Faso — breathing mercury for a few dollars a day, on land that armed groups tax and hold — and for the farming families pushed off their fields the moment gold is found beneath them.

For the herders of the uranium country...

For the Tuareg communities around Arlit and Akouta, who have lived beside the uranium mines that lit far-off cities for fifty years while their groundwater fell and their pastures thinned; and for all the herding peoples of the Sahel whose way of life the drought and the closing borders press in on.

For those who hold it together...

For the community organizers, the Red Cross volunteers, the imams and catechists and local-radio broadcasters who keep Sahelian towns alive where the state has gone. That their patient work outlasts the men with guns.

Seeds of Hope

Fulani herders still walk the corridor. This is the first and most fundamental act of resistance in the western Sahel — that 800,000 cattle still move south in October and north in June, that the transhumance continues despite armed checkpoints, despite taxation by JNIM and counter-operations by national militaries, despite the fracturing of the farmer-herder compact that made the journey possible. The herders walk because there is no alternative, and because the walk itself is the economy.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Gold from Mali and Burkina Faso reaches Dubai, Switzerland, and London. Barrick Gold (Loulo-Gounkoto, Mali) and B2Gold (Fekola, Mali) are listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange. Artisanal gold worth over $1 billion a year moves through informal channels to UAE refineries with limited origin checks. The Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org) tracks allegations and company responses — search by company name. Ask your jeweller or electronics manufacturer where their gold is sourced, and whether their supply chain excludes conflict-affected artisanal mining zones in the Sahel.
Uranium from Niger powered French nuclear plants for 53 years. Orano (formerly AREVA) operated the Arlit mines from 1971 to 2024, producing roughly 4% of global supply. The surrounding Tuareg communities lived without electricity and documented radioactive contamination in their water (Greenpeace 2010, CRIIRAD 2009). Niger's military government nationalised the mines in 2024. If you live in France, your electricity has a material connection to Arlit. The CRIIRAD contamination reports are publicly available.
Cotton from Burkina Faso's western belt — grown by smallholder farmers earning below production costs — reaches Asian textile manufacturers through SOFITEX, the state cotton company, and eventually becomes clothing sold in European and American shops. Ask the brands you buy from whether they source Burkinabè cotton, and what price the farmers received. Fair trade certification exists but covers a small fraction of production.
