Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Cité Soleil, a neighbourhood in Port-au-Prince controlled by armed gangs, a woman walks forty minutes to collect water from a standpipe that works three days a week. She carries it home in a yellow jerrycan, past a checkpoint where young men with American-made firearms decide who passes. The water will be boiled over charcoal bought from a vendor who bought it from a boy who cut the wood on a hillside in the Artibonite valley. The charcoal cost 25 gourdes — more than it cost last month, because the trees are farther away now. Her daughter is in school today because Mary's Meals provides lunch there; without the meal, the girl would be home helping carry water.

Across the Massacre River in Dajabón, Dominican border guards process a line of Haitian women carrying bundles of used clothing and cooking oil to sell at the bi-weekly market. The women cross legally on market days and are turned back every other day. Some of their husbands are in the cane fields south of Santiago, cutting sugar during the zafra — January to June, fourteen-hour days, earning by the tonne. The sugar becomes Brugal rum. The rum is poured in resort bars in Punta Cana where a Canadian couple on their honeymoon does not know, and has no reason to know, that the sweetness in their glass was cut by a man whose children are in another country and whose legal status could be revoked by presidential decree.

In Ocho Rios, Jamaica, a cruise ship discharges 3,000 passengers for six hours. They will visit Dunn's River Falls, buy jerk chicken, and return to the ship having spent an average of $90 each. The falls are fed by a spring whose flow has dropped 15% in the last decade. The jerk chicken vendor earns less per day than the cruise line charges for a single cabin's minibar. When the ship leaves, it takes the money and leaves the wastewater.

In Scarborough, Tobago, a fisherman pulls up his net and finds less than half what he caught five years ago. The reef off Buccoo has bleached three times since 2019. He knows the reef is dying. He does not know that the same warming that bleaches his reef also intensifies the hurricanes that will arrive in August, or that the ammonia plant sixty miles north in Point Lisas contributes to the nitrogen runoff that feeds the algae that smothers what coral survives. He knows only that the net is lighter, and the fuel to reach deeper water costs more.

What Presses

The weight on Caribbean Islands

Port-au-Prince, Haiti — The capital of Haiti is controlled by armed gangs. Not in the sense of contested — in the sense of administered. The G9 coalition and its rivals divide Port-au-Prince into territories with checkpoints, taxation, and violence that function as governance in the absence of a state. Over 1.3 million people are internally displaced. Women and girls are subjected to sexual violence as a tool of territorial control. Children are recruited. Aid workers are killed — 8 in the past three months — and 16 health facilities have been attacked. The gangs control the port, which means they control the food supply. The gangs control the roads, which means they control the remittances — the wire transfers from Miami and Montreal that are the primary income for most Haitian households. A woman receiving $200 from her sister in Florida may pay $40 at the gang checkpoint to collect it. The state has not held elections since 2016. There is no president. There is no plan.

Hispaniola Border — The Massacre River divides Haiti from the Dominican Republic — named for a 1937 massacre in which Dominican soldiers killed an estimated 20,000 Haitians on Trujillo's orders. Today the border is a labour valve. During the sugar zafra, January to June, Haitian men cross to cut cane in conditions the ILO has documented as forced labour: no contracts, wages paid below minimum, documents confiscated, deportation used as discipline. The bateyes — plantation worker camps — lack running water, sanitation, and legal protections. Between harvests, the Dominican government periodically deports tens of thousands of Haitians and Dominican-born people of Haitian descent — a practice the Inter-American Court has ruled violates human rights. The border opens and closes according to the needs of the plantation, not the needs of the people who cross it.

Eastern Caribbean — On islands where the highest point is a few hundred metres above sea level — Barbuda, Anguilla, the Grenadines — there is no higher ground. Hurricane Irma evacuated the entire population of Barbuda in 2017. Hurricane Maria cost Dominica 226% of its GDP. These islands rebuild with borrowed money, and the debt service consumes the fiscal capacity that would have funded resilience. A fisherman in Grenada watches the reef bleach and knows what it means: less fish, less storm protection, less of everything that made the island livable. He cannot stop the warming. He cannot leave. He fishes what remains and hopes the season is mild.

Haiti's 98% deforestation is not a historical fact — it is an ongoing daily process driven by the absence of alternative cooking fuel. Every meal cooked over charcoal removes another tree; every tree removed means the next hurricane's rainfall becomes a flash flood rather than groundwater recharge. The deforestation is not ignorance or negligence. It is the rational behaviour of families who must feed children tonight with the only fuel available. The trap is economic, not educational: gas costs more than charcoal, and charcoal costs more every month because the trees are farther away.
The Dominican Republic and Haiti share an island but inhabit different centuries of development. The border is a labour valve: open when Dominican sugar and construction industries need workers, closed when nationalist politics demands deportation. Haitian workers in Dominican bateyes live under conditions the ILO classifies as forced labour — no contracts, wages withheld, documents confiscated, deportation used as discipline. The sugar they cut becomes the rum tourists drink in Punta Cana resorts built by Haitian construction workers under the same conditions. The tourism economy and the labour exploitation are the same economy.
The Caribbean coral reef is dying and it is dying faster than the islands can adapt. Coral bleaching from sea surface warming, sedimentation from deforested hillsides, nitrogen runoff from agriculture, and overfishing are converging on a system that provides both the protein (reef fish) and the storm protection (wave energy absorption) that island communities depend on. When the reef dies, the fish leave and the waves arrive unbroken. There is no infrastructure substitute for a living reef.

What leaves the Caribbean — sugar, bauxite, rum, natural gas, cheap labour, tourist photographs — is worth billions at its destination. What stays is the bare hillside where forest was, the contaminated aquifer above the bauxite pit, the bleached reef that no longer breaks the storm surge, and the debt incurred to rebuild after the last hurricane, still accruing interest when the next one arrives. 326 killed (3 months); 6.6M food insecure (current); 1.3M displaced (total); weapons and ammunition leaving; precipitation 3% of normal.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

If you have been to the Caribbean on holiday — Punta Cana, Montego Bay, Barbados, any of the cruise ports — you have participated in an economy that depends on Haitian labour at terms the ILO calls forced. The rum in your cocktail was likely made from sugar cut by Haitian workers in Dominican bateyes. This is not guilt; it is geography. The organisations documenting these conditions — Centro Bonó (Dominican Republic), MUDHA (Movimiento de Mujeres Dominico-Haitianas) — are searchable and accept solidarity. Knowing where your holiday money goes is a beginning.
The carbon emissions that warm the Caribbean Sea and bleach its coral reefs come overwhelmingly from outside the Caribbean. Barbados emits less carbon in a year than a mid-sized European city emits in a month, but Barbados bears the cost of the hurricanes those emissions intensify. Prime Minister Mottley's Bridgetown Initiative (2022) proposed restructuring climate finance so that small island states are not forced to borrow at commercial rates to rebuild from disasters they did not cause. The initiative has the support of over 50 nations. Whether it is funded is a decision being made in capitals you can contact.
Haiti's charcoal crisis is a cooking fuel crisis. Families burn charcoal because they cannot afford propane or kerosene and because no alternative has been provided at scale. The organisations working on this — UNDP Haiti, the Alliance for Clean Cookstoves — have identified the interventions (subsidised LPG, solar cookers, efficient charcoal stoves) but lack the funding and the security access to deploy them. The solution is known. What is missing is the political will and the money. A single hurricane season's reconstruction costs would fund the cookstove transition several times over.

Global Witness — Caribbean investigation

Investigates illicit financial flows, corruption, and natural resource exploitation. Has documented how Caribbean offshore financial centres launder proceeds from extractive industries and arms trafficking, and how climate finance fails to reach frontline communities.

Traces the financial architecture the briefing identifies — where offshore banking in the Cayman Islands and British Virgin Islands launders proceeds from drug and weapons trafficking that fuel violence in Haiti and Jamaica, while the same jurisdictions shelter extractive industry profits from taxation.

Regional technical institute supporting community-based natural resource management across the Caribbean. Works with fisherfolk organizations, forest-dependent communities, and coastal communities on participatory governance of shared resources.

Oxfam — Haiti solidarity

Provides humanitarian assistance and supports community resilience in Haiti, the Caribbean's most crisis-affected state. Works on food security, water access, and disaster preparedness in contexts of armed group control and institutional collapse.

Operates at the sharpest point of Caribbean crisis the briefing documents — where armed groups control Port-au-Prince's fuel and food distribution, charcoal deforestation accelerates flood vulnerability, and remittance-dependent households lose access to basic services as state institutions disintegrate.

AIDA (Interamerican Association for Environmental Defense) advocacy

Uses law to protect the environment and communities affected by extractive and development projects across Latin America and the Caribbean. Has brought cases on coastal development, reef destruction, and mining in Caribbean island nations.
