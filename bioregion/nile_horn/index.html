Lives Lived Here

Before the war, a woman in Wad Madani — Sudan's second city, on the Blue Nile south of Khartoum — would wake before the call to prayer to make kisra, the thin sorghum pancake that is the daily bread of central Sudan. The sorghum came from Gezira farms irrigated by the Nile canal system; the onions and tomatoes from the market gardens along the riverbank; the sesame oil from Gedaref, trucked in from the east. She fed her family, sent the children to school, and by mid-morning was at the market selling groundnuts her sister-in-law grew in Kordofan. In January 2025, Wad Madani fell to the RSF. She is now in one of the camps around Port Sudan, 800 kilometres from home, and the kisra she makes — when she can find sorghum at all — costs four times what it did a year ago.

In Ethiopia's Oromia zone, a woman picks coffee cherries from trees her grandmother planted. Ethiopia is coffee's homeland — the forests of Kaffa where the plant was first cultivated — and this woman's smallholding produces some of the most sought-after beans on earth. But the price she receives at the Addis Ababa auction fluctuates with a New York commodity exchange she has never seen, and in a bad year she earns less than the cost of the fertiliser she bought on credit. Her husband was killed in the fighting in Amhara last year. Her eldest son has gone to Djibouti looking for work on the port. The coffee still grows.

In Lower Jubba, a Somali man who was a pastoralist until his herd died in the 2022 drought now cuts acacia trees into charcoal. He sells to traders who load sacks onto dhows at Kismayo port — 8 million bags a year shipped to Gulf states, according to the FAO, and Al-Shabaab taxes every bag. The rangeland he is cutting down is the same land his camels once grazed. He knows this. He has no alternative. Each tree he fells makes the next drought worse for the pastoralists who remain.

In the Nile Delta, a fellahin farmer in Kafr el-Sheikh grows rice in paddies that have been cultivated for three thousand years. Egypt's water stress stands at 141% — the country withdraws more freshwater than its renewable supply. Every drop comes from the Nile, and the Nile's flow is now a diplomatic variable: Ethiopia's GERD dam, when fully operational, will hold 74 billion cubic metres behind a wall of concrete in the Blue Nile gorge. The farmer does not follow the trilateral negotiations in Addis Ababa. He watches the canal level.

What Presses

The weight on Nile Basin and Horn

Darfur, Sudan — In the displacement camps around El Fasher — North Darfur's capital, now under siege — Fur and Masalit women walk hours for water that used to be a ten-minute journey. The RSF surrounds the city. The SAF bombs from above. The 800,000 people trapped inside are eating one meal a day when they eat at all, and the 21 million food insecure across Sudan include entire communities in Darfur who have not received humanitarian aid in months because neither side will allow access. Beneath the camps, beneath the farms the displaced were driven from, lies the gold of Jebel Amer — more than a billion dollars a year extracted by artisanal miners under RSF control, smuggled through Chad, refined in Dubai. The people living on top of the gold are the ones being cleared from it. Ninety-six aid workers have been killed. Ninety-nine health facilities have been attacked.

Jonglei and Upper Nile, South Sudan — In Jonglei state, cattle raids that once followed seasonal patterns have become mechanised warfare. Armed groups with automatic weapons attack villages, kill men, abduct women and children, and drive cattle herds numbering in the thousands across borders that exist on maps but not on the ground. In March 2026, escalating violence forced aid agencies to withdraw entirely from key areas — Save the Children reported families stranded without any essential services. The 987 killed in three months are Dinka and Nuer pastoralists whose cattle economy was already under pressure from flooding and drought; the violence is not separate from the ecological crisis but woven into it. Oil from Unity and Upper Nile flows through the pipeline to Port Sudan and onward to China, and the revenues that were meant to build schools and clinics fund the competing military networks instead.

Southern and southeastern Ethiopia — In the pastoral lowlands of Somali Region and southern Oromia, consecutive climate shocks have pushed herding communities past their adaptive range. The FAO reports that these areas are "particularly vulnerable due to consecutive and consistent climate shocks" — a diplomatic way of saying the rains have failed repeatedly and the herds are dying. In Amhara, armed Fano militias fight government forces while farmers try to bring in the teff harvest. In Tigray, the peace agreement ended the bombing but not the hunger; 16.8 million Ethiopians cannot feed themselves, and the number is rising.

In Darfur, the RSF does not control gold mines despite the war — it controls them because of it. The displacement of Fur and Masalit communities from Jebel Amer is not collateral damage from fighting between two armies; it is the point. The RSF generates over a billion dollars a year from gold that could not be extracted at this scale if the farming communities who lived on the land were still there. The war provides the pretext, the displacement provides the access, and the gold provides the funding for more war. This is not a feedback loop that could be broken by a ceasefire. The economic incentive to continue displacement is structural.
The Nile is not a river — it is a chain of dependencies with no slack. Ethiopia controls the headwaters and is filling a dam that will reshape downstream flow. Sudan sits at the confluence and is burning. Egypt sits at the terminus, withdrawing 141% of its renewable supply, importing 60% of its wheat, and feeding 70 million people on a bread subsidy that costs $3 billion a year. When one link in this chain moves — a dam filling faster, a war disrupting irrigation, a global wheat price spike — the entire downstream system responds. There is no buffer. Egypt cannot get water from anywhere else. Sudan cannot farm without the river. Ethiopia will not stop filling the dam that it calls its sovereign right and its 120 million people's path to electrification. The Nile binds 300 million people to a single variable with three governments and no agreement.
In South Sudan, oil revenues that were meant to build a state instead built competing military networks. The CNPC-led consortium pumps crude from Unity and Upper Nile, the pipeline carries it to Port Sudan, the revenue enters Juba — and from Juba it flows not to schools, hospitals, or roads but to the patronage systems that keep military commanders loyal. The Nuer and Shilluk communities living in the oil-producing areas are displaced, their land contaminated, and the wealth extracted from beneath them funds the very forces that displaced them. South Sudan is the world's newest country and one of its most vivid demonstrations that resource wealth, without institutions to distribute it, becomes resource violence.

Nine resource streams leave this bioregion — gold, oil, charcoal, gum arabic, coffee, cattle, livestock, teak, natural gas — and what comes back is weapons for the armies, debt for the governments, and nothing for the 48.8 million people who cannot feed themselves. Seeds of Hope

In Sudan, where the state has collapsed and the international community has largely looked away, neighbourhood Emergency Response Rooms — volunteer networks organised by the same young people who led the 2019 revolution — are running medical clinics, distributing food, and documenting atrocities in areas where no international organisation can operate. They work without funding, without protection, and without recognition, and they are the primary humanitarian presence in large parts of Khartoum, Darfur, and Kordofan.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Sudan supplies roughly 70% of the world's gum arabic — the emulsifier in Coca-Cola, Pepsi, and thousands of processed foods. The smallholder farmers and women harvesters who produce it are among the 21 million Sudanese who cannot feed themselves. Ask whether the products in your cupboard contain gum arabic (listed as E414 in the EU, "acacia gum" in the US). The supply chain connects your kitchen to Kordofan. The Gum Arabic Board of Sudan has historically been the sole exporter; the war has fragmented this. Track reporting by the Enough Project (enoughproject.org) on conflict commodities in Sudan.
Darfur gold refined in Dubai enters the global market as legitimate bullion. Global Witness has documented the route from Jebel Amer to DMCC-licensed refineries. The gold may be in your jewellery, your electronics, your pension fund's commodity holdings. The London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) sets responsible sourcing standards — ask whether your bank or pension fund requires LBMA compliance for gold holdings. Report at globalwitness.org/en/campaigns/conflict-minerals/the-destruction-of-darfur.
South Sudan's oil is extracted by a consortium led by China National Petroleum Corporation and flows to Asian markets. The Nuer and Shilluk communities displaced from the oil fields have received no benefit from the wealth beneath their land. If your country imports South Sudanese crude (marketed as Dar Blend), the supply chain connects to the displacement. The UN Panel of Experts on South Sudan documents revenue diversion in annual reports to the Security Council — these are public documents searchable at un.org.

Rift Valley Institute investigation

Independent research organization producing field-based studies on the Horn of Africa and Great Lakes. Conducts longitudinal research on pastoralist livelihoods, gold supply chains, and displacement patterns in Darfur and South Sudan.

Directly investigates the gold and cattle corridor flows the briefing traces — documenting how artisanal gold mining in Jebel Amer finances RSF operations, and how displacement severs pastoralist access to seasonal grazing routes across Darfur.

Enough Project advocacy

Policy advocacy organization focused on ending mass atrocities and resource-linked conflicts in Africa. Publishes investigative reports on conflict gold supply chains, sanctions evasion through UAE gold refineries, and RSF financing networks.

Traces the same Darfur gold-to-Dubai pipeline the briefing documents — artisanal mining under RSF control, smuggling through Chad and Libya, and refining in UAE, naming the corporate actors who purchase conflict-tainted gold.

Nile Basin Initiative solidarity

Intergovernmental partnership of ten Nile riparian states facilitating cooperative water resource management. Operates shared monitoring systems and joint infrastructure projects across the Blue Nile, White Nile, and Atbara sub-basins.

Addresses the Nile water allocation conflicts central to the briefing — the GERD dam dispute between Ethiopia, Sudan, and Egypt, and downstream irrigation dependencies that determine food security for 250 million people across the basin.

Sudan Transparency and Policy Tracker investigation

Clingendael Institute's Conflict Research Unit tracks wartime economic networks in Sudan, documenting how gold revenues, qat trade, and remittance flows sustain armed factions and shape civilian survival strategies.

Maps the war economy the briefing describes — charcoal production in deforested areas, qat supply chains from Ethiopia sustaining combatants, and how diaspora remittances through mobile money are both lifeline and extraction point for armed groups.

Médecins Sans Frontières — East Africa solidarity

Provides emergency medical care in displacement camps across Darfur, Kordofan, and Khartoum State. One of few international organizations maintaining operations in active conflict zones, documenting civilian casualties and healthcare infrastructure destruction.

Operates at the endpoints where the briefing's material flows become bodily harm — treating malnutrition from disrupted cattle corridors and millet harvests, burn injuries from scorched-earth campaigns, and waterborne disease from Nile infrastructure collapse.

Global Witness — Sudan advocacy

Investigates the role of natural resource exploitation in funding conflict, with specific reporting on Sudanese gold exports, corporate due diligence failures, and sanctions circumvention through intermediary states.

Targets the export node of the gold flow the briefing traces — where artisanal gold from Darfur enters formal markets through refineries in UAE and Turkey, naming the companies and financial institutions that launder conflict minerals into global supply chains.
