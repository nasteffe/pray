In St. James Parish, Louisiana, between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, the air smells like chlorine and burnt plastic on still mornings. The people who live here — overwhelmingly Black, overwhelmingly poor — call this stretch of the Mississippi Cancer Alley: 150 miles of petrochemical plants, 200 facilities, producing the plastics and gasoline and fertiliser that stock every supermarket shelf in America.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Plaquemines Parish, a Vietnamese shrimp fisherman motors into Barataria Bay, navigating around oil platform debris that still litters the shallow water fifteen years after the Deepwater Horizon blowout. His family came here as refugees in the 1970s and learned to shrimp from Cajun neighbours who had fished these waters for generations. The catch has declined every decade since he started — the dead zone, the wetland loss, the oil. His daughter works at a crawfish processing plant in Abbeville; she makes more than he does and does not want to inherit the boat.

In Manchester, a neighbourhood tucked between the Houston Ship Channel and a Valero refinery, a mother checks the air quality app on her phone before deciding whether her son can play outside. The flare stack is burning high today — upset conditions, the refinery calls it, which means an uncontrolled release of sulphur dioxide and benzene while they fix something. She has lived here her whole life. The house was affordable because the refinery was already here when her parents bought it; now it is worthless because the refinery is still here. Her son has asthma. So does the boy next door.

In Reynosa, across the river from McAllen, Texas, a woman assembles automotive wiring harnesses at a maquiladora owned by a German auto parts company. She earns approximately $4–5 an hour for work that would pay $18 in the plant across the border. The finished harnesses cross the bridge in trucks that pass through customs in minutes; she crosses the same bridge once a year to buy school supplies for her children, and the crossing takes four hours. The border is porous for products and rigid for people — that asymmetry is the business model of the corridor.

What Presses

The weight on Mississippi Delta

Shale oil and natural gas, migrant labor leave the region — Indigenous communities (pipeline routes through treaty lands), Appalachian communities (water contamination, methane emissions), surrounding residents (air quality, health impacts). 262,411 displaced from home.

Cancer Alley is not an accident of geography. Between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, petrochemical plants were sited in formerly enslaved communities along the River Road — the same plantation corridor where Black families had lived since Emancipation. The zoning decisions that placed 200 chemical facilities in majority-Black parishes were made in rooms those communities were not invited into. Today, residents of St. James Parish breathe chloroprene at levels 50 times the national cancer risk benchmark, and the EPA's own risk assessments confirm what the death certificates have been saying for decades.
The Mississippi River levee system protects cities and refineries but starves the delta of the sediment that built it. The Army Corps of Engineers maintains 3,500 miles of levees for navigation and flood control — which keeps barges moving and refineries dry — but the same containment prevents the river from depositing the 200 million tons of sediment per year that once replenished the coastal marshes. The marshes die, the storm buffer shrinks, and the next hurricane hits harder. The infrastructure that protects commerce destroys the land that protects everyone.
The Gulf of Mexico dead zone and the Midwest corn belt are the same system. Fertiliser applied in Iowa runs off into the Maumee, the Ohio, the Missouri, and the Mississippi, arriving in the Gulf as nitrogen and phosphorus that feed algal blooms and suffocate everything beneath them. The shrimp fishers of Louisiana and the corn farmers of Iowa have never met, but they share a river, and what one puts in the other takes out.

Cascade Chains

Oil pipeline channels cut through wetlands → saltwater intrusion kills freshwater marsh → storm buffer disappears → hurricane damage increases → refineries shut down and flare → fenceline communities absorb both the storm and the toxic release (USA — Louisiana loses 25 sq mi of wetland per year; 200 petrochemical facilities in Cancer Alley; Manchester, TX childhood asthma 3x national average)

Fertiliser runoff from 31 upstream states → Gulf dead zone (6,334 sq mi in 2021; five-year average 4,347 sq mi) → shrimp and oyster catch collapses → Vietnamese and Cajun fishing families lose livelihoods → coastal communities depopulate → wetland maintenance lost → further storm vulnerability (USA — Terrebonne and Plaquemines Parishes; dead zone measured annually by NOAA since 1985)

Cartel territorial control in Tamaulipas/Veracruz → maquiladora workers transit through violence → migrant labour flows northward under coercion → undocumented workers fill Gulf Coast agriculture and construction with no legal protection → wages suppressed across the sector (MEX/USA — 262K displaced in Mexico; maquiladora wages ~$4–5/hr vs $18+ across the border)

The trap: Achieving stability through constant expansion and violence that ensures instability. CJNG's model requires perpetual territorial conquest to maintain internal cohesion, but each new front creates new enemies and new fragmentation, making the consolidation of power permanently impossible.

Cargill (US grain trading + feedlot beef) · Privately-held global grain trading (corn, soy, wheat) — world's largest private agribusiness, no SEC disclosure obligations; Cargill Protein (cattle feedlots, beef packing — High River AB, Dodge City KS, Friona TX, Schuyler NE); Iowa Falls and Eddyville biorefineries (ethanol and corn-derived sweeteners); Cargill Salt, Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate, Cargill Animal Nutrition cross-subsidies

Driven by: We must feed a growing world responsibly through sustainable supply chains

The trap: Each regenerative-agriculture pilot, water-stewardship commitment, and farmer-support programme operates inside a procurement architecture whose price-setting power makes the scale and chemical intensity of corn-soy monoculture the only solvent farming model for the member growers Cargill buys from. The privately-held structure is itself structurally load-bearing: it lets Cargill claim stewardship leadership without the disclosure regime that would let the public weigh those claims against the throughput they ride on. The Ogallala is being mined to fund the same supply chain whose stewardship narrative would have the depletion reversed.

Tyson Foods (US vertical-integration poultry, beef, pork) · Vertical poultry operations — contract growers (~5,000 farms) raise birds Tyson owns from hatchery to processing; Beef packing (largest US beef processor by volume — Dakota City NE, Amarillo TX, Garden City KS, Lexington NE); Pork operations (post-2014 Hillshire acquisition — Storm Lake IA, Madison NE, Logansport IN); Branded value-added (Jimmy Dean, Hillshire Farm, Ball Park, Sara Lee Frozen Bakery)

Driven by: We must feed America with safe, affordable, responsibly raised protein

The trap: The contract-grower model converts independent farmers into capital-trapped operators of company-specification chicken houses they cannot exit without bankruptcy. The 'family farmer in the heartland' the brand celebrates is the same operator whose mortgage and chicken-house debt are calibrated to Tyson's price-per-pound. The migrant workforce that runs the line at packing plants is the same workforce whose presence the political coalition Tyson's lobbying funds organises against. The affordable-protein narrative makes both contradictions structurally necessary and politically illegible.

Three resource streams flow out of the Gulf Coast corridor — hydrocarbons, food, and human labour — worth hundreds of billions at their destination markets. What remains behind is the air in Cancer Alley, the water in the dead zone, the wetlands that disappear a football field at a time. The products reach every American household. The cost never leaves the corridor. 262,411 displaced (total); shale oil and natural gas, migrant labor leaving.

The Mississippi carries the topsoil of thirty-one states past Cancer Alley and into the Gulf, where it feeds nothing. The river was channelled for commerce, and commerce was served, and the wetlands that the river built over ten thousand years are dying for want of the sediment that now falls uselessly into deep water. The people of St. James Parish know this. They watch the land shrink and the plants multiply and the funerals come closer together, and they organise anyway — not because they expect to win, but because this is their home and they will not leave it without being heard.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Every gallon of gasoline sold in America connects to the Gulf Coast refinery corridor. The five largest refiners — ExxonMobil, Chevron, Valero, Marathon, Phillips 66 — operate facilities in Cancer Alley and the Houston Ship Channel. Their air permits, emissions reports, and environmental justice complaints are public record at the EPA's Enforcement and Compliance History Online (ECHO) database (echo.epa.gov). Search by facility name and zip code. Read what the monitors measure, and compare it to what the companies report.
The plastics in your home — bags, bottles, packaging, synthetic clothing — are made from ethylene produced in Gulf Coast petrochemical plants, many of them in St. James and St. John the Baptist Parishes, Louisiana. Formosa Plastics, whose $9.4 billion expansion was initially blocked by community organising in 2022 before a 2024 appeals court reinstated the permits, is one company. The industry trade group is the American Chemistry Council. The communities are represented by RISE St. James (risestjames.org), the Louisiana Bucket Brigade (labucketbrigade.org), and Earthjustice. These organisations accept support and publish air monitoring data.
If you eat Gulf shrimp, the fishery that caught it is competing with the dead zone fed by Midwest fertiliser runoff and the wetland loss caused by oil pipeline channels. The connection between a shrimp dinner in Chicago and a fishing family in Terrebonne Parish passes through the same river system. The Gulf Hypoxia Task Force publishes annual dead zone measurements (epa.gov/ms-htf). The data is there. The question is what you do with it.
