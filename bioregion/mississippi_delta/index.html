moderate Precipitation 180% of normal; temperatures +9.3°C above baseline.

In St. James Parish, Louisiana, between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, the air smells like chlorine and burnt plastic on still mornings. The people who live here — overwhelmingly Black, overwhelmingly poor — call this stretch of the Mississippi Cancer Alley: 150 miles of petrochemical plants, 200 facilities, producing the plastics and gasoline and fertiliser that stock every supermarket shelf in America.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Plaquemines Parish, a Vietnamese shrimp fisherman motors into Barataria Bay, navigating around oil platform debris that still litters the shallow water fifteen years after the Deepwater Horizon blowout. His family came here as refugees in the 1970s and learned to shrimp from Cajun neighbours who had fished these waters for generations. The catch has declined every decade since he started — the dead zone, the wetland loss, the oil. His daughter works at a crawfish processing plant in Abbeville; she makes more than he does and does not want to inherit the boat.

For the gifts of this place…

For the gifts of this place — for the great river and the black delta soil, for the music this country gave the world, for the shrimp and oysters of the coast and the deep warmth of its people. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what the river carries away…

For all that this river bears out to the world — the grain of a whole continent, the oil and the chemicals — and for the reckoning that the same river, and the same coast, are left to hold what the trade leaves behind.

For those who breathe the cost — Cancer Alley…

For the families of the Louisiana river parishes, in the stretch they call Cancer Alley, where more than a hundred chemical plants crowd against small, mostly Black towns that freed people founded, and the air itself has turned against them. For St. James and Reserve and the towns like them, and for their long fight simply to breathe.

For those the water is taking…

For the Gulf communities losing their ground to the sea — the Isle de Jean Charles band moved off their sinking island, the shrimpers and the storm-poor of a coast that loses a field of land every hour. For those who rebuild after each hurricane with less each time.

For holding both…

For eyes to see that one river carries both festival and funeral, both plenty and poison; and for love that does not look away from the neighbours for whom this is no calm place at all.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Every gallon of gasoline sold in America connects to the Gulf Coast refinery corridor. The five largest refiners — ExxonMobil, Chevron, Valero, Marathon, Phillips 66 — operate facilities in Cancer Alley and the Houston Ship Channel. Their air permits, emissions reports, and environmental justice complaints are public record at the EPA's Enforcement and Compliance History Online (ECHO) database (echo.epa.gov). Search by facility name and zip code. Read what the monitors measure, and compare it to what the companies report.
The plastics in your home — bags, bottles, packaging, synthetic clothing — are made from ethylene produced in Gulf Coast petrochemical plants, many of them in St. James and St. John the Baptist Parishes, Louisiana. Formosa Plastics, whose $9.4 billion expansion was initially blocked by community organising in 2022 before a 2024 appeals court reinstated the permits, is one company. The industry trade group is the American Chemistry Council. The communities are represented by RISE St. James (risestjames.org), the Louisiana Bucket Brigade (labucketbrigade.org), and Earthjustice. These organisations accept support and publish air monitoring data.
If you eat Gulf shrimp, the fishery that caught it is competing with the dead zone fed by Midwest fertiliser runoff and the wetland loss caused by oil pipeline channels. The connection between a shrimp dinner in Chicago and a fishing family in Terrebonne Parish passes through the same river system. The Gulf Hypoxia Task Force publishes annual dead zone measurements (epa.gov/ms-htf). The data is there. The question is what you do with it.
