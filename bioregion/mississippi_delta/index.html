In St. James Parish, Louisiana, between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, the air smells like chlorine and burnt plastic on still mornings. The people who live here — overwhelmingly Black, overwhelmingly poor — call this stretch of the Mississippi Cancer Alley: 150 miles of petrochemical plants, 200 facilities, producing the plastics and gasoline and fertiliser that stock every supermarket shelf in America.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Plaquemines Parish, a Vietnamese shrimp fisherman motors into Barataria Bay, navigating around oil platform debris that still litters the shallow water fifteen years after the Deepwater Horizon blowout. His family came here as refugees in the 1970s and learned to shrimp from Cajun neighbours who had fished these waters for generations. The catch has declined every decade since he started — the dead zone, the wetland loss, the oil. His daughter works at a crawfish processing plant in Abbeville; she makes more than he does and does not want to inherit the boat.

In Manchester, a neighbourhood tucked between the Houston Ship Channel and a Valero refinery, a mother checks the air quality app on her phone before deciding whether her son can play outside. The flare stack is burning high today — upset conditions, the refinery calls it, which means an uncontrolled release of sulphur dioxide and benzene while they fix something. She has lived here her whole life. The house was affordable because the refinery was already here when her parents bought it; now it is worthless because the refinery is still here. Her son has asthma. So does the boy next door.

In Reynosa, across the river from McAllen, Texas, a woman assembles automotive wiring harnesses at a maquiladora owned by a German auto parts company. She earns $2.10 an hour for work that would pay $18 in the plant across the border. The finished harnesses cross the bridge in trucks that pass through customs in minutes; she crosses the same bridge once a year to buy school supplies for her children, and the crossing takes four hours. The border is porous for products and rigid for people — that asymmetry is the business model of the corridor.

What Presses

The weight on Mississippi Delta

St. James Parish, Louisiana — Between the Blind River and the Mississippi, in a parish that is 50% Black and where the median household income is $38,000, there are twelve petrochemical facilities within a ten-mile radius of the elementary school. The chloroprene monitor at the Fifth Ward schoolyard regularly reads fifty times the level the EPA considers acceptable cancer risk. Parents know. They have always known. The funerals tell them what the monitors confirm. Sharon Lavigne lost her sister and five neighbours to cancer before she started organising. The Formosa plant was stopped, but the twelve existing plants still run, and the air has not improved.

Terrebonne Parish, Louisiana — Isle de Jean Charles is disappearing. The island, home to the Biloxi-Chitimacha-Choctaw tribe, has lost 98% of its land since 1955 — oil channels, subsidence, and sea level rise eating it from every side. The tribe was offered a relocation site on higher ground in 2016 under a $48 million HUD grant; as of 2025, fewer than half the families have moved, because the new site has no church, no cemetery, no fishing access — no version of the life they built on the island. To relocate is to survive. To relocate is also to disappear. They are being asked to choose between their land and their lives, and no one who makes that demand has to live with the answer.

Reynosa, Tamaulipas — The maquiladoras run three shifts. The women who work them cross the city in the dark, through neighbourhoods where the Gulf Cartel and its splinter factions contest territory block by block. A woman assembling wiring harnesses for a German automaker earns in a day what the same work pays in an hour across the river in McAllen. The finished product crosses the bridge in minutes. She waits four hours if she is lucky. The border exists to keep the wage differential intact; that differential is the reason the plant is here and not there. Everyone involved understands this. No one is required to say it.

Cancer Alley is not an accident of geography. Between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, petrochemical plants were sited in formerly enslaved communities along the River Road — the same plantation corridor where Black families had lived since Emancipation. The zoning decisions that placed 200 chemical facilities in majority-Black parishes were made in rooms those communities were not invited into. Today, residents of St. James Parish breathe chloroprene at levels 50 times the national cancer risk benchmark, and the EPA's own risk assessments confirm what the death certificates have been saying for decades.
The Mississippi River levee system protects cities and refineries but starves the delta of the sediment that built it. The Army Corps of Engineers maintains 3,500 miles of levees for navigation and flood control — which keeps barges moving and refineries dry — but the same containment prevents the river from depositing the 200 million tons of sediment per year that once replenished the coastal marshes. The marshes die, the storm buffer shrinks, and the next hurricane hits harder. The infrastructure that protects commerce destroys the land that protects everyone.
The Gulf of Mexico dead zone and the Midwest corn belt are the same system. Fertiliser applied in Iowa runs off into the Maumee, the Ohio, the Missouri, and the Mississippi, arriving in the Gulf as nitrogen and phosphorus that feed algal blooms and suffocate everything beneath them. The shrimp fishers of Louisiana and the corn farmers of Iowa have never met, but they share a river, and what one puts in the other takes out.

Three resource streams flow out of the Gulf Coast corridor — hydrocarbons, food, and human labour — worth hundreds of billions at their destination markets. What remains behind is the air in Cancer Alley, the water in the dead zone, the wetlands that disappear a football field at a time. The products reach every American household. The cost never leaves the corridor. 1,244 killed (3 months); 262,411 displaced (total); shale oil and natural gas, avocados, migrant labor leaving; precipitation 51% of normal; 460 fires (7 days).

The Mississippi carries the topsoil of thirty-one states past Cancer Alley and into the Gulf, where it feeds nothing. The river was channelled for commerce, and commerce was served, and the wetlands that the river built over ten thousand years are dying for want of the sediment that now falls uselessly into deep water. The people of St. James Parish know this. They watch the land shrink and the plants multiply and the funerals come closer together, and they organise anyway — not because they expect to win, but because this is their home and they will not leave it without being heard.

Seeds of Hope

In Welcome, Louisiana, Sharon Lavigne — a retired schoolteacher and lifelong St. James Parish resident — founded RISE St. James in 2018 after watching neighbours and family members die of cancer in a community surrounded by petrochemical plants. In 2022, a federal judge revoked the air permits for a $9.4 billion Formosa Plastics complex that would have doubled the chemical load in her parish. The ruling cited environmental justice for the first time in Louisiana permitting history.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Every gallon of gasoline sold in America connects to the Gulf Coast refinery corridor. The five largest refiners — ExxonMobil, Chevron, Valero, Marathon, Phillips 66 — operate facilities in Cancer Alley and the Houston Ship Channel. Their air permits, emissions reports, and environmental justice complaints are public record at the EPA's Enforcement and Compliance History Online (ECHO) database (echo.epa.gov). Search by facility name and zip code. Read what the monitors measure, and compare it to what the companies report.
The plastics in your home — bags, bottles, packaging, synthetic clothing — are made from ethylene produced in Gulf Coast petrochemical plants, many of them in St. James and St. John the Baptist Parishes, Louisiana. Formosa Plastics, whose $9.4 billion expansion was blocked by community organising, is one company. The industry trade group is the American Chemistry Council. The communities are represented by RISE St. James (risestjames.org), the Louisiana Bucket Brigade (labucketbrigade.org), and Earthjustice. These organisations accept support and publish air monitoring data.
If you eat Gulf shrimp, the fishery that caught it is competing with the dead zone fed by Midwest fertiliser runoff and the wetland loss caused by oil pipeline channels. The connection between a shrimp dinner in Chicago and a fishing family in Terrebonne Parish passes through the same river system. The Gulf Hypoxia Task Force publishes annual dead zone measurements (epa.gov/ms-htf). The data is there. The question is what you do with it.
