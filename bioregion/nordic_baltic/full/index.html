Lives Lived Here

In a village in Norrbotten, northern Sweden, a Sámi reindeer herder marks calves in early June. Her family's siida — the cooperative herding unit — manages 3,000 animals across a territory that crosses into Norway, following routes older than the border. This year the calving grounds are quieter: LKAB's Kiruna expansion has pushed the fence line closer, and the vibration from blasting disturbs the does. She sells reindeer meat to a cooperative in Jokkmokk that ships vacuum-packed cuts to restaurants in Stockholm, where a plate of suovas costs more than she earns for the whole carcass. The iron ore beneath her pasture travels by rail to German steelmakers who supply European car factories building electric vehicles. The green transition needs her land more than it needs her.

In Jutland, a Danish pig farmer hoses down a barn holding 2,000 sows. Denmark has more pigs than people — 30 million animals producing nitrogen that washes through the sandy soil into streams that feed the Baltic. His operation is efficient by every metric the EU tracks except the one the Baltic measures: oxygen. The dead zone off the Danish coast grows each summer. His pork goes to Germany, China, Japan. He has never seen the seabed he is helping to kill.

In Klaipėda, Lithuania, a dockworker loads grain onto a bulk carrier. The wheat came from smallholdings in Utena and Vilnius districts — farms of less than 20 hectares where the farmer earns less per kilogram than the trader who buys it. His wife works in a care home in Malmö, two weeks on, two weeks home. The remittance she sends back is worth more than his monthly wage at the port. Their daughter studies nursing in Vilnius and plans to follow her mother to Sweden. The village they live in has lost a quarter of its working-age population since Lithuania joined the EU.

What Presses

The weight on Nordic-Baltic

Sápmi, northern Scandinavia — In Jokkmokk, Swedish Lapland, a Sámi herder named Lars-Anders drives his snowmobile along a fenceline that was not there five years ago. On the other side, LKAB is drilling test cores for iron ore. His reindeer cannot cross. The calving grounds that his grandmother used are now inside a prospecting concession. He has filed an objection with the Mining Inspectorate; the Inspectorate has approved the permit anyway. His siida — eight families, 4,000 reindeer — will have to find new winter pasture or reduce the herd. Reducing the herd means one or two families stop herding. Stopping herding means the knowledge stops. His daughter is in university in Umeå. She wants to come back to the herd. He is not sure there will be a herd to come back to.

Ida-Virumaa, northeastern Estonia — In Kohtla-Järve, a town built for the oil shale industry, the air smells of sulphur on still days. The Eesti Energia power plants burn oil shale for 80 percent of Estonia's electricity — the most carbon-intensive energy source in the EU. The workers are mostly Russian-speaking Estonians whose families came in the Soviet era to work the mines. The EU's emissions trading system is making the plants uneconomic. When the plants close, the town has nothing else. The workers know this. The transition plan promises retraining and green jobs. The workers have heard transition plans before.

Western Jutland, Denmark — A pig farmer in Ringkøbing-Skjern municipality manages 5,000 sows. His operation is modern, efficient, and compliant with every regulation Denmark applies. The nitrogen from his fields enters the Skjern River system and reaches the North Sea and, via currents, the Baltic. He is aware of this — the inspectors come, the targets are discussed, the HELCOM reports are cited. He cannot reduce his herd and service his debt. The debt is structured around the herd size. The subsidy is structured around the herd size. The dead zone is structured around the herd size. He did not design the system. He cannot afford to leave it.

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global holds over $1.8 trillion (USD) from North Sea oil and gas revenues — fossil fuels whose combustion is warming the Arctic three to four times faster than the global average, melting the permafrost and disrupting the reindeer herding that Sámi communities in northern Norway have practised for millennia. The fund has divested from some coal companies on ethical grounds. It has not divested from itself.
Sweden and Finland clearcut boreal forest — Europe's largest carbon sink and the Sámi's primary lichen grazing ecosystem — and certify the practice as sustainable under FSC standards that measure replanting, not biodiversity. Stora Enso, UPM, and SCA control 70 percent of Swedish pulp capacity. Old-growth forest is down to 5 percent of its historical extent. The paper in your hand may be part of this.
Danish pig farming produces 30 million animals a year — more pigs than people — whose nitrogen runoff feeds the Baltic Sea's dead zones. The pork goes to Germany, China, and Japan. The dead seabed stays. No country around the Baltic is meeting its Helsinki Commission nitrogen reduction targets. The sea cannot flush itself fast enough to recover.

Cascade Chains

Arctic warming → rain-on-snow events → lichen locked under ice crust → reindeer starvation → Sámi herders forced to emergency-feed with purchased hay → economic dependency on the same state that approves mine and wind farm permits on their grazing land

Industrial forestry certification as 'sustainable' → continued clearcutting of old-growth boreal → carbon sink loss + biodiversity collapse → Sámi lichen habitat degradation → herding range contraction → cultural erosion as young Sámi leave herding for wage labour

Danish/Lithuanian agricultural nitrogen → Baltic eutrophication → dead zones → cod stock collapse → small-scale fishers lose livelihood → industrial trawler consolidation → remaining catch (herring, sprat) under increasing pressure as ecosystem simplifies

Who Sustains This

Government Pension Fund Global (Norway) · Norwegian continental-shelf petroleum revenues (Equinor production + state direct financial interest SDFI); Capital appreciation of ~$1.7T global equity, fixed-income, real-estate, and renewables portfolio; Norwegian state petroleum tax (78% effective rate on offshore profits)

Driven by: We must demonstrate that an oil economy can manage capital ethically for future generations

The trap: Each ethical exclusion reinforces the legitimacy of the petroleum proceeds being deployed. The fund's existence — its capacity to fund the welfare-state generosity Norway projects internationally — depends on continuing the extraction it tells global markets to wind down. The more credibly the fund acts as a green investor, the more politically sustainable continued Norwegian production becomes; the more Norwegian production continues, the larger the deposit base of the green-investor instrument grows. The decarbonisation narrative and the petrostate reality are not contradictions to be resolved but the two sides of a single operation.

Luossavaara-Kiirunavaara AB (LKAB) · Iron-ore pellet exports (~80% of EU iron-ore supply) from Kiruna and Malmberget underground mines; HYBRIT joint venture (with SSAB and Vattenfall) for hydrogen-direct-reduction 'fossil-free' iron; Per Geijer rare-earth deposit (largest known REE find in Europe, announced 2023); Swedish state budget allocations for green-steel demonstration plants

Driven by: We must deliver fossil-free iron to make Europe's steel green

The trap: The green-steel transition the world needs requires more ore, not less, and the ore body that anchors the narrative sits beneath Sápmi land the Swedish state never freely held. The mine that destroys Sami winter pasture becomes the precondition for Sweden's claim to climate leadership; the climate leadership becomes the moral framework that makes the destruction politically tolerable. HYBRIT advances each year and the displacement advances at the same pace — the technology being developed is the technology that requires the displacement to continue.

Sveaskog AB (Swedish state forestry company) · Productive forest holdings of ~3.1 million hectares (~14% of Sweden's productive forest); Pulpwood, sawlog, and biofuel sales to Swedish and Finnish pulp/paper and sawmill industries; Carbon-credit issuance under EU and voluntary frameworks; Swedish state budget remittances of profit distributions

Driven by: We must manage the forest sustainably for climate, biodiversity, and the Swedish bioeconomy

The trap: FSC certification, replanting schedules, and rotation forestry all operate within a productive cycle that converts old-growth carbon storage into young-stand pulpwood. Each individual harvest audit verifies compliance; the cumulative effect of the harvests across the holding is what no audit can register, because no audit is written to register it. The certification is the mechanism by which a state forestry company can preserve the symbolic image of Nordic sustainability while operating an industrial regime whose ecological outcome — lichen loss, soil carbon release, biodiversity decline — is well documented in the same scientific literature the company's sustainability reports cite.

Six resource streams flow from this region — iron ore, crude oil, natural gas, timber, farmed salmon, nickel — and the wealth they create is among the most equitably distributed on earth, within these borders. The question this briefing asks is not whether Nordic societies care. It is who pays for the caring. The salmon feed comes from West African fisheries. The pension fund returns come from fossil fuel combustion elsewhere. The green transition minerals come from Sámi land. The eldercare comes from Baltic women whose own children grow up with a parent in another country. The Nordic model works. The question is for whom, and at whose expense. timber, pulp, paper, crude oil, natural gas, farmed Atlantic salmon leaving.

The difficulty of praying for this region is that we are not praying from outside it. The pension fund that warmed the Arctic holds shares in companies we own. The salmon on the plate was fed with fish meal taken from someone else's coast. The iron in the car was mined from under a reindeer calving ground. The carer who looked after grandmother last winter left her own children in Kaunas. To pray for the Sámi herder and the Baltic dockworker is to pray about our own participation in what presses on them. This is not comfortable. It is not meant to be.

Seeds of Hope

In 2020, the Swedish Supreme Court ruled in the Girjas case that the Girjas Sámi village holds exclusive hunting and fishing rights over its traditional territory — the first time a Swedish court recognised Sámi land rights against the state. The ruling has not stopped mining permits, but it established a legal precedent that every subsequent case must reckon with.

Looking Ahead

  • The structural pressures on this region are accelerating, not receding.
  • The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act designates iron, lithium, cobalt, and rare earths as strategically essential — which means expanded extraction in Scandinavia, which means more pressure on Sámi land.
  • Norway's Fensfeltet rare earth deposit, if developed, would be Europe's largest — and it sits in an area of reindeer pasture.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global (NBIM, nbim.no) publishes its full portfolio. You can search it by company name. If your own pension fund invests in the same companies — and most European and North American funds do — you are a co-beneficiary of the same extraction chain. The fund's ethical exclusion list is also public. Ask your fund manager: do you follow the same exclusions, or different ones?
The Fosen wind farm Supreme Court ruling (HR-2021-1975-S) found that the turbines violate Sámi rights under Article 27 of the ICCPR. A negotiated settlement was reached in two stages: December 2023 (southern siidas/Storheia) and March 2024 (northern siidas/Roan). Statkraft will pay ~7 million NOK/year for 25 years and provide replacement grazing land; the Sámi secured a veto on any license extension past 2045. The turbines remain operational. Sámi negotiators and human rights advocates have noted the settlement did not remedy the underlying ICCPR violation. The case is documented at the Norwegian National Human Rights Institution (nhri.no).
Farmed salmon from Norway — sold under brands including Mowi, SalMar, and Cermaq — is fed with fish meal sourced partly from West African and South American fisheries, where local communities lose protein to the aquaculture supply chain. When you buy farmed Atlantic salmon, ask the retailer for the feed source disclosure. The ASC certification label does not currently require full feed chain transparency.
The Sámi Parliament (Sámediggi) exists in Norway, Sweden, and Finland. Each publishes position statements on mining, wind energy, and forestry in Sámi languages and English. Their websites (samediggi.no, sametinget.se, samediggi.fi) are the primary source for understanding what is at stake from the perspective of those who bear the cost. Read them before forming a view on Nordic extractive policy.

Sámi Council advocacy

Voluntary Sámi organization representing the Sámi people across Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Russia. Advocates for Indigenous land rights, self-determination, and protection of reindeer herding corridors against wind farm and mining encroachment.

Defends the reindeer herding corridors the briefing documents — where wind energy and critical mineral extraction (rare earths in Kiruna, Gállok iron ore) fragment seasonal migration routes that sustain Sámi pastoral livelihoods and cultural continuity.

Coalition Clean Baltic investigation

Network of environmental NGOs across the Baltic Sea region monitoring marine pollution, eutrophication, and fishery collapse. Tracks agricultural runoff, industrial discharge, and shipping emissions affecting Baltic herring and cod stocks.

Monitors the Baltic fishery collapse the briefing traces — where industrial agriculture runoff from intensive dairy in Denmark and Sweden creates dead zones that destroy the herring stocks coastal communities depend on, connecting land-based nutrient flows to marine ecosystem breakdown.

Earthworks — Critical Minerals investigation

Investigates environmental and social impacts of mineral extraction, including rare earth and iron ore mining in Scandinavia. Documents tailings contamination, water pollution, and impacts on Indigenous communities near extraction sites.

Tracks the critical mineral extraction the briefing identifies — LKAB's iron and rare earth operations in Kiruna, Boliden's copper and zinc smelting, and how these supply chains connect Nordic extraction to global EV battery and steel markets while displacing Sámi communities.

Forest Peoples Programme solidarity

Supports forest-dependent peoples' rights globally, including Sámi communities affected by industrial forestry in Finland and Sweden. Documents how monoculture pine plantations replace old-growth forest and destroy lichen pastures essential for reindeer.

Addresses the industrial forestry flow the briefing describes — where clear-cutting for pulp and biomass energy eliminates the ground lichen (Cladonia) that reindeer depend on for winter forage, forcing herders onto smaller ranges and intensifying conflicts with mining and wind energy.

Baltic Environmental Forum advocacy

Environmental policy organization working across Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania on chemicals management, biodiversity, and climate adaptation. Advocates for stronger regulation of industrial pollution entering Baltic waterways.

Works on the chemical and nutrient flows entering the Baltic the briefing documents — agricultural runoff, pharmaceutical contamination, and industrial discharge that accumulate in the semi-enclosed sea, connecting intensive Nordic-Baltic production systems to marine dead zones.

Swedish Society for Nature Conservation (Naturskyddsföreningen) advocacy

Sweden's largest environmental organization, campaigning against old-growth forest logging, mining expansion in Sápmi, and unsustainable aquaculture. Publishes investigations on Nordic corporate supply chains and environmental impacts.

Campaigns at the intersection of the briefing's forestry and mineral flows — opposing Beowulf Mining's Gállok iron ore project and SCA's industrial forestry in reindeer herding areas, naming the tension between Sweden's green transition ambitions and Indigenous land rights.
