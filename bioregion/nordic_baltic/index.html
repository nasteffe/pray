Lives Lived Here

In a village in Norrbotten, northern Sweden, a Sámi reindeer herder marks calves in early June. Her family's siida — the cooperative herding unit — manages 3,000 animals across a territory that crosses into Norway, following routes older than the border. This year the calving grounds are quieter: LKAB's Kiruna expansion has pushed the fence line closer, and the vibration from blasting disturbs the does. She sells reindeer meat to a cooperative in Jokkmokk that ships vacuum-packed cuts to restaurants in Stockholm, where a plate of suovas costs more than she earns for the whole carcass. The iron ore beneath her pasture travels by rail to German steelmakers who supply European car factories building electric vehicles. The green transition needs her land more than it needs her.

In Jutland, a Danish pig farmer hoses down a barn holding 2,000 sows. Denmark has more pigs than people — 30 million animals producing nitrogen that washes through the sandy soil into streams that feed the Baltic. His operation is efficient by every metric the EU tracks except the one the Baltic measures: oxygen. The dead zone off the Danish coast grows each summer. His pork goes to Germany, China, Japan. He has never seen the seabed he is helping to kill.

In Klaipėda, Lithuania, a dockworker loads grain onto a bulk carrier. The wheat came from smallholdings in Utena and Vilnius districts — farms of less than 20 hectares where the farmer earns less per kilogram than the trader who buys it. His wife works in a care home in Malmö, two weeks on, two weeks home. The remittance she sends back is worth more than his monthly wage at the port. Their daughter studies nursing in Vilnius and plans to follow her mother to Sweden. The village they live in has lost a quarter of its working-age population since Lithuania joined the EU.

What Presses

The weight on Nordic-Baltic

Sápmi, northern Scandinavia — In Jokkmokk, Swedish Lapland, a Sámi herder named Lars-Anders drives his snowmobile along a fenceline that was not there five years ago. On the other side, LKAB is drilling test cores for iron ore. His reindeer cannot cross. The calving grounds that his grandmother used are now inside a prospecting concession. He has filed an objection with the Mining Inspectorate; the Inspectorate has approved the permit anyway. His siida — eight families, 4,000 reindeer — will have to find new winter pasture or reduce the herd. Reducing the herd means one or two families stop herding. Stopping herding means the knowledge stops. His daughter is in university in Umeå. She wants to come back to the herd. He is not sure there will be a herd to come back to.

Ida-Virumaa, northeastern Estonia — In Kohtla-Järve, a town built for the oil shale industry, the air smells of sulphur on still days. The Eesti Energia power plants burn oil shale for 80 percent of Estonia's electricity — the most carbon-intensive energy source in the EU. The workers are mostly Russian-speaking Estonians whose families came in the Soviet era to work the mines. The EU's emissions trading system is making the plants uneconomic. When the plants close, the town has nothing else. The workers know this. The transition plan promises retraining and green jobs. The workers have heard transition plans before.

Western Jutland, Denmark — A pig farmer in Ringkøbing-Skjern municipality manages 5,000 sows. His operation is modern, efficient, and compliant with every regulation Denmark applies. The nitrogen from his fields enters the Skjern River system and reaches the North Sea and, via currents, the Baltic. He is aware of this — the inspectors come, the targets are discussed, the HELCOM reports are cited. He cannot reduce his herd and service his debt. The debt is structured around the herd size. The subsidy is structured around the herd size. The dead zone is structured around the herd size. He did not design the system. He cannot afford to leave it.

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global holds 1.3 trillion euros from North Sea oil and gas revenues — fossil fuels whose combustion is warming the Arctic three to four times faster than the global average, melting the permafrost and disrupting the reindeer herding that Sámi communities in northern Norway have practised for millennia. The fund has divested from some coal companies on ethical grounds. It has not divested from itself.
Sweden and Finland clearcut boreal forest — Europe's largest carbon sink and the Sámi's primary lichen grazing ecosystem — and certify the practice as sustainable under FSC standards that measure replanting, not biodiversity. Stora Enso, UPM, and SCA control 70 percent of Swedish pulp capacity. Old-growth forest is down to 5 percent of its historical extent. The paper in your hand may be part of this.
Danish pig farming produces 30 million animals a year — more pigs than people — whose nitrogen runoff feeds the Baltic Sea's dead zones. The pork goes to Germany, China, and Japan. The dead seabed stays. No country around the Baltic is meeting its Helsinki Commission nitrogen reduction targets. The sea cannot flush itself fast enough to recover.

Six resource streams flow from this region — iron ore, crude oil, natural gas, timber, farmed salmon, nickel — and the wealth they create is among the most equitably distributed on earth, within these borders. The question this briefing asks is not whether Nordic societies care. It is who pays for the caring. The salmon feed comes from West African fisheries. The pension fund returns come from fossil fuel combustion elsewhere. The green transition minerals come from Sámi land. The eldercare comes from Baltic women whose own children grow up with a parent in another country. The Nordic model works. The question is for whom, and at whose expense. timber, pulp, paper, crude oil, natural gas, farmed Atlantic salmon leaving.

The difficulty of praying for this region is that we are not praying from outside it. The pension fund that warmed the Arctic holds shares in companies we own. The salmon on the plate was fed with fish meal taken from someone else's coast. The iron in the car was mined from under a reindeer calving ground. The carer who looked after grandmother last winter left her own children in Kaunas. To pray for the Sámi herder and the Baltic dockworker is to pray about our own participation in what presses on them. This is not comfortable. It is not meant to be.

Seeds of Hope

In 2020, the Swedish Supreme Court ruled in the Girjas case that the Girjas Sámi village holds exclusive hunting and fishing rights over its traditional territory — the first time a Swedish court recognised Sámi land rights against the state. The ruling has not stopped mining permits, but it established a legal precedent that every subsequent case must reckon with.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global (NBIM, nbim.no) publishes its full portfolio. You can search it by company name. If your own pension fund invests in the same companies — and most European and North American funds do — you are a co-beneficiary of the same extraction chain. The fund's ethical exclusion list is also public. Ask your fund manager: do you follow the same exclusions, or different ones?
The Fosen wind farm Supreme Court ruling (HR-2021-1975-S) found that the turbines violate Sámi rights under Article 27 of the ICCPR. As of early 2026, the ruling has not been enforced — the turbines still operate. The case is documented at the Norwegian National Human Rights Institution (nhri.no). Amnesty International and the UN Special Rapporteur on Indigenous Peoples have called for compliance. The Norwegian government has not complied.
Farmed salmon from Norway — sold under brands including Mowi, SalMar, and Cermaq — is fed with fish meal sourced partly from West African and South American fisheries, where local communities lose protein to the aquaculture supply chain. When you buy farmed Atlantic salmon, ask the retailer for the feed source disclosure. The ASC certification label does not currently require full feed chain transparency.
The Sámi Parliament (Sámediggi) exists in Norway, Sweden, and Finland. Each publishes position statements on mining, wind energy, and forestry in Sámi languages and English. Their websites (samediggi.no, sametinget.se, samediggi.fi) are the primary source for understanding what is at stake from the perspective of those who bear the cost. Read them before forming a view on Nordic extractive policy.
