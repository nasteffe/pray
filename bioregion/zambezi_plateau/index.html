Lives Lived Here

In Mashonaland West, a Zimbabwean farmer walks his tobacco field in the early morning, checking leaves for ripeness. He grows tobacco on contract for British American Tobacco — the company provides seed and fertiliser on credit, sets the price at harvest, and deducts the debt before paying. His children help with the curing: they spend July and August tending the barn fires, missing school. He knows tobacco exhausts the soil faster than maize, but maize does not pay the school fees that tobacco — after the debt is subtracted — barely covers. His wife keeps a kitchen garden behind the house where she grows rape, tomatoes, and groundnuts. The garden feeds the family. The tobacco field feeds the contract.

For Cabo Delgado…

For the more than a million people driven from their homes in northern Mozambique, where an insurgency burns beside one of the world's largest gas discoveries. For those who fled Palma and the villages around it — that the wealth of the gas reaches them, rather than passing over their heads to the sea.

For the cyclone coast…

For the communities of Mozambique, Malawi, and Madagascar struck again and again by strengthening storms — Idai, Freddy, and the ones still to come — rebuilding in mud and thatch each time; and for Madagascar's deep south, where drought has brought famine to people who did least to warm the world.

For those who dig the plateau's wealth…

For the Copperbelt miners of Zambia, the ruby diggers of Montepuez, the diamond miners of Marange — the mineral riches of this land flowing out through the hands of people paid two to four dollars a day, and sometimes killed when they stand between a company and a concession.

For the dam-displaced…

For the Gwembe Valley Tonga, moved off their land two generations ago for the Kariba dam and never made whole; and for all the communities of this plateau displaced so that others might have power and light.

For Zimbabwe…

For the people of Zimbabwe enduring the long unravelling of their economy — nurses and teachers whose wages vanish to inflation, households held together by money sent home from the diaspora. For honest government, and for daily bread.

For those who carry relief…

For the church networks, the women's savings groups, the Malawian and Mozambican volunteers who carry cholera treatment and cyclone relief down washed-out roads. That their labour of love is seen, and shared.

What to bring with you from this briefing

Gemfields plc, listed on the London Stock Exchange, controls approximately 50% of the world's ruby production (up to 80% for premium stones) from Montepuez, Cabo Delgado — the same province where over a million people have been displaced by insurgency. Gemfields has faced lawsuits from community members alleging forced evictions, beatings, and killings by mine security. Court filings and community testimonies are documented by the Business and Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org), searchable under "Gemfields." The rubies appear in jewellery sold in London, New York, and Hong Kong. Ask where the stone was mined.
The tobacco in a cigarette sold in London or Tokyo may have been grown by a child in Malawi or Zimbabwe. An estimated 10-15% of the tobacco workforce in both countries is under 15. Children tend curing barns through the night and handle green leaves that deliver nicotine through the skin — a condition called Green Tobacco Sickness. British American Tobacco and Philip Morris source from both countries under contract farming arrangements. The Tobacco Atlas (tobaccoatlas.org) tracks the supply chain. If you hold shares in a tobacco company or a fund that includes one, this is what the dividend pays for.
Lake Malawi's chambo fishery collapse is not a natural disaster — it is a governance failure. Mesh-size regulations exist but are not enforced. Breeding-season closures are declared but not observed. The fish that fed millions of people are being caught as juveniles, before they can reproduce, because the lakeshore communities have no other protein source and no authority enforces the rules that would let the stock recover. WorldFish and the Malawi Department of Fisheries have published the data. The solution is known. What is missing is the political will to impose short-term pain on fishers for long-term survival — and the food aid that would sustain families through a genuine closure. This is a place where targeted giving (e.g., through WFP Malawi or Ripple Africa's fish conservation programme) could change an outcome.
