Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the Ashanti cocoa belt, a farming family begins the day's harvest. The woman breaks the pods; her children — some of them too young for the machete they carry — scoop the beans onto banana leaves to ferment. In five days the beans will be dry. A pisteur on a motorbike will weigh them against a price set by COCOBOD in Accra, which is itself responding to futures contracts set on the London and New York exchanges. The farmer's share of a chocolate bar's retail price is less than six percent. Her cocoa travels by truck to Tema port, by container ship to Amsterdam or Hamburg, by lorry to a Mondelez or Nestlé factory. Six months from now, it will be a wrapped bar on a shelf in a shop where no one has heard of Haut-Sassandra.

At the fish landing site in Joal, south of Dakar, a woman named a transformatrice lays out sardinella on wire racks above a smoking pit. She has done this since her mother taught her, and her mother before that. The smoked fish — guédj, kétiakh — travels by bush taxi to Bamako, to Ouagadougou, feeding families a thousand kilometres from the coast who depend on this protein because their own herds are shrinking and the rains are failing. But sardinella per-capita availability has declined approximately 44% since 2009 (from 16 kg/year to 9 kg/year in Senegal [FAO]), and a growing share of what the pirogues bring in is now diverted to fishmeal factories — ground into feed for European aquaculture and Chinese livestock, worth more per tonne as animal feed than as human food. The transformatrice works longer hours for less fish. The families in Mali pay more for less protein. The fishmeal ship sails for Europe.

In the Birim River valley of eastern Ghana, a cocoa farmer watches the water run brown. Upstream, galamsey miners have dug pits in the riverbed with Chinese-imported excavators, washing gold with mercury and cyanide. The Birim feeds the Densu, which feeds the Weija Reservoir, which supplies Accra's drinking water. The gold travels to Switzerland for refining; the mercury settles in the sediment, enters the food chain, accumulates in the fish that riverside communities eat for their only protein. The water treatment plant was not built for this. The city drinks what it can filter. The farmer drinks what arrives.

Ghana and Ivory Coast together produce more cocoa than the rest of the world combined, and the chocolate industry generates over $130 billion a year from their harvest. The farmers who grow it earn less than six percent of the retail price — below the extreme poverty line. An estimated 1.56 million children work in hazardous cocoa labour across the two countries, not because their parents are indifferent but because the price of cocoa does not cover the cost of hired help and school fees at the same time. The Living Income Differential — a $400-per-tonne premium Ghana and Ivory Coast negotiated in 2019 — was partially circumvented by chocolate companies reducing quality premiums by the same amount. The industry absorbed the reform without changing what the farmer earns.
Senegal's artisanal fishery feeds seventy percent of the country's animal protein and supplies landlocked neighbours whose own food systems are failing. The EU pays the Senegalese government for industrial fishing access through the Fisheries Partnership Agreement, but the payment structure does not compensate the Lebou and Serer communities whose ancestral fishing grounds are depleted. The same fish that once fed families in Saint-Louis and Bamako is now ground into fishmeal for European salmon farms — worth more per tonne as animal feed than as human food. The protein leaves Africa to fatten fish that Europeans eat.
In Ghana, artisanal gold mining (galamsey) provides income for an estimated million people locked out of formal employment — and poisons the rivers that supply drinking water to millions more. The Pra, Ankobra, and Birim rivers carry mercury and cyanide from mining operations to downstream communities, cocoa farms, and city water treatment plants. The gold is refined in Switzerland; the mercury accumulates in river sediment and in the bodies of children who eat fish from contaminated water. Ghana's government has banned galamsey repeatedly; each ban fails because it criminalises the miners without offering alternative livelihoods. The excavators are Chinese-imported; the mercury is Chinese-supplied; the refined gold is Swiss-exported. The contamination is Ghanaian.

Cascade Chains

Cocoa expansion destroys Upper Guinea forest → Rainfall declines as canopy disappears → Cocoa yields fall on deforested land → Farmers clear more forest to maintain income → The crop consumes its own growing conditions (GHA, CIV — 80%+ forest cover lost; cocoa farmers averaging ~$0.60/person/day [Imani Center 2024] cannot afford to replant shade trees that take 5-10 years to mature; 2024-25 farmgate price increases of 45-58% improved nominal income but most remain below $2/day living income threshold)

EU and Chinese trawlers deplete sardinella stocks → Artisanal catch declines (~44% per-capita availability decline 2009-2018, FAO) → Women fish processors lose raw material → Smoked fish supply to landlocked Mali and Burkina Faso falls → Protein deficit compounds Sahel food crisis already driven by drought and conflict (SEN — 200,000 artisanal fishers; fishmeal factories divert food fish to European aquaculture feed)

Galamsey mining contaminates rivers with mercury → Downstream cocoa farms lose clean water → Water treatment plants for Kumasi and Accra cannot filter contamination → Urban drinking water quality degrades → Health costs rise for communities that did not choose the mine (GHA — Pra, Ankobra, Birim rivers; gold refined in Switzerland; mercury stays in sediment for decades)

Who Sustains This

Cargill Cocoa (Côte d'Ivoire processing) · Abidjan grinding and processing facility (world's largest single cocoa processing plant); Direct cocoa procurement from Ivorian and Ghanaian cooperatives (60%+ of global supply); Downstream supply contracts to Mars, Mondelēz, Nestlé, Ferrero, Hershey

Driven by: We must trace, certify, and improve the supply chain we depend on

The trap: The certification regime works on the farm; the price the farm receives is set upstream of the certification, where the demand the certification serves is concentrated. A processor's traceability programme can prove that a particular kilogram of cocoa was not grown on cleared forest by a child, without altering the demand structure that pushes farmers toward clearing forest and using child labour to remain solvent at the price offered. The structural conditions cocoa sustainability programmes address are reproduced by the demand they presuppose.

Newmont Corporation (Ghana operations) · Ahafo mine (production since 2006; ~600K oz/year); Akyem mine (production since 2013; ~430K oz/year); Global gold price arbitrage as world's largest gold producer post-Newcrest acquisition

Driven by: We must mine to the highest standards and earn the trust of the communities we operate in

The trap: The trust framework addresses outcomes; the continued mining recreates the conditions the framework is meant to ameliorate. Each Community Development Fund payment, each ICMM-standards audit, each tailings incident response operates within an operating relationship where the mine is what is being trusted, the community is what is being asked to trust, and the trust is renewed in proportion to the gold removed. The longer the trust framework operates, the more gold has been removed under it; the more gold has been removed, the more the framework is needed.

Ghana Cocoa Board (COCOBOD) · Sole legal channel for Ghana cocoa exports (~800K tonnes/year, world's #2 producer); Syndicated annual cocoa-financing facility (~$1.5–2B, drawn from international banks); IMF Extended Credit Facility envelope (2023, conditioned on COCOBOD operational reform)

Driven by: We must protect the cocoa farmer and the cocoa export base

The trap: The intermediary that is supposed to protect the farmer from the global market is bound to that market by debt. COCOBOD's foreign-currency obligations to international banks set a floor under what it must extract from the cocoa chain to remain solvent; the same chain whose pricing power it is meant to exercise on the farmer's behalf is the chain whose pricing power its creditors require it to manage. The Living Income Differential was the most ambitious assertion of producer sovereignty in a generation; its operation depends on the cooperation of the buyers whose pricing the assertion is meant to constrain.

Eight resource streams flow out of the Guinea Coast — cocoa, gold, fish, rubber, groundnuts, phosphate, timber, diamonds — worth tens of billions at their destination markets. What does not come back is the forest, cleared for cocoa and never replanted; the fish, taken by trawlers and ground into meal for European livestock; the water, poisoned by mercury and undrinkable downstream. The women who do most of the work own least of the land. The children who harvest the cocoa have never tasted the chocolate. 6.6M food insecure (current); 22,672 displaced (total); cocoa beans, gold (industrial and artisanal/galamsey), marine fish (sardinella, anchovy, tuna) leaving.

The cocoa tree takes five years to bear fruit. The woman who tends it earns less than the cost of the chocolate her labour produces. She knows this. She plants anyway, because the tree is what she has. The contradiction is not hers to resolve.

Looking Ahead

  • The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), if implemented as scheduled, will require plot-level traceability for cocoa imports — a standard that two million smallholder farms in Ghana and Ivory Coast are not equipped to meet without investment that the chocolate industry has not yet committed.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The chocolate you buy likely contains cocoa from Ghana or Ivory Coast, where an estimated 1.56 million children work in hazardous labour — carrying heavy loads, using machetes, applying pesticides. The ILO/NORC 2020 survey (publicly available) documented that child labour in cocoa increased by 14% between 2008 and 2019 despite industry pledges to eliminate it. Every major chocolate company has signed the Harkin-Engel Protocol (2001) and its successor commitments. Ask your preferred chocolate brand: what percentage of your cocoa is traceable to the farm, and what is the farmer's income? Tony's Chocolonely, Divine Chocolate (partly farmer-owned via Kuapa Kokoo), and Fairtrade-certified bars are available alternatives — not perfect, but structurally different.
The fish in your salmon's feed may come from West African waters. Sardinella and other small pelagics from Senegal and Mauritania are increasingly processed into fishmeal for European and Asian aquaculture rather than feeding West African families. The Environmental Justice Foundation (EJF) has documented how EU fisheries agreements and IUU fishing are collapsing stocks that 200,000 Senegalese artisanal fishers depend on. Changing Markets Foundation's 'Fishing the Feed' reports are publicly available. If you eat farmed salmon, ask your retailer where the feed comes from.
Gold refined in Switzerland may originate from Ghanaian galamsey operations that have contaminated rivers serving millions of people. The Swiss refineries (Metalor, Valcambi, PAMP) process gold from multiple sources, and traceability from artisanal operations is poor. The mercury used in small-scale mining enters rivers, accumulates in fish, and is consumed by children downstream. If you buy gold jewellery, ask whether the refiner participates in the London Bullion Market Association's responsible sourcing programme — and know that even LBMA standards struggle with artisanal supply chains.

Environmental Justice Foundation investigation

Investigates illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing by EU and Chinese industrial fleets off the West African coast. Documents the collapse of artisanal fish stocks and the impact on coastal communities in Senegal, Ghana, and Guinea.

Mighty Earth investigation

Monitors cocoa supply chains from Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire, documenting deforestation of Upper Guinea forest for cocoa expansion and the failure of industry sustainability pledges to halt child labour and forest loss.

Tracks the self-consuming cocoa cycle the briefing describes — where expansion destroys the Upper Guinea forest whose rainfall feeds cocoa farming — and the $130B chocolate industry's extraction from farmers earning $0.50-1.50/day.

WaterAid Ghana solidarity

Works with communities in galamsey-affected areas of Ghana to provide clean water access where artisanal gold mining has contaminated river systems with mercury and heavy metals.

Serves communities downstream of the galamsey mining the briefing identifies — where mercury from gold washing enters the same river systems that supply drinking water, connecting artisanal mining economics to public health at the watershed level.

WACAM (Wassa Association of Communities Affected by Mining) advocacy

Ghanaian community organization advocating for the rights of mining-affected communities, campaigning against illegal galamsey operations and the environmental damage of both artisanal and industrial gold mining.

Represents the communities at the intersection of gold mining and river contamination the briefing documents — where galamsey mercury pollution and industrial mining waste converge in Ghana's Pra, Birim, and Ankobra river basins.

International Cocoa Initiative advocacy

Multi-stakeholder organization working to end child labour and forced labour in cocoa production in Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire, monitoring supply chains and supporting community-level child protection systems.

Addresses the child labour embedded in the cocoa monoculture the briefing describes — the human cost at the base of a supply chain where Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire produce 60%+ of global cocoa under conditions of structural poverty and labour exploitation.
