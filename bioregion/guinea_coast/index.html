In the Haut-Sassandra region of western Ivory Coast, before the sun is fully up, a woman is already on the cocoa farm. She has been doing this since she was a girl — breaking pods, scooping the wet white pulp into heaps for fermentation, carrying the dried beans to the pisteur who arrives on a motorbike with a scale that she cannot read. She earns less than a dollar fifty on the days she works.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the Ashanti cocoa belt, a farming family begins the day's harvest. The woman breaks the pods; her children — some of them too young for the machete they carry — scoop the beans onto banana leaves to ferment. In five days the beans will be dry. A pisteur on a motorbike will weigh them against a price set by COCOBOD in Accra, which is itself responding to futures contracts set on the London and New York exchanges. The farmer's share of a chocolate bar's retail price is less than six percent. Her cocoa travels by truck to Tema port, by container ship to Amsterdam or Hamburg, by lorry to a Mondelez or Nestlé factory. Six months from now, it will be a wrapped bar on a shelf in a shop where no one has heard of Haut-Sassandra.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Environmental Justice Foundation investigation

Investigates illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing by EU and Chinese industrial fleets off the West African coast. Documents the collapse of artisanal fish stocks and the impact on coastal communities in Senegal, Ghana, and Guinea.

Directly investigates the industrial overfishing the briefing identifies — EU and Chinese fleets depleting the Guinea Current upwelling that sustains artisanal fishers providing 70% of Senegal's animal protein, while fishing agreements pay the state but not the Lebou and Serer communities.

Mighty Earth investigation

Monitors cocoa supply chains from Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire, documenting deforestation of Upper Guinea forest for cocoa expansion and the failure of industry sustainability pledges to halt child labour and forest loss.

Tracks the self-consuming cocoa cycle the briefing describes — where expansion destroys the Upper Guinea forest whose rainfall feeds cocoa farming — and the $130B chocolate industry's extraction from farmers earning $0.50-1.50/day.

WaterAid Ghana solidarity

Works with communities in galamsey-affected areas of Ghana to provide clean water access where artisanal gold mining has contaminated river systems with mercury and heavy metals.

Serves communities downstream of the galamsey mining the briefing identifies — where mercury from gold washing enters the same river systems that supply drinking water, connecting artisanal mining economics to public health at the watershed level.

WACAM (Wassa Association of Communities Affected by Mining) advocacy

Ghanaian community organization advocating for the rights of mining-affected communities, campaigning against illegal galamsey operations and the environmental damage of both artisanal and industrial gold mining.

Represents the communities at the intersection of gold mining and river contamination the briefing documents — where galamsey mercury pollution and industrial mining waste converge in Ghana's Pra, Birim, and Ankobra river basins.

International Cocoa Initiative advocacy

Multi-stakeholder organization working to end child labour and forced labour in cocoa production in Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire, monitoring supply chains and supporting community-level child protection systems.

Addresses the child labour embedded in the cocoa monoculture the briefing describes — the human cost at the base of a supply chain where Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire produce 60%+ of global cocoa under conditions of structural poverty and labour exploitation.
