The weight on Gulf Coast–Petrochemical Corridor

From Gulf Coast–Petrochemical Corridor, shale oil and natural gas, avocados, migrant labor flow outward — the cost borne by Appalachian communities (water contamination, methane emissions), Indigenous communities (pipeline routes through treaty lands), farming communities under cartel extortion.

Cancer Alley is not an accident of geography. Between Baton Rouge and New Orleans, petrochemical plants were sited in formerly enslaved communities along the River Road — the same plantation corridor where Black families had lived since Emancipation. The zoning decisions that placed 200 chemical facilities in majority-Black parishes were made in rooms those communities were not invited into. Today, residents of St. James Parish breathe chloroprene at levels 50 times the national cancer risk benchmark, and the EPA's own risk assessments confirm what the death certificates have been saying for decades.
The Mississippi River levee system protects cities and refineries but starves the delta of the sediment that built it. The Army Corps of Engineers maintains 3,500 miles of levees for navigation and flood control — which keeps barges moving and refineries dry — but the same containment prevents the river from depositing the 200 million tons of sediment per year that once replenished the coastal marshes. The marshes die, the storm buffer shrinks, and the next hurricane hits harder. The infrastructure that protects commerce destroys the land that protects everyone.
The Gulf of Mexico dead zone and the Midwest corn belt are the same system. Fertiliser applied in Iowa runs off into the Maumee, the Ohio, the Missouri, and the Mississippi, arriving in the Gulf as nitrogen and phosphorus that feed algal blooms and suffocate everything beneath them. The shrimp fishers of Louisiana and the corn farmers of Iowa have never met, but they share a river, and what one puts in the other takes out.
