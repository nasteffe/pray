Drive west from Kansas City and the land flattens until the sky is the only horizon. Somewhere under your wheels, six hundred feet down, the Ogallala Aquifer holds water that fell as rain when mammoths walked this grass. For sixty years, centre-pivot irrigation has drawn that water up through wells to grow corn — corn for cattle feed, corn for ethanol, corn for high-fructose syrup, corn for export.

Lives Lived Here

Two hundred miles north, outside Ogallala, Nebraska — the town that gave the aquifer its name — a fourth-generation farmer watches the well monitor on his phone. The static water level has dropped eleven feet since his father started irrigating in the 1970s. He grows corn because the USDA subsidy structure and the ethanol mandate make corn the only crop that pencils out. He knows the aquifer is finite. He also knows that switching to dryland wheat would halve his income and make the bank note on his equipment unpayable. He plants corn again.

For the gifts of this place…

For the gifts of this open country — for the last of the tallgrass prairie, for soil that feeds much of the world, for the great rivers and the vast quiet of the plains. That plenty may not become forgetfulness.

For what our comfort costs…

For the abundance grown here and sent everywhere — and for the ancient Ogallala water pumped dry to grow it, a gift from the deep past spent in a single lifetime. Teach us to trace the loaf and the steak back to the aquifer and the hands that raised them.

For those who do the killing-floor work…

For the immigrant and refugee workers who stand the meatpacking lines in the small towns of the plains — Sudanese, Guatemalan, Burmese — doing the fast, cold, dangerous work the country would rather not see, and holding those towns together. For their safety and their dignity.

For the reservations…

For the Lakota of Pine Ridge and Rosebud and the other nations of this land, whose reservations hold some of the deepest poverty in the country amid all this plenty. For treaties kept, for the young who are choosing to stay, and for what was promised the seventh generation.

For holding both…

For honesty about a breadbasket that does not feed all its own; and for love that reaches from the full table toward the empty one down the road.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The beef you buy was very likely processed in a Great Plains meatpacking plant — Tyson, JBS, Cargill, and National Beef control over 80% of the US market. The workers who cut it are overwhelmingly immigrants working at line speeds that produce the highest injury rates in American manufacturing. The USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service sets line speed regulations; the Government Accountability Office has documented the gap between permitted speeds and worker safety. Your congressional representative votes on the Farm Bill that governs both the subsidy structure and the regulatory framework. The next Farm Bill reauthorisation is a concrete legislative moment.
The Gulf of Mexico dead zone is a direct consequence of corn belt fertiliser use — 1.7 million tonnes of nitrogen carried downstream annually. The EPA's Mississippi River/Gulf of Mexico Hypoxia Task Force has set voluntary nutrient reduction targets since 2001; twenty-five years later, the dead zone has not shrunk. The task force's reports are public. Louisiana's shrimp industry has lost an estimated 40% of its economic value over two decades. If you eat corn-fed beef or use ethanol fuel, the dead zone is downstream of your consumption.
The InterTribal Buffalo Council (itbc.com) coordinates bison restoration across 82 tribes and 20,000+ animals. Their work restores prairie ecology, rebuilds tribal food sovereignty, and demonstrates an alternative relationship to the land that industrial agriculture replaced. They accept support and publish annual reports on herd health and grassland recovery. Pine Ridge's Tanka Bar — bison meat and cranberry, produced on the reservation — is available for purchase and represents one of the few reservation-based food enterprises reaching a national market.
