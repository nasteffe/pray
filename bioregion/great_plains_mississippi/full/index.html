Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Garden City, Kansas, a Guatemalan woman drives to the Tyson meatpacking plant where she has worked the kill floor for three years. Her hands are numb most mornings — carpal tunnel from the repetitive cutting at line speeds that OSHA has tried and failed to regulate downward. She earns ~$20-21 an hour (average pay at Tyson/JBS plants as of 2025, up 46% since 2019 after strikes and wage negotiations). The beef she processes will sell for $14 a pound in a Brooklyn grocery store. She sends $400 a month to her mother in Huehuetenango, minus the 6% Western Union fee. The town needs her and resents her in equal measure.

Two hundred miles north, outside Ogallala, Nebraska — the town that gave the aquifer its name — a fourth-generation farmer watches the well monitor on his phone. The static water level has dropped eleven feet since his father started irrigating in the 1970s. He grows corn because the USDA subsidy structure and the ethanol mandate make corn the only crop that pencils out. He knows the aquifer is finite. He also knows that switching to dryland wheat would halve his income and make the bank note on his equipment unpayable. He plants corn again.

On Pine Ridge Reservation, an Oglala Lakota grandmother drives forty-five minutes to the nearest grocery store in Chadron, Nebraska. The store on the reservation closed years ago. Her grandchildren eat commodity food — government-surplus cheese, canned meat, white flour — because fresh produce does not survive the supply chain to a place the supply chain was not built to reach. The buffalo that once fed her people by the millions are returning in small herds managed by the tribe and by the InterTribal Buffalo Council, grazing land that has not seen a plough. The herd is 800 head. It is not enough to feed the reservation. It is enough to remember what the grass was for.

What Presses

The weight on Great Plains–Mississippi

Pine Ridge Reservation, South Dakota — The Oglala Lakota live on 3,500 square miles of land that was never ceded — it was guaranteed by the Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 and then taken anyway. Life expectancy here is 64 for men, 67 for women — twenty years below the national average. The nearest full-service hospital is in Rapid City, an hour and a half by car if you have a car. The Indian Health Service clinic on the reservation is chronically underfunded, chronically understaffed, and the subject of a 2024 Government Accountability Office report documenting wait times of weeks for urgent care. Adolescent suicide rates are five times the national average. The Pine Ridge grocery store closed; the nearest supermarket is in Chadron, Nebraska, 45 minutes away. Children grow up on commodity food — canned, processed, designed for shelf life, not for growing bodies. Diabetes prevalence is eight times the national average. This is not a developing country. This is the wealthiest nation on earth, ninety minutes from Mount Rushmore, where four million tourists a year photograph presidential faces carved into a mountain the Lakota call the Six Grandfathers.

Garden City, Kansas — The Tyson and National Beef plants here employ over 5,000 workers between them, the majority immigrants from Guatemala, Somalia, Myanmar, and Mexico. Line speeds run at 250 to 300 head per hour. Workers make the same cutting motion thousands of times per shift. Carpal tunnel surgery is so common that local clinics run dedicated programmes. Workers who report injuries risk losing shifts; undocumented workers who report injuries risk losing everything. During the 2020 COVID-19 outbreak, the Garden City plant became one of the deadliest workplaces in America — the governor declared it essential, the workers kept cutting, and the virus moved through the floor because the line does not stop. The beef they process reaches consumers who will never learn the name of the town where it was cut.

Western Kansas and the Texas Panhandle — Drive through Sublette or Satanta or Hugoton and you will see the circles shrinking. Centre-pivot irrigators that once reached a quarter-mile radius now reach less as the well pressure drops. Farmers who irrigated 1,000 acres in the 1990s irrigate 600 today. Their sons have moved to Wichita or Denver. The school in Sublette consolidated with the next town over. The hospital in Satanta closed. The aquifer level drops another foot each year, and each foot represents water that fell as rain before the last ice age. Kansas State University researchers estimate that at current rates, 70% of groundwater-irrigated farmland in southwest Kansas will have insufficient water for irrigation by 2060. The farmers know this. They pump anyway, because the bank note comes due this year and the aquifer does not send invoices.

The Ogallala Aquifer underlies eight states and supports $20 billion in annual agricultural production. It is fossil water — rain that fell during the Pleistocene, held in porous sandstone, recharging at less than one inch per year in most areas while wells draw it down by one to three feet annually. In the Texas Panhandle and western Kansas, the aquifer has lost more than 30% of its predevelopment volume. When it is gone, the land returns to dryland farming or abandonment. There is no replacement, no alternative source, no infrastructure project that can substitute for what took geological time to create.
The Mississippi dead zone is the downstream signature of the Great Plains agricultural model. Every bushel of corn requires nitrogen; the nitrogen that does not enter the plant enters the water. The Missouri and Mississippi carry 1.7 million tonnes of nitrogen to the Gulf each year, feeding an algal bloom that consumes all oxygen below the surface. The dead zone reached 6,334 square miles in 2021 — larger than Connecticut. Louisiana shrimpers who once worked those waters have seen their catch and their income decline for twenty years. The person who eats the corn never sees the dead shrimp.
Meatpacking plants in Garden City, Dodge City, Liberal, Lexington, and Storm Lake are staffed overwhelmingly by immigrant workers — Guatemalan, Somali, Burmese, Mexican — doing work at line speeds that produce repetitive stress injuries, amputations, and chemical exposures at rates that dwarf any other American industry. During COVID-19, these plants became the deadliest workplaces in the country while being declared critical infrastructure that could not close. The communities that depend on this labour force simultaneously benefit from it economically and vote for policies that criminalise it. The workers have no political voice proportional to their economic necessity.

Cascade Chains

Ogallala depletion -> irrigated farmland converts to dryland -> yields drop 40-60% -> farm debt becomes unpayable -> rural depopulation accelerates -> schools and hospitals close -> remaining communities lose services that made staying possible (KS, TX Panhandle, NE — USGS reports 30% depletion in southern sections since predevelopment; Kansas Geological Survey projects functional exhaustion in western Kansas within 25 years)

Industrial corn-soy monoculture -> nitrogen and phosphorus runoff -> Mississippi River carries 1.7 million tonnes of nitrogen annually -> Gulf of Mexico hypoxic zone reaches 6,000+ square miles -> shrimp fishery collapse -> Gulf Coast fishing communities lose livelihoods (NOAA 2024 dead zone measurement; EPA nutrient loading reports; Louisiana shrimpers reporting 40% income decline over two decades)

USDA subsidy structure rewards acreage over stewardship -> farmers locked into corn-soy rotation -> Ogallala pumping continues past sustainable yield -> soil carbon released by tillage -> prairie root systems that stored more carbon than the Amazon destroyed -> atmospheric carbon increases while soil fertility declines (Sanderman et al. 2017 estimate 133 Gt carbon released from 12,000 years of land conversion; Great Plains tallgrass prairie reduced to 4% of original extent)

The trap: Each regenerative-agriculture pilot, water-stewardship commitment, and farmer-support programme operates inside a procurement architecture whose price-setting power makes the scale and chemical intensity of corn-soy monoculture the only solvent farming model for the member growers Cargill buys from. The privately-held structure is itself structurally load-bearing: it lets Cargill claim stewardship leadership without the disclosure regime that would let the public weigh those claims against the throughput they ride on. The Ogallala is being mined to fund the same supply chain whose stewardship narrative would have the depletion reversed.

Tyson Foods (US vertical-integration poultry, beef, pork) · Vertical poultry operations — contract growers (~5,000 farms) raise birds Tyson owns from hatchery to processing; Beef packing (largest US beef processor by volume — Dakota City NE, Amarillo TX, Garden City KS, Lexington NE); Pork operations (post-2014 Hillshire acquisition — Storm Lake IA, Madison NE, Logansport IN); Branded value-added (Jimmy Dean, Hillshire Farm, Ball Park, Sara Lee Frozen Bakery)

The trap: The contract-grower model converts independent farmers into capital-trapped operators of company-specification chicken houses they cannot exit without bankruptcy. The 'family farmer in the heartland' the brand celebrates is the same operator whose mortgage and chicken-house debt are calibrated to Tyson's price-per-pound. The migrant workforce that runs the line at packing plants is the same workforce whose presence the political coalition Tyson's lobbying funds organises against. The affordable-protein narrative makes both contradictions structurally necessary and politically illegible.

Bayer Crop Science / Monsanto legacy (US operations) · Roundup (glyphosate) global revenue and Liberty/glufosinate complement; Genuity trait-stack seed sales (Roundup Ready, VT Double PRO, Bollgard) — US corn-soy-cotton dominance; Climate FieldView digital-agriculture platform subscriptions; Dicamba (XtendiMax, RoundupReady 2 Xtend) and 2,4-D (Enlist) chemistry sales

Driven by: We must innovate to feed the world sustainably through better seeds and crop protection

The trap: Each new trait stack and digital-agriculture platform deepens the input-debt dependency of the farms it claims to liberate from yield risk. The Roundup litigation reserves carry the externality the seed-chemical economy could not internalise; Carbon Initiative payments operate inside the same farm-debt structure that requires continued chemical-rotational throughput to service input loans. The 'feed the world' framing presupposes the demand structure that produces the Gulf dead zone, the dicamba-drift conflicts between neighbouring farms, and the herbicide-resistance arms race the chemistry is supposed to resolve.

Two resource streams flow out of the Great Plains — fossil fuel and industrial grain — worth hundreds of billions at their destination. What does not come back is the water, drawn from an aquifer that recharges at a fraction of the rate it is pumped; the topsoil, washed into the Mississippi at rates that exceed formation by a factor of ten; and the health of the workers whose bodies are the last unautomated step in the meatpacking line. 7 killed (3 months); shale oil and natural gas, corn, soybeans, and dairy products leaving; precipitation 60% of normal; 173 fires (7 days).

The Ogallala Aquifer is ancient rain held in stone. For millions of years it simply waited, and the grass above it grew and died and grew again in a rhythm that needed nothing from anyone. The rhythm now is ours — planting and pumping and harvesting on a schedule that treats a million years of accumulation as a single season's input. The water does not refuse. It simply goes. What remains when it is gone is the question the prairie has always known how to answer, if anyone is still there to ask it.

Seeds of Hope

On the Cheyenne River and Pine Ridge reservations, the InterTribal Buffalo Council coordinates the return of bison to tribal lands — over 20,000 animals across 86 member tribes (as of 2025), grazing prairie that industrial agriculture never reached. The buffalo do what no government programme has managed. They restore the grass. They aerate the soil. They bring back the prairie dog towns and the black-footed ferrets and the burrowing owls.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The beef you buy was very likely processed in a Great Plains meatpacking plant — Tyson, JBS, Cargill, and National Beef control over 80% of the US market. The workers who cut it are overwhelmingly immigrants working at line speeds that produce the highest injury rates in American manufacturing. The USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service sets line speed regulations; the Government Accountability Office has documented the gap between permitted speeds and worker safety. Your congressional representative votes on the Farm Bill that governs both the subsidy structure and the regulatory framework. The next Farm Bill reauthorisation is a concrete legislative moment.
The Gulf of Mexico dead zone is a direct consequence of corn belt fertiliser use — 1.7 million tonnes of nitrogen carried downstream annually. The EPA's Mississippi River/Gulf of Mexico Hypoxia Task Force has set voluntary nutrient reduction targets since 2001; twenty-five years later, the dead zone has not shrunk. The task force's reports are public. Louisiana's shrimp industry has lost an estimated 40% of its economic value over two decades. If you eat corn-fed beef or use ethanol fuel, the dead zone is downstream of your consumption.
The InterTribal Buffalo Council (itbc.com) coordinates bison restoration across 82 tribes and 20,000+ animals. Their work restores prairie ecology, rebuilds tribal food sovereignty, and demonstrates an alternative relationship to the land that industrial agriculture replaced. They accept support and publish annual reports on herd health and grassland recovery. Pine Ridge's Tanka Bar — bison meat and cranberry, produced on the reservation — is available for purchase and represents one of the few reservation-based food enterprises reaching a national market.
