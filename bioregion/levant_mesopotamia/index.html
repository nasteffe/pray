What to bring with you from this briefing

In Gaza, 97% of the coastal aquifer is unfit for drinking. This is not a natural disaster — it is the result of over-extraction, sewage contamination from destroyed infrastructure, and seawater intrusion. The WASH Cluster Palestine documented this in 2024. The aquifer serves 2 million people. There is no alternative water source. If you have access to clean water today, you have something that 2 million people in Gaza do not, and the reason is infrastructure, not geography.
Iraqi oil from Basra and Kirkuk reaches global markets through the Strait of Hormuz and the Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline. BP, ExxonMobil, LUKOIL, and TotalEnergies operate these fields. Their annual reports are public. The communities around Basra's oil fields live with gas flaring, contaminated water, and temperatures exceeding 50 degrees. If your pension fund or energy provider sources from these companies, you are connected to those communities. The Business & Human Rights Resource Centre (business-humanrights.org) tracks corporate accountability filings by company name.
Jordan receives 36 hours of piped water per week in some rural areas — a rationing system that predates the Syrian refugee influx but has been deepened by it. Jordan hosts 1.3 million Syrian refugees, making it one of the largest per-capita refugee hosts in the world. The EU trade concession that rewards this hosting requires Syrian workers in qualifying factories. If you buy garments made in Jordan's qualifying industrial zones, the trade deal that enabled their manufacture exists because of the refugee crisis. The connection between the garment and the camp is direct.
