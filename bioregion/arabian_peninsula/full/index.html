In the highlands above Sana'a, stone terraces cut into the mountainside hold soil that Yemeni farmers have tended for three thousand years — sorghum, millet, coffee, and the dark green qat that consumes 40% of the country's irrigation water. The terraces are engineered for a climate that delivers rain in two brief monsoon windows, March to May and July to September, and they work. They have always worked. What is breaking them is not drought but blockade.

What Presses

The weight on Arabian Peninsula

The land is drying. Drought grips Saudi Arabia, Yemen. Oil (transit), crude oil, petrochemical products (plastics, fertilizers, chemicals) leave the region — Yemeni coastal communities (conflict exposure), Red Sea fishers (disrupted livelihoods), Yemeni civilians (targeted by all sides due to strategic location). 5.0 million cannot meet daily food needs. 4.8 million displaced from home.

Yemen's famine is not caused by drought alone — it is manufactured by blockade. The country imports 90% of its food through ports that are bombed, inspected, and delayed by a coalition whose member states also supply the weapons that destroyed Yemen's domestic food infrastructure. The same governments that sell arms to the coalition fund the humanitarian response that feeds the people the arms displaced. WFP convoys enter through ports that coalition navies control.
Saudi Arabia's water crisis is invisible behind desalination capacity, but it is terminal. The Kingdom withdrew from wheat production when fossil aquifers began to fail — water tables that took ten thousand years to accumulate are being emptied in decades. Desalination replaces the supply but at enormous energy cost: roughly a quarter of Saudi domestic oil consumption goes to producing fresh water. The feedback loop — burn oil to make water to grow food to sustain cities built on oil — has no exit that does not involve consuming the resource it depends on.
The kafala system connects South Asian poverty to Gulf construction and domestic service through a contract structure that restricts worker mobility, ties visas to employers, and limits legal recourse. Remittances sent home sustain families in Kerala, Sindh, and Chittagong — but the workers who send them cannot leave, cannot complain, and in the case of domestic workers, cannot be inspected. The labour that builds Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 megaprojects is performed under conditions that Vision 2030's own reform language acknowledges must change.

Cascade Chains

Naval blockade → Port delays → Food import disruption → Price spikes in Sana'a and Hajjah markets → 17.1 million food insecure (YEM — 90% food import dependent; Hodeida port operating at reduced capacity under inspection regime)

Three resource streams flow from or through the Arabian Peninsula — crude oil, petrochemical products, and oil transit — worth hundreds of billions at destination markets. What does not flow back is the water, dropping three metres a year in Saudi aquifers that took millennia to fill; the years lost by migrant workers under contracts they cannot exit; or the calories that do not reach Hodeida because the port sits on the wrong side of a naval cordon. 5.0M food insecure (current); 4.8M displaced (total); oil (transit), crude oil, petrochemical products (plastics, fertilizers, chemicals) leaving; precipitation 13% of normal.

The terrace above Ibb does not know it is three thousand years old. It knows only the weight of soil and the angle of rain. The aquifer beneath Riyadh does not know it is almost empty. It knows only the draw of the pump. What is being consumed in both places is time — stored as water in rock, stored as soil on stone — and what is being produced is a present that cannot sustain itself without borrowing from a past that will not return.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The weapons used in Yemen airstrikes — including strikes on weddings, funerals, and hospitals documented by the UN Panel of Experts — are manufactured by companies listed on stock exchanges you can access: Raytheon (now RTX, NYSE), BAE Systems (LSE), Thales (Euronext Paris). Export licences are granted by the US State Department, the UK Department for Business and Trade, and the French Ministry of Armed Forces. Legal challenges to these licences have been filed by Campaign Against Arms Trade (CAAT) in the UK and by parliamentary groups in multiple EU countries. These are public processes with public records.
The kafala sponsorship system governs the working conditions of an estimated 23 million migrant workers across the Gulf states. Saudi Arabia announced reforms in 2021 allowing some workers to change employers, but enforcement remains limited and domestic workers are largely excluded. The International Labour Organization (ILO) and Human Rights Watch publish regular assessments. If you employ a cleaning service, a construction firm, or purchase goods manufactured in the Gulf, the labour conditions under which they were produced are connected to this system.
Socotra island, a UNESCO World Heritage Site 350 kilometres off the Yemeni coast, hosts 700 species found nowhere else on earth, including the dragon blood tree (Dracaena cinnabari). Since 2018, UAE military installations have been built on the island. The Socotra Governance and Biodiversity Project (SGBP) and local conservation groups continue to document and protect endemic species. Their work is accessible through the Socotra Conservation Fund and through UNESCO reporting on the site's status.
