Beneath the ice at the bottom of the world, the continent holds its breath. Twenty-six and a half million cubic kilometres of frozen freshwater — seventy percent of all the fresh water on Earth — resting on bedrock that in places sits below sea level. At Thwaites Glacier, the grounding line where ice meets ocean floor is retreating at a kilometre a year. The warm water eating at its underside cannot be seen, only measured.

Lives Lived Here

The Antarctic Circumpolar Current is the world's largest ocean current — it circles the continent unimpeded by any landmass, connecting the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. It isolates Antarctica thermally, but warming deep water is now reaching the continental shelf, eating at ice shelves from below. The krill that form the foundation of the food web depend on sea ice for larval habitat — as ice declines, krill decline, and the whales and penguins that rebuilt their populations after the end of commercial whaling find the food web has thinned beneath them.

What Presses

The weight on

Thwaites Glacier — The warm water is reaching the grounding line from below. You cannot see it; you can only measure it. The International Thwaites Glacier Collaboration has sent autonomous underwater vehicles beneath the ice shelf and found channels of warm water eating at the ice from beneath. The grounding line — where the glacier lifts off the bedrock and begins to float — is retreating at roughly a kilometre a year. If the ice shelf that buttresses Thwaites fails, the glacier behind it will accelerate. The physics are simple: ice held back by ice shelf; ice shelf breaks; ice moves faster; sea level rises. Sixty-five centimetres from this one glacier. The glaciers next to it hold more.

The Southern Ocean — A factory trawler from Aker BioMarine processes krill into omega-3 capsules on board. The same krill that a humpback whale has crossed hemispheres to eat. CCAMLR sets catch limits; the vessels self-report their catch. The krill depend on sea ice for larval habitat; the sea ice is declining. The fishery is expanding. The arithmetic is not complicated.

The ice does not negotiate. It responds to temperature, which responds to emissions, which respond to politics, which respond to economics. By the time the political system registers the signal, the ice has already committed to decades of response. Thwaites Glacier is responding to warming that began before most current politicians were born.
The Antarctic Treaty System was a Cold War achievement — twelve nations agreeing that one continent would be for science, not sovereignty. Sixty-five years later, the system holds, but the expanding station footprints (China has built five stations, plans more) suggest that the distinction between science and strategic positioning is narrowing.

For Prayer

For the ice...

For the ice sheets that hold the future of every coast on Earth — for Thwaites Glacier retreating at a kilometre a year, responding to warming that began before most of us were born. That we do not treat distance as permission for inaction.

For those who will bear the cost...

For the communities of Tuvalu, Kiribati, Bangladesh, and every low-lying coast whose future is being written in Antarctic ice loss. They contributed least to the emissions; they will bear the cost first.

For the ocean...

For the Southern Ocean food web — for the krill that feed the whales that feed the wonder that funds the research that documents the loss. That the factory trawlers do not take what the ecosystem cannot spare.

For those who watch...

For the scientists at 70 research stations measuring what the rest of the world is doing to the climate — recording data that should change policy but often only changes graphs. That their evidence finds ears.

For ourselves...

For those of us whose emissions are melting the ice whose loss will flood the coasts whose people did not cause the warming. That we hold this contradiction without resolving it into comfortable abstraction.

Seeds of Hope

The Antarctic and Southern Ocean Coalition (ASOC) — 150+ environmental organisations — maintains the only sustained civil society presence in Antarctic Treaty governance. CCAMLR established the Ross Sea marine protected area in 2016, the world's largest, after years of negotiation. Scientists working through SCAR produce the evidence that should drive policy — the gap between what they document and what Treaty parties act on is the governance crisis in miniature.

Take Action

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Antarctic and Southern Ocean Coalition (ASOC) advocacy

Coalition of 150+ environmental organisations advocating for Antarctic protection within the Antarctic Treaty System. Holds observer status at ATCM and CCAMLR. Campaigns for expanded marine protected areas and against krill fishery expansion.

The primary civil society voice in Antarctic governance — monitors compliance with the Madrid Protocol mineral ban, advocates for Southern Ocean marine protected areas, and tracks the expanding footprints of national programs.

International Thwaites Glacier Collaboration (ITGC) investigation

Joint UK-US research programme studying the Thwaites Glacier — the most significant unknown in sea-level rise projections. Field campaigns measure grounding line retreat, basal melt rates, and ice dynamics.

Directly investigates the single most consequential process on the continent — Thwaites Glacier's potential collapse, which would trigger 65cm of global sea-level rise and destabilise neighbouring glaciers. Every coastal community's future depends on what this glacier does.

Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR) investigation

International body managing Antarctic marine living resources. Sets krill catch limits, designates marine protected areas, and monitors compliance. The Ross Sea MPA (2016) is the world's largest.

Governs the krill fishery that extracts from the base of the Southern Ocean food web. Catch limits are set regionally but enforcement is self-reported — the gap between regulation and verification is where ecosystem risk accumulates.

Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research (SCAR) investigation

International coordinating body for Antarctic science. Produces assessments of Antarctic climate change, biodiversity, and ice-sheet dynamics that inform policy within the Antarctic Treaty System.

Produces the scientific evidence on ice-sheet mass balance, sea-ice trends, and ecosystem change that should drive policy — the gap between what SCAR documents and what Treaty parties act on is the governance crisis.
