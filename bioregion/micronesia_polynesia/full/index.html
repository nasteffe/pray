What Presses

The weight on Micronesia and Polynesia

Tuna fishing licenses, tuna licenses and .tv domain, tuna licenses and compact funding, tuna licenses leave the region — I-Kiribati people (nation faces existential sea-level rise), marine ecosystem (overfishing pressure), 12,000 Tuvaluans (sea-level rise threatens national existence).

The Pacific atoll nations whose existence is threatened by sea-level rise contributed less than 0.1% of cumulative global greenhouse gas emissions. Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, and FSM together emit roughly 150,000 tonnes of CO2 annually — less than a single medium-sized coal-fired power plant. The nations bearing the full cost of the emissions transition are not the nations that made the emissions. This is the central moral fact of Pacific climate politics, and the international community has so far responded with aspirational pledges and a Loss and Damage fund of $700 million — for nations facing the loss of their national territory.
The Pacific Parties to the Nauru Agreement demonstrated that collective action by small island nations can reshape resource extraction terms: the Vessel Day Scheme tripled tuna access fees since 2010, turning a bilateral negotiating weakness into collective market power. The same principle — that 9 nations controlling 3.5 million square kilometres of ocean can negotiate as one — is being applied to climate loss and damage claims. Whether it produces proportionate results depends on whether the nations causing the emissions accept that their legal and moral obligation to the Pacific extends beyond voluntary contributions.

Who Sustains This

Parties to the Nauru Agreement (PNA Office, Majuro) · Vessel-Day Scheme licensing revenue (~$500M+/year across the 8 PNA member states: FSM, KIR, MHL, NRU, PLW + non-bioregion PNG, SLB, TUV); Skipjack tuna fishery (~50% of global commercial supply originates in PNA waters); Forum Fisheries Agency coordination + Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission framework; Member-state contributions to PNA Office operations in Majuro

Driven by: We must secure equitable benefit from our tuna for our people

The trap: The Vessel-Day Scheme is genuinely one of the most successful sovereign cartels in fisheries — small island states forced a price floor on Japanese, Korean, Taiwanese, and Chinese distant-water fleets where no individual state could have. But the sovereignty operates at the licensing layer, not the processing or consumption layer; the tuna is still caught by foreign fleets, processed on foreign factory ships, and consumed in Tokyo and Barcelona. The licence fee funds 40-70% of national budgets while the fishery on which the budget depends remains structured outside the licensors' control. Each successful renegotiation deepens the dependency on a revenue line that is also the proof of how little sovereignty exists beyond the licence.

US Compact of Free Association regime (Marshall Islands theatre) · 2024 COFA renewal (20-year extension) — ~$2.3B trust capitalisation + ~$700M nuclear-claims envelope; Reagan Test Site at Kwajalein Atoll (US Army strategic-defense missile testing infrastructure); Runit Dome (Enewetak Atoll) custodial framework + ongoing leakage and litigation; Strategic-denial provision — US right to exclude other powers' military access to RMI/FSM/PLW EEZs

Driven by: We must honour our compact obligations to the Marshallese people and partners in the Pacific

The trap: The 2024 COFA renewal added trust capitalisation and a new nuclear-claims envelope; both real, both contested as inadequate by the Marshallese parliament and the Runit-area communities whose ancestral atolls remain uninhabitable. The strategic-denial provision — the US's right to exclude other powers' military presence from RMI/FSM/PLW EEZs — is what makes COFA renewal a security-policy priority for Washington at the same time as climate-induced displacement makes Marshallese migration to Hawai'i, Arkansas, and Washington State an existential reality. The trust-relationship language treats the nuclear ledger as historical and the strategic ledger as current; both ledgers describe the same Marshallese territory.

Government of Tuvalu / Falepili Union framework · Tuna-licensing revenue under PNA Vessel-Day Scheme framework (~$10M+/year); Tuvalu Trust Fund (~$200M sovereign reserve); Internet domain .tv revenue (~$10M+/year, brand-licensing royalties); Diaspora remittances (~$NZ 5-10M/year from NZ/Australia/US Tuvaluan workers)

Driven by: We must preserve Tuvalu as a nation through every form available — territorial, digital, diasporic

The trap: The Falepili Union granted Tuvaluans the right of permanent settlement in Australia under a climate-mobility framework that is, in international law, a precedent of sovereignty-preserving- rather-than-territorially-bounded statehood. In exchange Australia received a veto over Tuvalu's security and defence arrangements — a structural constraint the same Tuvaluan government simultaneously named as protecting the sovereignty the climate emergency was eroding. The 'digital nation' plan to preserve government services and cultural heritage in cloud infrastructure functions as a wager that the sovereignty being preserved is something other than territorial — which is both visionary statecraft and a concession to the question the climate emergency refuses to answer on Tuvalu's behalf.

For Kiribati, Tuvalu, and the Marshall Islands...

For the nations at one to two metres above sea level — whose taro pits are flooded by king tides in February and March, whose freshwater lenses thin with each storm overwash, whose governments are negotiating migration agreements and purchasing land in other countries while insisting on their continued sovereignty. That the world's response matches the scale of the injustice.

For the Bikini and Enewetak communities...

For the Bikini Islanders who have not been home since 1946 — eighty years — and for the Enewetak community living with the Runit Dome, a concrete cap over plutonium-contaminated debris, cracking as the ocean rises around it. That the United States pays what the Nuclear Claims Tribunal awarded, and that the contamination is contained before the dome fails entirely.

For the freshwater lens...

For the thin freshwater layer beneath the coral sand of Pacific atolls — the sole drinking water and farming water for the people who live above it — which thins with each storm overwash and each dry season and each year's sea-level rise. That it holds long enough for the people above it to find safety.

For the reef...

For Palau's Rock Islands, Kiribati's Phoenix Islands, and the coral reefs that sustain coastal communities across nine nations — providing shoreline protection and most of the dietary protein — now bleaching under ocean temperatures exceeding the thermal tolerance of corals that have survived three thousand years of Pacific variation. That some survive what is coming.

For the diaspora communities...

For the Tongan community in Auckland, the Marshallese community in Springdale, Arkansas, the Tuvaluans in Auckland and Sydney — sending remittances home to islands they may never permanently return to, holding the culture, the language, and the political claim of nations whose physical territory is disappearing. That their identity outlasts the land.

For the climate negotiators...

For the Pacific Island delegates at UNFCCC, AOSIS, and the Loss and Damage Fund negotiations — making the legal case that a nation's existence cannot be measured only in tonnes of CO2, that sovereignty does not require land above sea level, and that a Loss and Damage Fund far too small cannot be a serious response to the loss of nations. That they are heard.

For Palau's decision...

Give thanks for Palau's 2015 decision to close most of its waters as a marine sanctuary — forgoing the revenue those waters would have generated, choosing ecosystem integrity over immediate income, insisting that the ocean has a future worth protecting. That this choice is recognised for what it is: a form of sovereignty asserted at cost.

Organizations working at the nodes this briefing traces

Pacific Islands Forum Fisheries Agency (FFA) advocacy

Regional body managing tuna fisheries access across Pacific Island EEZs, negotiating licence fees with distant-water fleets from China, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. EEZ access fees fund national budgets for Kiribati, Tuvalu, and FSM.

Manages the primary revenue flow the briefing describes — tuna caught in Pacific Island EEZs by distant-water fleets generates the licence fees that fund national budgets. The fish is eaten in Tokyo and Barcelona, not in Tarawa or Funafuti; FFA negotiates the terms of this asymmetry.

Marshall Islands Nuclear Claims Tribunal advocacy

Established to adjudicate claims arising from the 67 US nuclear tests on Bikini and Enewetak atolls (1946-1958). Awarded $2 billion in compensation; the US has paid approximately $150 million. The Runit Dome containing radioactive debris is cracking as seas rise.

Directly addresses the nuclear legacy the briefing centers — Bikini Islanders have not been home since 1946, the Runit Dome is failing, and the US has paid less than 8% of the awarded compensation. This is the cost-bearer relationship with the longest unresolved timeline in the briefing.

Tuvalu Government — Statehood Beyond Territory solidarity

Tuvalu's government is pioneering the legal concept of statehood beyond physical territory — arguing that a nation can remain sovereign even as its land disappears beneath the waves. Negotiated the Falepili Union with Australia, the first national climate evacuation agreement in history.

Tuvalu faces the existential crisis the briefing describes — 12,000 citizens on atolls at 1-2 metres elevation, freshwater lenses salinizing, national inundation within decades from emissions they did not produce (less than 0.1% of global CO2). The .tv domain generates more revenue than the nation's physical economy.

Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS) advocacy

Intergovernmental organization of 39 small island and low-lying coastal states negotiating as a bloc on climate policy. Led the push for the 1.5C target in the Paris Agreement and for loss and damage finance at COP.

The collective negotiating voice the briefing references — Pacific atolls that emit less than 0.1% of cumulative global emissions using coordinated diplomacy to demand accountability from major emitters. AOSIS transformed the moral weight of nations that contribute least to climate change into political leverage.

Palau National Marine Sanctuary solidarity

Palau closed 80% of its 500,000 km2 EEZ as a marine protected area in 2015 — one of the world's largest. This was a sovereign decision to forgo fishing revenue in order to protect the ocean, a counter-model to the extractive licence-fee economy.

The briefing's resistance narrative centers Palau's sanctuary as the Pacific's most striking assertion of ecological sovereignty — a small island nation choosing long-term marine protection over short-term revenue from distant-water fleets. The counter-model to the tuna extraction that funds other Pacific nations.
