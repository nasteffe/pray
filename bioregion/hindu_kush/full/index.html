Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Helmand valley, a farmer's wife lights the tandoor and begins baking bread from the wheat her family grew last season on land irrigated by the Shamalan canal. The canal draws from the Helmand River, which draws from Hindu Kush snowmelt, which arrives later and ends sooner each year. Her husband is no longer in the poppy field — the Taliban's 2022 ban ended that. The Taliban collector still visits, now looking for alternative crops to tax. She knows what the poppy used to pay for. She also knows that without it, there was no flour for the tandoor in January, and the wheat alone does not cover the difference. In Faryab province, a woman sits cross-legged on a carpet loom, knotting wool that was sheared from a Kuchi herder's sheep last spring. The carpet will take four months to complete and sell for $200 to a trader in Mazar-i-Sharif who will export it for $2,000. She is one of thousands of women carpet-weavers whose work represents one of Afghanistan's few livelihoods not captured entirely by the armed group economy — though the trader's export license runs through Taliban customs. Her daughter, who is fifteen, sits beside her. The girl was in the sixth grade when the education ban came. She is learning the knots instead. In Kabul, a hawala broker receives a call from a cousin in Hamburg. The cousin sends €300 — enough for his mother's household to buy food and heating fuel for a month. The money moves through a trust network older than the banking system it replaced: no wire transfer, no SWIFT code, no account number. Since the fall of the Republic in 2021, formal banking channels have been severed by international sanctions, and the $3 billion a year in diaspora remittances that once sustained Afghan households now depends entirely on hawala brokers whose operations exist in a legal grey zone. The cousin in Hamburg works twelve-hour shifts in a warehouse. He has not seen his mother in five years. On the road between Jalalabad and Torkham, a truck carrying lapis lazuli from Badakhshan waits at the border crossing. The driver has paid three separate Taliban checkpoints to reach this point. Across the border, a Pakistani middleman in Peshawar will grade the stones and sell them to international buyers. The value of the cargo multiplies at each handover. The miners who cut the stone earn the least.

What Presses

The weight on Hindu Kush

Opium, lapis lazuli, lithium, rare earth elements leave the region — farming families locked into single cash crop, communities experiencing addiction and health collapse, women excluded from alternative livelihood pathways. 13.8 million cannot meet daily food needs. 3.2 million displaced from home.

The opium economy was not a criminal sideshow — it was the economy. When farming families in Helmand planted poppy, they were making the only rational decision available: poppy yielded four times the revenue of wheat, tolerated drought better, and had a guaranteed buyer at the farm gate. The Taliban banned it in April 2022, enforced the ban with eradication teams, and collapsed cultivation 95% by 2023 (from 233,000 to 10,800 hectares, UNODC). Farmer income from opium fell by over $1 billion in 2023 alone. The Taliban collected an estimated $400–500M annually from opium before the ban (UNODC 2022); that revenue stream has also collapsed. No legal alternative provides comparable returns. The humanitarian consequences of eradication without alternatives — precisely what aid agencies warned would happen — are now arriving as deepened food insecurity in Helmand and Kandahar.
The qanat systems represent something no other infrastructure in Afghanistan does: technology that works without electricity, without fuel, without foreign parts, and without government. Built over three millennia by specialist engineers (muqannis) whose knowledge is transmitted within families, qanats tap mountain aquifers by gravity alone. Sixty percent are now non-functional — not because the technology failed, but because the communities that maintained them were displaced by conflict and drought. Each collapsed qanat is irreversible: the knowledge to rebuild it left with the muqanni who maintained it.
Afghanistan sits on an estimated $1–3 trillion in lithium, copper, and rare earth minerals — precisely the materials the global green transition requires for batteries and electronics. Chinese companies have begun preliminary agreements with the Taliban for extraction rights. No environmental impact framework exists. No labour protection exists. No community consultation mechanism exists. The communities most likely to be displaced by mining are pastoral Kuchi herders and highland farming villages — the same people already bearing the cost of glacial retreat. The green transition that promises to address the emissions causing that retreat may accelerate the displacement it produces.

Three resource streams flow out of the Hindu Kush — opium (now largely banned but partially rebounding in the northeast), lapis lazuli, and (soon) lithium. The opium ban removed the one income source farming families could rely on, without replacing it; the Taliban that enforces the ban still governs the girls out of school. The lapis lazuli is mined by children who will never attend one. The lithium is wanted by the same economies whose emissions are melting the glaciers that water the fields. The qanat does not know who dug it. It carries water because the slope is right and the tunnel is clear. Three thousand years of knowledge travel in that gradient — knowledge held not in books but in the hands of the muqanni who cleans the shaft each spring. When the muqanni is displaced, the water stops. No one plans this. No one reverses it. The tunnel fills with silence, and the village above it empties.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

The qanat/kariz systems of Afghanistan are recognised by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage — three-thousand-year-old gravity-fed water tunnels maintained by specialist engineers (muqannis) whose knowledge is transmitted within families. Sixty percent are now non-functional due to displacement and conflict. The International Centre on Qanats and Historic Hydraulic Structures (ICQHS, icqhs.org) documents preservation efforts. Supporting qanat restoration means supporting the most sustainable, locally governed water infrastructure in Central Asia — technology that works without electricity, fuel, or foreign parts.
Afghan women carpet-weavers in Faryab, Herat, and Balkh provinces produce one of the country's few export products not controlled by armed groups. Fair-trade Afghan carpet importers exist in the UK, EU, and US — buying directly from women's cooperatives rather than through Taliban-taxed export channels. Turquoise Mountain (turquoisemountain.org), founded in Kabul, trains Afghan artisans and connects them to international markets. When you buy an Afghan carpet, ask where it came from and who wove it.
The lithium beneath Afghanistan's mountains is wanted for the batteries in electric vehicles, phones, and laptops. No extraction framework — environmental, labour, or community — currently exists. The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act and the US Inflation Reduction Act both create demand for these minerals. Ask your device manufacturer: do your supply chain commitments extend to jurisdictions where no rule of law protects the communities at the extraction site? The question is not hypothetical. The surveying has begun.
