In the Panjshir Valley, a muqanni — a tunnel engineer whose father taught him and whose grandfather taught his father — descends into a qanat shaft cut three thousand years ago to check the flow. In Faryab province, a woman sits cross-legged on a carpet loom, knotting wool that was sheared from a Kuchi herder's sheep last spring. The carpet will take four months to complete and sell for $200 to a trader in Mazar-i-Sharif who will export it for $2,000. She is one of thousands of women carpet-weavers whose work represents one of Afghanistan's few livelihoods not captured entirely by the armed group economy — though the trader's export license runs through Taliban customs. Her daughter, who is fifteen, sits beside her. The girl was in the sixth grade when the education ban came. She is learning the knots instead. In Kabul, a hawala broker receives a call from a cousin in Hamburg. The cousin sends €300 — enough for his mother's household to buy food and heating fuel for a month. The money moves through a trust network older than the banking system it replaced: no wire transfer, no SWIFT code, no account number. Since the fall of the Republic in 2021, formal banking channels have been severed by international sanctions, and the $3 billion a year in diaspora remittances that once sustained Afghan households now depends entirely on hawala brokers whose operations exist in a legal grey zone. The cousin in Hamburg works twelve-hour shifts in a warehouse. He has not seen his mother in five years. Across the border, a Pakistani middleman in Peshawar will grade the stones and sell them to international buyers. The value of the cargo multiplies at each handover. What Presses

The weight on Hindu Kush

Helmand Province — In the canal country south of the Hindu Kush, where the Boghra and Shamalan canals carry snowmelt to the largest irrigated area in Afghanistan, a farming family faces the same calculation every October: plant poppy, or go hungry by February. Wheat yields have fallen as canal flow shortens — snowmelt arrives earlier and ends sooner — and the wheat price at market does not cover the cost of seed and fertiliser. Poppy tolerates drought better, yields four times the cash, and has a buyer at the farm gate who pays in advance. The Taliban collector arrives after harvest and takes his share. The family knows what the money funds. They also know what happens to families who planted wheat instead: they are the ones queueing at the WFP distribution point, if the distribution point is still open, if the female staff who ran it have not been banned.

Badakhshan Province — In the northeast, where the Panj River marks the border with Tajikistan and the mountains rise above 5,000 metres, the lapis lazuli mines have operated for six thousand years. The stone that coloured Renaissance paintings is now cut by boys as young as ten, working under Taliban-supervised operations that generate $20 million or more annually. The revenue leaves. The miners earn subsistence wages, when they are paid at all. The truck route crosses into Pakistan through border points controlled by successive checkpoints, each demanding payment. In the villages around the mines, there is no school — for boys or girls — and no clinic. The mine is the only employer. The mountain gives, and the mountain takes.

Kabul — A city built for 500,000 now holds over 4 million, swollen by waves of displacement — from the Soviet war, the civil war, the American war, and now the economic collapse that followed the Taliban takeover. Water pressure drops each summer as the aquifer is over-pumped. A mother in Dasht-e-Barchi — the Hazara neighbourhood that has been the target of repeated bombings — fills a jerry can from a communal tap and carries it home. Her daughter, who was studying medicine before the education ban, helps her carry it. The daughter is nineteen. She has been out of school for four years. In the evenings, she studies from smuggled textbooks in a room where the neighbours cannot hear.

The opium economy is not a criminal sideshow — it is the economy. When farming families in Helmand plant poppy, they are making the only rational decision available: poppy yields four times the revenue of wheat, tolerates drought better, and has a guaranteed buyer at the farm gate. The Taliban tax it at every stage — cultivation, processing, transport — generating $400–500 million annually. Every alternative requires market access, banking infrastructure, and transport security that the current government cannot or will not provide. Eradication without alternatives is famine by policy.
The qanat systems represent something no other infrastructure in Afghanistan does: technology that works without electricity, without fuel, without foreign parts, and without government. Built over three millennia by specialist engineers (muqannis) whose knowledge is transmitted within families, qanats tap mountain aquifers by gravity alone. Sixty percent are now non-functional — not because the technology failed, but because the communities that maintained them were displaced by conflict and drought. Each collapsed qanat is irreversible: the knowledge to rebuild it left with the muqanni who maintained it.
Afghanistan sits on an estimated $1–3 trillion in lithium, copper, and rare earth minerals — precisely the materials the global green transition requires for batteries and electronics. Chinese companies have begun preliminary agreements with the Taliban for extraction rights. No environmental impact framework exists. No labour protection exists. No community consultation mechanism exists. The communities most likely to be displaced by mining are pastoral Kuchi herders and highland farming villages — the same people already bearing the cost of glacial retreat. The green transition that promises to address the emissions causing that retreat may accelerate the displacement it produces.

Three resource streams flow out of the Hindu Kush — opium, lapis lazuli, and (soon) lithium. The opium funds the government that bans girls from school. The lapis lazuli is mined by children who will never attend one. The lithium is wanted by the same economies whose emissions are melting the glaciers that water the fields. Nothing that leaves comes back as education, as clean water, as a road to market for the saffron that could replace the poppy. 183 killed (3 months); 13.8M food insecure (current); 3.2M displaced (total); opium, lapis lazuli, lithium, rare earth elements leaving; precipitation 68% of normal.

The qanat does not know who dug it. It carries water because the slope is right and the tunnel is clear. Three thousand years of knowledge travel in that gradient — knowledge held not in books but in the hands of the muqanni who cleans the shaft each spring. When the muqanni is displaced, the water stops. No one plans this. No one reverses it. The tunnel fills with silence, and the village above it empties.

Carry This

What to bring with you from this briefing

Afghanistan's qanat systems — 3,000-year-old gravity-fed tunnels maintained by muqannis — are UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. 60% are now non-functional. They need no electricity, fuel, or foreign parts. See ICQHS preservation efforts at icqhs.org.
Women carpet-weavers in Faryab, Herat, and Balkh produce one of Afghanistan's few exports not controlled by armed groups. Turquoise Mountain (turquoisemountain.org) connects cooperatives to international buyers. Ask where your Afghan carpet was woven.
Afghanistan's lithium is wanted for EV and phone batteries. No environmental, labour, or community extraction framework exists. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act and US Inflation Reduction Act drive demand. The surveying has begun. Ask your device manufacturer where their supply chain commitments end.

Intergovernmental research centre monitoring Hindu Kush Himalaya glaciers, snowpack, and water systems. Publishes authoritative assessments of glacier retreat, peak water projections, and downstream water security for 2 billion people.

Produces the glacier and snowmelt data the briefing draws on — documenting the approach of peak water, when Hindu Kush rivers will run higher briefly as stored ice melts and then decline permanently, threatening the qanat systems that took three millennia to build.
