In the central basin of the Congo, the forest makes its own rain. Forty per cent of what falls here was breathed out by the canopy an hour earlier — transpired through leaves, gathered into cloud, returned as storm. The forest is not watered; it waters itself. Beneath that canopy, women paddle pirogues along 40,000 kilometres of navigable river, carrying smoked fish and cassava flour between villages that have no road.

Lives Lived Here

Before dawn on the Congo River near Mbandaka, a woman loads a pirogue with cassava flour and smoked catfish. She will paddle and drift for three days downstream to sell at the Kinshasa river market, sleeping on the boat, cooking over a small fire balanced on a mud hearth in the hull. She is one of thousands of women who run the river trade — the Congo's 40,000 km of waterways are the only transport network that works, and women dominate it. Her cassava comes from a forest clearing that her family has farmed in rotation for decades: cut, burn, plant, harvest, leave to fallow for five years, return. The system worked when forest was abundant. Now the fallow shrinks because charcoal cutters — many of them displaced farmers from the east — are clearing the regrowth. Kinshasa, a city of 17 million with almost no electricity grid, burns five million tonnes of charcoal a year.

In Haut-Katanga, in the south, a boy of twelve descends into an artisanal cobalt tunnel dug by hand into red laterite soil. There are 40,000 miners like him, including children, working without helmets, without timber shoring, without any protection from the tunnel collapses that kill miners regularly. He earns a few dollars a day. The cobalt he digs is sold to a comptoir — a trading house — which sells it to a Chinese refinery in Lubumbashi, which ships it to battery cathode manufacturers, which supply Tesla, Apple, and Samsung. Fifteen to thirty per cent of the world's cobalt travels this route. The boy does not know what a lithium-ion battery is. He knows the price per kilogram and the sound a tunnel makes before it falls.

Across the river in Brazzaville, a government official reviews oil concession bids from TotalEnergies and Eni for new offshore blocks near Pointe-Noire. Congo-Brazzaville's oil has kept President Sassou-Nguesso in power for 36 years — the revenue funds a patronage network that is indistinguishable from the state itself. The coastal fishing communities near Pointe-Noire whose catches have declined since the rigs arrived are not consulted.

What Presses

The weight on Congo Basin

eastern_drc In North Kivu, the hills around Goma have been contested for so long that the fighting has its own economy. M23, backed by Rwanda, controls coltan mining sites whose output crosses the border to Kigali and enters the global electronics supply chain. The armed forces of the DRC cannot retake the territory — decades of campaigns have not established state control in the east, and each offensive displaces more families into camps around Goma that were temporary fifteen years ago. 5.2 million people are displaced across the country. Women and girls face extreme risk of sexual violence — the World Bank reports an enduring crisis of gender-based violence directly linked to displacement and armed group presence. In Haut-Katanga, artisanal cobalt miners dig with their hands for a global industry that promises a clean energy future, and the tunnel collapses that kill them do not appear in any EV advertisement.

central_african_republic In CAF, 1.9 million people cannot feed themselves in a country of 5.5 million. The diamond and gold corridors of the east — Vakaga, Haut-Mbomou — are controlled by whoever has the most guns this season: ex-Seleka factions, anti-Balaka militias, or Russian Africa Corps contractors who arrived offering security and stayed for the mining concessions. On 4 March, two MSF staff members were arrested in Zemio — a town on the southern border where humanitarian access depends entirely on armed group tolerance. The Fulani and Mbororo pastoralists who move cattle through the northern corridors pay taxation at every armed checkpoint. What reaches the Cameroonian and Chadian livestock markets is what the armed groups did not take.

forest_peoples The BaMbuti of North Kivu and Ituri, and the BaTwa of the Great Lakes region, face a dispossession that comes from both directions. Armed groups have pushed them from their forest territories. Conservation parks — Kahuzi-Biega, Virunga — have fenced them out of the forest that remains, designating their homeland as protected area and their presence as intrusion. They are Indigenous to a forest that two different kinds of power agree they should not be in. Their knowledge of the forest ecosystem — the caterpillar seasons, the honey trees, the movement of game — is the kind of knowledge that conservation science values in principle and displaces in practice.

The cobalt in your phone battery and the coltan in your laptop capacitor were likely dug by hand from tunnels in eastern DRC — tunnels that collapse and kill miners regularly, in territory controlled by armed groups that have fought over these minerals for three decades. Due diligence legislation (the EU Conflict Minerals Regulation, the Dodd-Frank Act) has added paperwork but not stopped the killing. The minerals still flow; they simply pass through one more trading house in Kigali or Kampala before entering the formal supply chain. Your device works because someone else's child is underground.
The Congo forest makes 40% of its own rain through transpiration — it breathes water into the atmosphere, which falls again as storm. When the forest is cut for charcoal to cook in Kinshasa, or logged for timber bound for Chinese factories, or cleared for slash-and-burn farming by families displaced from the east, the moisture cycle breaks. Less forest means less rain means less forest. The basin is approaching a point after which the process feeds itself, and 30 gigatonnes of peat carbon — currently locked under waterlogged soil — begins to dry and release. No climate target survives the loss of the Congo Basin peatlands.
In CAR, Russian Africa Corps contractors arrived as an alternative to French and UN security forces — and took mining concessions as payment. They guard industrial gold and diamond sites while documenting atrocities against the civilians they were deployed to protect. The security they provide is a new form of extraction: protection at the price of the resource, offered to a government that cannot say no because it has no other army.

Eight resource streams flow out of the Congo Basin — coltan, cobalt, diamonds, gold, crude oil, tropical timber, charcoal, and livestock — and what does not come back is the forest, consumed for charcoal and logged for timber; the people, 5.2 million displaced in DRC alone; and the children, 40,000 of them mining cobalt so that the rest of the world can hold a clean energy transition in its hand. 1,843 killed (3 months); 26.7M food insecure (current); 5.6M displaced (total); coltan (tantalum), cobalt, timber leaving; precipitation 51% of normal; 192 fires (7 days).

What to bring with you from this briefing

The cobalt in lithium-ion batteries — in phones, laptops, EVs — is partially sourced from artisanal mines in DRC's Haut-Katanga province where 40,000 miners, including children, dig by hand in tunnels that collapse regularly. Tesla, Apple, and Samsung are all downstream buyers. Ask your device manufacturer: where is your cobalt sourced, and what accountability framework covers artisanal supply? Amnesty International's 2016 report "This Is What We Die For" documents the supply chain. The situation has not materially changed.
Coltan (tantalum ore) from eastern DRC supplies 80% of the world's tantalum, used in capacitors in nearly all electronics. Despite the EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (in force since 2021) and Section 1502 of the US Dodd-Frank Act, armed groups continue to profit from the trade. The UN Group of Experts on the DRC (report S/2024/432) documents ongoing smuggling routes through Rwanda and Uganda. These reports are publicly available at undocs.org.
