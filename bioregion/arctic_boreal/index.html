Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in Fort Chipewyan, Alberta — a community of 900 people accessible only by ice road in winter and boat or plane in summer — a Mikisew Cree fisher checks his nets in Lake Athabasca as he has done since childhood, as his father did, as his grandmother did. The walleye are smaller now. Some have tumours. He eats them anyway, because the grocery barge charges $14 for a head of lettuce and the ice road was open for only six weeks last winter. Downstream of his nets, the Athabasca River carries whatever the tailings ponds have released this season. He knows this. The community has been testing the water for twenty years. The results are not reassuring.

In Old Crow, Yukon, a Gwich'in mother packs dried caribou meat for her daughter's school lunch — country food from the Porcupine caribou herd, which migrates 2,400 kilometres between the Yukon and the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge in Alaska each year. The herd has declined from 178,000 animals in 1989 to approximately 100,000 today. Her grandmother packed the same food, from the same herd, following the same migration route. The difference is the calving grounds: warmer springs bring earlier snowmelt, which brings earlier insect emergence, which means the caribou arrive at the coastal plain after the peak forage window has passed. The herd shrinks not from a single catastrophe but from a timing mismatch — the land's calendar shifting faster than the animals' instincts can follow.

In Newtok, Alaska, a Yup'ik family has been packing for nine years. Their village is relocating to Mertarvik, nine miles upriver, because the Ninglick River is eating the land beneath their houses at 70 feet per year — permafrost that once held the bank rigid is now thawing, and autumn storms that sea ice once deflected now hit the exposed coast at full force. Congress has appropriated money. FEMA has studied the problem. The Army Corps has drawn plans. The family is still packing. They have moved some belongings to the new site, which has a few houses, a generator, and no running water. Their old house has running water but may not have ground beneath it next autumn.

What Presses

The weight on Arctic–Boreal

Fort Chipewyan, Alberta — Nine hundred people live here, at the western tip of Lake Athabasca, accessible by ice road for six weeks in winter and by boat or float plane the rest of the year. The Athabasca River arrives from the south, having passed through the oil sands region where tailings ponds covering 220 square kilometres sit adjacent to its banks. The Mikisew Cree and Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation have fished this lake for generations. In 2006, community members began reporting elevated rates of cholangiocarcinoma — bile duct cancer, rare enough that a cluster of cases in a village of 900 demanded explanation. The community funded its own health study after Alberta Health Services declined to investigate. Dr. John O'Connor, the local physician who raised the alarm, was investigated by Health Canada for causing "undue alarm." He was later cleared. The cancers were confirmed. The tailings ponds remain.

Old Crow, Yukon — The most northerly community in Yukon, home to the Vuntut Gwitchin First Nation, population 250. There is no road. Supplies arrive by air, or by barge when the Porcupine River is navigable. The community's food security depends substantially on the Porcupine caribou herd, which calves on the coastal plain of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge in Alaska. Old Crow sits at the intersection of two crises: the caribou herd declining as climate shifts calving-forage timing, and the permafrost beneath the village thawing, cracking foundations and tilting structures. The temperature at which permafrost ceases to be permanent is 0°C. The annual mean temperature in Old Crow has been rising toward that line for decades.

Newtok, Alaska — A Yup'ik village of 380 people on the Ninglick River, which is eroding the village at 70 feet per year. The permafrost that once held the riverbank is thawing. The sea ice that once absorbed storm energy before it reached the coast forms later and breaks earlier. In 2003, the Army Corps of Engineers estimated the village had 15 years before it became uninhabitable. It is 2026. The relocation to Mertarvik, nine miles away, is partially complete — some families have moved, most have not, because the new site lacks water and sewage infrastructure. The cost of relocating a single Alaska Native village is estimated at $100–400 million. There are 31 villages in Alaska facing similar threats. The federal government has funded portions of several relocations and completed none.

The Athabasca tar sands are a sacrifice zone with a formal name: the tailings ponds are classified as "fluid tailings" under Alberta law, and the companies that created them are legally required to remediate them. Remediation timelines stretch to 2080 and beyond. The Mikisew Cree and Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation are downstream now, not in 2080. Suncor, Canadian Natural Resources, and Cenovus are listed on the Toronto and New York stock exchanges. Their tailings liabilities are disclosed in annual reports. The gap between the legal obligation and the lived reality is measured in cancer diagnoses.
Caribou decline is not a wildlife management problem. It is a food sovereignty crisis for Inuit, Gwich'in, Dene, and Cree communities who depend on caribou for protein, clothing, tools, and cultural continuity. The Bathurst herd in the Northwest Territories has collapsed from 450,000 in the 1980s to approximately 8,200 — a 98% decline. Mining roads fragment migration corridors. Industrial fire suppression alters the lichen regeneration cycle. Climate-driven insect harassment changes calving behaviour. Each cause is individually manageable; together they are systemic, and the herd does not recover between interventions.
The permafrost methane feedback connects this region to every other region on earth. Arctic amplification — the Arctic warming four times faster than the global mean — destabilises the polar vortex, sending cold outbreaks into mid-latitudes (the "polar vortex" events that freeze Texas and the UK). The same warming thaws permafrost, releasing methane that accelerates the warming that caused the thaw. Your winter heating bill, your spring allergy season, your summer drought risk — all are shaped by what is happening to the ground beneath a Gwich'in elder's house in Old Crow, Yukon.

Three resource streams flow out of the Arctic-Boreal region — bitumen, shale gas, and industrial grain — worth hundreds of billions at destination markets. What does not leave is the methane, now releasing from thawing permafrost at rates that feed back into the warming that caused the thaw. The tailings ponds in Alberta will require remediation for centuries. The permafrost that held the ground stable for Indigenous communities will not refreeze in any human lifetime. The cost stays here; the profit does not. 16 killed (3 months); bitumen crude oil (oil sands), shale oil and natural gas, corn, soybeans, and dairy products leaving.

The permafrost is not a metaphor. It is the ground beneath the houses, the airstrips, the graves. When it thaws, everything built on the assumption that the ground would hold must be moved or lost. The Gwich'in and the Inuit did not build on an assumption — they built on knowledge of the land as it was. The land is becoming something else, faster than any community can relocate, and slower than any news cycle can sustain attention.

Seeds of Hope

The Gwich'in Steering Committee, formed in 1988 when the US government first proposed oil drilling in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, has maintained a 38-year campaign to protect the Porcupine caribou herd's calving grounds on the coastal plain of northeast Alaska. Their argument has never changed — the caribou are not a resource to be managed but a relative whose survival and theirs are the same question.
