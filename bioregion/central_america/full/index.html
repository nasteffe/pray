Lives Lived Here

Before dawn in the Huehuetenango highlands, a Maya farmer picks coffee cherries by hand into a basket strapped across her forehead. She is paid by weight — a good day is $4 — and the cherries travel from her hands to the wet mill in the valley, to the dry mill near the coast, to a container at Puerto Quetzal, to a roaster in Portland who sells the beans as "single-origin Huehuetenango" for $22 a bag. She has never tasted what they make from it. Her daughter, who finished secondary school, works at a call centre in Guatemala City. Her son left for Arizona two years ago. He sends $200 a month through Western Union. The transfer costs $12. The $200 is more than she earns in a month from coffee.

In San Pedro Sula, a woman packs shirts at a maquiladora — an export-processing factory in the free trade zone where Honduran labour law bends to foreign investment terms. She earns the minimum wage, which does not cover rent and food for her three children. The shirts will carry labels from brands sold in malls she will never enter. Her husband was a campesino in Colón before Dinant Corporation cleared their communal plot for oil palm. He drives a taxi now. On the radio, another environmental defender has been killed — the fourth this year — and no one has been arrested.

In Tapachula, on Mexico's southern border, a Honduran family waits. They left San Pedro Sula after the second extortion demand — pay the tax or lose the house. The journey through Guatemala took eight days. Now they wait for a transit permit that may take months. The shelter is full. The children attend a makeshift school run by a Catholic sister who speaks enough Garífuna to teach them. The father picks fruit on a nearby farm for cash. The mother calls her sister in Houston every Sunday evening to say they are fine. They are not fine. But they are together, and the road still goes north.

In Limón Province, a Nicaraguan migrant on a banana plantation slices bunches from the stem with a machete, his arms stained blue from the fungicide Chiquita requires on every hand of fruit. He crossed the border six months ago because there was no work at home that paid enough to eat. His remittances keep his mother and two sisters fed in Matagalpa. The bananas reach Miami in eleven days. The money reaches Matagalpa in three minutes.

What Presses

The weight on Central America

Diaspora remittances, coffee beans, gold, bananas leave the region — migrant workers (wage suppression, discrimination, occupational hazards), families dependent on single remittance earner, households in rural areas without banking access. 5.7 million cannot meet daily food needs. 1.3 million displaced from home.

Coffee is Central America's signature crop and its signature injustice. Seventy percent of farms are under five hectares. A picker earns $3 to $5 a day. A bag of single-origin Huehuetenango beans retails for $22 in the US. The commodity price is set on the New York ICE exchange by traders who have never seen a coffee cherry. When the price drops below production cost — as it does in bad years — the farmer cannot switch crops because the trees take four years to mature. She can only wait, or leave. The coffee in your cup this morning may be from the same hillside her son left behind when he walked to Arizona.
Honduras is the most dangerous country on earth for environmental defenders. Garífuna communities along the Caribbean coast in Colón and Atlántida have fought palm oil expansion by Dinant Corporation for over a decade. Dinant received International Finance Corporation (World Bank Group) investment. The IFC's own compliance advisor found that the investment failed to meet environmental and social standards. Defenders continue to be killed. Courts continue to fail to convict. The plantations continue to expand.
The Panama Canal — which handles 5% of global maritime trade and generated $5.7 billion in total revenues in FY2025 (recovering from drought) — depends on Gatun Lake, which depends on rainfall in the canal watershed, which is shrinking. In 2023, drought forced the Canal Authority to restrict daily transits from 36 to a minimum of 18 per day, backing up global shipping. The same year, mass protests by Ngäbe-Buglé indigenous communities and Panamanian citizens shut down the Cobre Panamá copper mine, whose tailings threatened Gatun Lake's watershed. Water for global shipping and water for indigenous communities are the same water — and there is not enough of both.

Cascade Chains

Dry Corridor drought → Primera harvest failure → Food insecurity by August → Migration northward (GTM/HND/SLV — 3.4M food insecure in Guatemala alone; Dry Corridor communities with no irrigation fallback; milpa planting dependent on May rains that arrived late or as destructive floods in recent years)

Hurricane landfall → Hillside soil loss → Coffee farm destruction → Smallholder debt and displacement → Migration (HND/GTM — Eta and Iota 2020 affected 4M people; 70% of coffee farms under 5 hectares with no insurance; recovery from one storm incomplete before the next arrives)

Palm oil expansion → Q'eqchi' and Garífuna land seizure → Loss of subsistence farming → Forced wage labour or migration (GTM/HND — Dinant Corporation and REPSA operating on formerly communal land; Honduras world's most dangerous country for environmental defenders)

Cartel territorial control → Extortion of growers and transit communities → Forced displacement → Migration corridor overload → Tapachula bottleneck (MEX — Sinaloa and CJNG contesting routes; avocado sector under cartel taxation in Michoacán; migrants kidnapped, extorted, killed on transit routes)

Who Sustains This

Sinaloa Cartel · Fentanyl precursor trade (China-sourced); methamphetamine; cocaine transit; avocado extortion

Driven by: We must control the plaza — whoever controls the route controls everything

The trap: Building alternative order through destruction of social order. Cartels position themselves as providers of employment and infrastructure where the state has failed, but their violence and corruption destroy the civic fabric that would make genuine development possible. The patron who feeds the community is also the one who makes it ungovernable.

Jalisco New Generation Cartel · Methamphetamine production; fentanyl; extortion; illegal mining

Driven by: We must expand or be consumed — there is no standing still

The trap: Achieving stability through constant expansion and violence that ensures instability. CJNG's model requires perpetual territorial conquest to maintain internal cohesion, but each new front creates new enemies and new fragmentation, making the consolidation of power permanently impossible.

Guatemalan Military and Security Forces · State budget; private security contracts; institutional landholdings

Driven by: We must defend the nation from communism/narcos/disorder

The trap: Protecting democracy by preventing democratic change. The Guatemalan military's institutional identity was forged in Cold War counterinsurgency and genocide. Post-peace institutions (CICIG) that threatened impunity networks were expelled, demonstrating that the 'democratic' state can only tolerate democracy that doesn't challenge oligarchic power structures.

Honduran Military and National Police · State budget; private security industry; institutional enterprises

Driven by: We must maintain order against gangs and instability

The trap: Fighting organized crime while being penetrated by it. The Honduran security forces receive anti-narcotics aid while elements maintain documented links to trafficking networks. The war on drugs provides both the threat and the revenue stream simultaneously.

Nine flows leave Central America — coffee, bananas, palm oil, pineapples, avocados, gold, migrant labour, remittances, and the canal tolls that every container ship pays to cross from the Atlantic to the Pacific. What does not come back is the topsoil, stripped by monoculture and flooding; the groundwater, drained by avocado orchards and pineapple plantations; the mangroves, cleared for shrimp ponds and export crops; and the people, 1.2 million displaced and uncounted more walking north. Panama's Supreme Court shut down the Cobre Panamá mine in 2023 after mass protests by Ngäbe-Buglé communities and urban Panamanians — a rare instance where extraction was stopped before the damage was done. The copper is still in the ground. The question is whether it stays there. 5.7M food insecure (current); 1.3M displaced (total); diaspora remittances, coffee beans, coffee beans leaving.

The milpa does not know it is ancient. It only knows the rain came or did not come. The woman planting it does not think of herself as the keeper of a four-thousand-year tradition. She thinks about whether the beans will be enough to last until December.

For the Dry Corridor's hungry...

For the farming families of the Dry Corridor, from Guatemala through Honduras, where the rains fail year on year and the maize fails with them — for those who eat less so their children may eat, and who face the choice between hunger and the road north.

For those who take the road...

For the many who leave — walking north through Mexico, or through the mud of the Darién Gap — fleeing gangs and drought and dead-end poverty. For safety on a road that preys on them, and for the families living on remittances that come at such a cost.

For those under the gang's rule...

For the neighbourhoods governed by fear across the region — the young pressed to join or die, the shopkeepers paying the rent of extortion, the mothers of the disappeared. For the courage of those who stay and refuse.

For those who grow and gather...

For the seasonal workers of the coffee slopes and the banana and pineapple plantations, paid by the piece for work that breaks the body; and for the Miskitu, Rama, and Garífuna of the Caribbean coast whose land is taken for gold and cattle.

For the silenced...

For the peoples living under governments that have closed the churches, jailed the priests, and exiled the critics. That truth still finds a way to be spoken.

For those who accompany...

For the shelter-keepers along the migrant road, the catechists and Sisters who feed and bury and record, the human-rights defenders so often killed for it. That they are protected, and their work remembered.

What to bring with you from this briefing

The coffee in your cup may come from Huehuetenango, Guatemala, where 90% of producers are smallholders — most of them Maya — earning $3 to $5 a day during harvest. The New York ICE exchange sets the price. Specialty roasters add value. The farmer absorbs the volatility. If your coffee is labelled "single-origin" or "direct trade," ask the roaster: what price did the farmer receive per pound? Fair trade certification (Fairtrade International) guarantees a floor price, but the floor is low. Cooperative-direct models (like those organised through Fedecocagua) capture more, but they are fragile. Your question — asked at the counter, in an email to the brand — is a form of pressure that costs nothing but attention.
In 2023, Panamanians shut down the largest copper mine in Central America. Ngäbe-Buglé indigenous communities and urban protesters blocked roads across the country until the Supreme Court ruled First Quantum Minerals' contract unconstitutional. The mine closed. The copper — worth billions — stayed in the ground. First Quantum filed a $20 billion ICSID arbitration claim but suspended it in March 2025 to open re-negotiations with Panama (63% of Panamanians oppose reopening). Whether a country can say no to extraction and survive the legal consequences remains unresolved — but the suspension itself is a signal that pure legal force has limits.
Honduras has the highest per-capita rate of environmental defender killings in the world. Garífuna communities in Colón and Atlántida, organised through OFRANEH (ofraneh.org), have won Inter-American Court rulings on ancestral land rights that the state has been slow to enforce. Dinant Corporation — which received International Finance Corporation (World Bank Group) investment — operates palm oil plantations on contested land. The IFC's Compliance Advisor Ombudsman found the investment failed to meet standards. The Accountability Counsel (accountabilitycounsel.org) tracks these cases. Your pension fund may hold World Bank bonds.
